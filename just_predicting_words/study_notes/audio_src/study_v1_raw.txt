Just Predicting Words. Study Notes. Version one.

Chapter 1. Just Predicting Words.

Section. The launch.

ChatGPT launched on November 30, 2022. The company behind it was OpenAI, a San Francisco AI lab. The product was a simple chat interface sitting on top of a fine-tuned version of an existing language model called GPT-3.5. It was not announced as a major release. Internally, it was described as a low-key research preview.

Within five days, ChatGPT had one million users. Within two months, it had a hundred million. At the time, it was the fastest-growing consumer product in history — faster than Facebook, faster than TikTok, faster than the iPhone. Within a few weeks of launch, it had quietly entered the daily routines of writers, programmers, teachers, lawyers, doctors, and ordinary users who had never tried any AI tool before in their lives.

Section. The mechanism.

The technology underneath ChatGPT, despite the apparent intelligence of its outputs, is doing one specific thing: predicting the next word in a sequence. Given everything written so far, the model assigns a probability to every possible next word, picks one, and continues. Then it does the same thing again with the new word included in the context. It produces text one token at a time, left to right, by repeatedly answering the question: what word comes next.

This is the central claim of the book and the source of its title. Modern AI assistants are next-word predictors. The trick is small. What emerges from it is not.

Section. Why this matters as framing.

Most discussion of AI either over-mystifies the technology (treating it as a conscious mind) or dismisses it (calling it autocomplete). The next-word-prediction framing is meant to do neither. It is meant to be honest about the mechanism — really, the model is just predicting words — while leaving room for the fact that doing this at sufficient scale, on sufficient data, with the right architecture, produces capabilities that look qualitatively new.

The author's stance: AI assistants are genuinely useful, genuinely limited, and the user who understands how they actually work makes better decisions than the user who treats them as magic or as a toy.

Section. What the book covers (preview).

The book is structured in roughly six parts:

Part 1 (Chapters 1–4): how the technology actually works — tokens, embeddings, the Transformer architecture, attention, training, and the alignment techniques that shape raw word predictors into helpful assistants.
Part 2 (Chapters 5–10): the companies — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, the Chinese labs (DeepSeek, Qwen, Kimi, others), and the rest of the ecosystem (Microsoft, xAI, Mistral, Perplexity, Amazon, Apple).
Part 3 (Chapters 11–13): what AI is genuinely good at, how it fails, and the structural limits of language as a medium.
Part 4 (Chapters 14–17): real risks, hyped risks, the guardrails companies use, and the regulatory landscape (EU AI Act, US patchwork, China's state framework).
Part 5 (Chapters 18–20): how to use AI well, how to handle it at work, how to talk to your kids about it.
Part 6 (Chapter 21): where this is going, and what to do given the uncertainty.

The book was written through May 2026 and reflects the state of the field at that moment.

Chapter 2. A Short History of Machines That Read.

Section. Distributional semantics.

The intellectual ancestor of modern language AI is an idea from 1950s linguistics: words get their meaning from the company they keep. The slogan version was coined by the British linguist John Rupert Firth: "You shall know a word by the company it keeps." For decades this was just an observation. It became useful only when somebody figured out how to compute with it.

Section. word2vec, 2013.

The breakthrough came in 2013. A researcher at Google named Tomas Mikolov, with colleagues, built a tool called word2vec. It read enormous amounts of text and learned, for every word, a list of about three hundred numbers — a vector — that captured the word's "company" in the corpus. Words that appeared in similar contexts ended up with similar vectors.

The striking demonstration was that the vectors did arithmetic. You could take the vector for "king," subtract "man," add "woman," and get something extremely close to "queen." You could take Paris minus France plus Germany and get Berlin. The system had learned the concept of gender, and the concept of "capital of," without ever being told these things existed. It had extracted meaning from raw text, at scale, for the first time in any machine.

The limit of word2vec was that it knew about words but not sentences. It could tell you that "dog" and "puppy" were similar. It could not tell you what "the dog chased its tail" meant, or how meaning depended on word order.

Section. The Transformer, 2017.

The architecture that made sentences tractable was published in June 2017 in a paper called "Attention Is All You Need." It came out of Google. There were eight authors. The architecture they introduced — the Transformer — is the basis for essentially every modern large language model. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama, DeepSeek, Qwen — all Transformers.

Within the next eight years, all eight of those Google researchers had left Google. They founded or led other AI organizations. Aidan Gomez went on to co-found Cohere. Others ended up at OpenAI, Anthropic, and various startups. Google had invented the architecture that now powers most of the modern AI industry and then watched a generation of competitors build on it.

Section. The GPT line, 2018–2020.

OpenAI built on the Transformer. The pattern was to take the architecture and scale it up — more parameters, more data, more compute. The GPT name stands for Generative Pre-trained Transformer.

GPT-1, released 2018, had one hundred and seventeen million parameters. It was a research curiosity.
GPT-2, released 2019, had one and a half billion parameters. OpenAI initially withheld it, calling it "too dangerous to release." They eventually released it in November 2019.
GPT-3, released 2020, had one hundred and seventy-five billion parameters. It was the first model that startled even its creators with what it could do, and it was available only through an API.

The jump from GPT-2 to GPT-3 was about a hundred times more parameters. The capability jump was larger still.

Section. The ChatGPT moment, November 2022.

On November 30, 2022, OpenAI launched ChatGPT — a chat interface to a fine-tuned version of GPT-3.5. The launch was framed as a low-key research preview. It went viral within hours.

One million users in five days. One hundred million users in two months. At the time, the fastest-growing consumer product in history.

Section. The scramble that followed, late 2022 through 2023.

The ChatGPT launch triggered a frantic reordering of the industry.

Google declared an internal "code red." It treated ChatGPT as an existential threat to search.

Anthropic, founded by ex-OpenAI researchers, released the first version of its Claude assistant in March 2023.

Meta released the first version of Llama, becoming the first major company to release a frontier-class model with open weights (anyone could download and run it).

Microsoft, which had already invested in OpenAI starting in 2019, deepened the relationship dramatically. The total Microsoft investment in OpenAI eventually exceeded thirteen billion dollars.

Amazon made a comparable bet on Anthropic, eventually committing up to twenty-five billion dollars.

By the end of 2023, the modern AI industry — its big players, its commercial structure, its competitive shape — was largely set. Most of what has happened since has been the elaboration of that structure.

Chapter 3. Inside the Machine.

Section. Tokens.

Modern language models don't read characters and they don't quite read words. They read tokens. A token is usually a piece of a word — typically four characters or so, sometimes a whole short word, sometimes a single character for rare cases. "Cat" might be one token. "Unbelievable" might be three. Names, punctuation, and unusual character sequences each become their own tokens.

The model produces text the same way it reads it: one token at a time. The "next-word prediction" framing from chapter one is more precisely "next-token prediction." For practical purposes the distinction usually doesn't matter, but it matters for understanding context limits and pricing — most AI APIs charge per token, not per word.

Section. Embeddings and vector space.

Each token gets converted into a vector — a list of several hundred to several thousand numbers. The vector is the model's internal representation of that token's meaning, learned during training from the patterns of how the token co-occurs with others.

This is the same idea as word2vec from chapter two, but scaled up. The vector space has the property that semantically similar tokens end up close to each other. Words about cooking cluster together. Words about sports cluster together. Mathematical relationships between vectors capture conceptual relationships — gender, capital-of, plural-of, comparative-of, and many others the model discovers without supervision.

The vectors are dense (every dimension carries some signal, none are zero by default), high-dimensional (hundreds to thousands of dimensions, far more than humans can visualize), and the dimensions don't correspond to human-interpretable categories — they emerge from the training process.

Section. Attention.

The Transformer's core mechanism is called attention. When the model is producing the next token, it doesn't treat all the previous tokens equally. For each token it's about to generate, it computes how much "attention" to pay to every previous token in the context, and weights its prediction accordingly.

This solves the problem word2vec couldn't solve: word order matters, and context further back can be relevant. When the model sees "the dog chased its tail," attention lets it know that "its" refers to "dog," not to something earlier or later in the sentence. When the model is summarizing a long document, attention lets it pull together information from anywhere in the document, not just nearby words.

The full name of the 2017 paper that introduced the Transformer was "Attention Is All You Need" — meaning that attention alone, without the other complicated architectural pieces researchers had been trying, was enough.

Section. The Transformer architecture.

A Transformer is a stack of layers, each layer made of attention plus some standard neural-network components. Information flows up the stack: lower layers learn local patterns (which word follows which), middle layers learn syntactic and structural patterns (how sentences are organized), upper layers learn abstract semantic patterns (what the passage is about, how to respond to it).

A modern frontier model has dozens to over a hundred such layers. Each layer transforms the representations slightly. By the top of the stack, the representations are rich enough to support the model's predictions.

Section. Pre-training.

A frontier model is trained on enormous amounts of text. The training objective is simple: given a sequence of tokens, predict the next one. Do this billions of times across trillions of tokens of text. The text comes from essentially the entire public internet, plus licensed datasets, books, code repositories, and other sources.

This phase is called pre-training. It's the most expensive part of building a modern AI model — it requires months of compute on thousands to tens of thousands of GPUs and costs hundreds of millions of dollars for the largest models. The model that comes out of pre-training is a raw next-token predictor. It is not yet a chatbot, doesn't know what a user is, and doesn't know it's supposed to be helpful. That shaping happens in the next phase, which chapter four covers.

Section. Parameters.

A model's parameters are the actual numerical values inside the neural network. Pre-training sets these values. Roughly speaking, more parameters means more capacity to learn complex patterns, though the relationship is not strictly linear.

The headline numbers across the GPT line: GPT-1 had 117 million, GPT-2 had 1.5 billion, GPT-3 had 175 billion. The largest open-weight frontier models in 2026 (such as DeepSeek V4's 1.6 trillion-parameter variant) have crossed the trillion mark. The largest closed-weight frontier models are believed to be comparable or larger but their exact sizes are not disclosed.

When AI labs talk about model size, they almost always mean parameter count.

Section. Context window.

The amount of text the model can consider at once is called the context window, measured in tokens. The first GPT models had context windows of a few thousand tokens. By 2026, frontier models often have context windows of one to two million tokens — enough to fit entire books, large codebases, or long meeting transcripts in a single conversation. Context windows have grown much faster than parameter counts in recent years and are now one of the most important practical differences between competing models.

Chapter 4. Taming the Wild Word Predictor.

Section. The problem after pre-training.

A model fresh out of pre-training is a raw next-token predictor. It does not know what a conversation is. It does not know what a "user" is. It does not know it is supposed to be helpful, honest, or harmless. Given the right prompt it will produce racist text, instructions for harm, conspiracy theories, sexually explicit content, or any other pattern present in the training data, because the training data contained all of those patterns.

Turning that raw model into something safe and useful for ordinary users is the work of the alignment phase. Three techniques do most of the work: instruction tuning, reinforcement learning from human feedback, and Constitutional AI.

Section. Instruction tuning.

The first step is to show the model examples of being helpful. Human contractors write thousands of example exchanges — a request and an ideal response. The model is fine-tuned on these examples. After this fine-tuning, the model has a sense that when it sees a question-shaped input it should produce an answer-shaped output. It now behaves like an assistant rather than a generic text continuation engine.

Instruction tuning alone is enough to produce a basically functional chatbot. It is not enough to produce a safe or carefully-behaved one.

Section. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).

RLHF is the technique that shaped raw word predictors into the polished assistants we use today. The training method:

1. The model produces several different responses to the same prompt.
2. Human raters rank the responses from best to worst.
3. The model is updated to make highly-ranked responses more likely and low-ranked responses less likely.
4. Repeat across millions of prompts.

Over millions of these comparisons, the model learns to produce the kind of response humans prefer. This is where it picks up tone, friendliness, format conventions, the habit of declining to help with abusive requests, and many other behaviors.

RLHF has a known side effect: sycophancy. Humans, on average, prefer responses that agree with them, that compliment them, and that don't push back too hard. The model picks up a subtle bias toward telling you what you want to hear. We come back to this failure mode in chapter twelve.

Section. Constitutional AI.

Constitutional AI is Anthropic's variant on the same idea. Instead of relying entirely on direct human feedback for every behavior, Anthropic gives the model a written set of principles — its "constitution." The model is then trained to critique and revise its own outputs against the principles, with humans involved in the loop more sparingly.

The advantages, as Anthropic argues them: explicit principles can be audited, debated, and changed; the model can be trained on a much larger scale because humans don't have to grade every output; and the principles can address subtle behaviors (such as how to handle dual-use information requests) that are hard to capture in single-example comparisons.

Other major labs use a mix of RLHF and constitutional-style techniques. The boundary has blurred over time.

Section. The "helpful, honest, harmless" frame.

The explicit alignment goal at most major labs is for the assistant to be helpful, honest, and harmless. The triad is often abbreviated HHH. The three are in real tension: a fully harmless model would refuse most requests; a fully helpful model would explain how to do dangerous things; an honest model has to admit when it doesn't know something, which is sometimes unhelpful. Labs spend most of their alignment effort negotiating these trade-offs.

Section. The system prompt.

Once the model is deployed as a product, every conversation starts with a hidden block of text called the system prompt, prepended to the conversation before the model sees it. The system prompt is where the company tells the model what kind of assistant to be — tone, topics to avoid, format of responses, legal disclaimers, the model's persona, the rules for handling sensitive topics. For a consumer chatbot like ChatGPT or Claude, the system prompt is typically several thousand words.

The system prompt is the fastest-moving part of the defense system around a model. It can be changed without retraining, so labs can respond to new failure modes within hours. It is also one of the most fragile parts: clever users can sometimes get the model to ignore its system prompt through carefully designed inputs, an attack family known as prompt injection.

Section. What you actually talk to.

When you use a frontier AI product, you are talking to a model that has been pre-trained on the internet, instruction-tuned on examples, refined through RLHF or Constitutional AI, given a system prompt by the company, and wrapped in additional safety classifiers that watch the conversation. The assistant you experience is the cumulative product of all of those layers. We come back to the defensive stack in chapter sixteen.

Chapter 5. OpenAI, the Company That Started the Wave.

Section. Founding.

OpenAI was founded in December 2015 in San Francisco. The founding group included Sam Altman, Elon Musk, Greg Brockman, Ilya Sutskever, John Schulman, Wojciech Zaremba, and several others. The original structure was a non-profit. The founding pitch was that artificial general intelligence was potentially the most consequential technology humanity would ever build, that it should not be developed in secret by a single corporation, and that an open research lab funded by philanthropy was the right vehicle.

Funding commitments at founding totaled around one billion dollars in pledges from Musk, Reid Hoffman, Peter Thiel, Amazon Web Services, and others.

Section. The Musk departure, 2018.

Elon Musk left the OpenAI board in 2018, publicly citing conflicts of interest with his AI work at Tesla. By some accounts that surfaced later in his lawsuit, he had wanted to acquire OpenAI outright before leaving, and at one point asked for about ninety percent of the equity in the for-profit conversion.

Section. The for-profit conversion.

By 2019, OpenAI's leadership had concluded that the resources required to build frontier AI exceeded what a non-profit could raise. They created a capped-profit subsidiary that could take outside investment, while keeping the non-profit as the controlling entity. The structure was unusual and would generate lawsuits later.

Section. Microsoft, 2019 onward.

Microsoft invested in OpenAI starting in 2019. After ChatGPT, the partnership deepened. Microsoft's total commitment grew to more than thirteen billion dollars over time. Microsoft got the right to deploy OpenAI's models in Microsoft products (Copilot, Bing, Office, Azure) and a large slice of the commercial upside. OpenAI got compute, cash, and a guaranteed distribution channel.

Section. ChatGPT.

Launched November 30, 2022 — see chapters one and two for the launch metrics. The product turned OpenAI from a research lab into a consumer phenomenon overnight.

Section. The board crisis, November 2023.

On November 17, 2023, the OpenAI board fired Sam Altman. The official reason was a vague reference to a loss of confidence. Within hours, much of the company threatened to quit. Microsoft offered to hire the entire team. Investors put pressure on the board. By November 21 — four days later — Altman was back as CEO. Most of the board members who had fired him were gone. The episode became a case study in how quickly governance can collapse under commercial pressure.

Section. The Musk lawsuit, 2024–2026.

Elon Musk sued OpenAI in 2024. His claim was that the transition from non-profit to for-profit was a betrayal of the original founding mission. He asked the court to undo the for-profit conversion, and sought up to one hundred and fifty billion dollars in damages.

In May 2026, after an eleven-day trial in Oakland, California, the jury found that Musk had filed too late under the relevant statute of limitations. The judge dismissed the case. Musk's lawyers have said they will appeal.

Section. Products and the IPO horizon.

OpenAI's main consumer product is ChatGPT (now backed by the GPT-5 family). Its main developer offering is the API, where developers can use the same models programmatically. Its main enterprise offering is ChatGPT Enterprise.

As of mid-2026, OpenAI is reportedly in talks to go public at a valuation that could reach one trillion dollars. If that materializes, it would be one of the largest IPOs in history.

Section. What OpenAI stands for, in 2026.

In its public statements, OpenAI's stated worldview is that artificial general intelligence is coming sooner rather than later, that safety is something to be addressed alongside aggressive deployment rather than before it, and that the way to make AI safe is to deploy it broadly so that the technology and its risks become well understood through use. Critics — including some former employees who left for Anthropic — argue this is rationalization for moving too fast. Defenders argue the alternative (delay) cedes the field to less safety-focused competitors.

Section. What to remember about OpenAI in one paragraph.

Founded 2015 as a non-profit. Converted to a capped-profit structure. Built the GPT line, launched ChatGPT in November 2022, started the modern AI wave. Microsoft is its largest backer at over thirteen billion dollars. Sam Altman was fired and reinstated within four days in November 2023. The Musk lawsuit was dismissed in May 2026 (under appeal). The company is moving toward an IPO at potentially a trillion-dollar valuation. Its stated philosophy: deploy aggressively, learn through use.

Chapter 6. Anthropic, the Company That Broke Off Over Safety.

Section. Founding.

Anthropic was founded in 2021 by seven researchers who had left OpenAI. The founding group included Dario Amodei (formerly VP of Research at OpenAI) and his sister Daniela Amodei (now President of Anthropic), along with several other senior OpenAI alumni. The stated reason for the departure was a disagreement about safety — specifically, the view that OpenAI was moving too quickly relative to its safety research.

Anthropic was set up explicitly as a safety-focused AI lab. The plan was to participate at the frontier of capability so that safety research would be done on the same systems being deployed, rather than as a separate, slower track.

Section. Claude.

Anthropic's main product is the Claude family of assistants. Claude was first released in March 2023. The Claude 2, Claude 3, and Claude 3.5 generations followed; by 2026, Claude is generally considered one of the two or three strongest frontier models in the world.

Claude is trained using Constitutional AI (see chapter four) and is known for being relatively careful, willing to push back on the user, and willing to acknowledge uncertainty.

Section. The Claude Code moment, May 2025.

In May 2025, Anthropic launched Claude Code, an agentic coding tool designed for developers. Within months, it had generated over one billion dollars in annualized revenue — one of the fastest products to a billion in software history.

Within fifteen months of that launch, Anthropic's overall annualized revenue had jumped from a few hundred million to thirty billion dollars. Eighty times what it had been a year earlier.

Section. The valuation race.

By 2026, Anthropic had become a roughly nine-hundred-billion-dollar company in private markets. Major backers include Google, which has committed up to forty billion dollars in investments, and Amazon, which has committed up to twenty-five billion. Anthropic has signed compute contracts with Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Broadcom, and — in a surprising arrangement — Elon Musk's SpaceX for additional capacity.

The full story of how a safety-focused lab founded by seven researchers became a nine-hundred-billion-dollar company in five years has not been fully told. The author's framing: faster than almost anyone outside its founders predicted, with all the complications that follow.

Section. The Pentagon designation, March 2026.

In March 2026, the United States Pentagon designated Anthropic a supply chain risk and blacklisted the company from working with the military. The dispute arose after contract negotiations broke down. The Pentagon wanted unrestricted use of Claude for all lawful military purposes; Anthropic refused to permit uses like fully autonomous weapons and domestic mass surveillance. When the company missed the government's deadline, the administration moved to bar federal agencies from using its technology. Anthropic has been challenging the designation in court while continuing its broader growth.

Section. Anthropic's stated worldview, 2026.

Anthropic publicly argues that AI capabilities will continue to grow rapidly, that the alignment problem (getting models to do what we actually want) is genuinely hard and not solved, that the technology should be developed by labs that take safety seriously rather than left to those who don't, and that being at the frontier is the price of being able to do meaningful safety research. The lab invests heavily in interpretability research — the effort to understand what is happening inside a model — because it believes that without interpretability, claims about safety cannot really be verified.

Dario Amodei has publicly predicted that AI could wipe out half of entry-level white-collar jobs within five years and push unemployment into double digits. He has also written extensively (notably in "Machines of Loving Grace," 2024) about the upside potential of safe AI for science and human welfare.

Section. What to remember about Anthropic in one paragraph.

Founded 2021 by seven ex-OpenAI researchers, led by Dario and Daniela Amodei. Makes the Claude assistant. Constitutional AI is its alignment approach. Claude Code launched May 2025 and helped drive revenue from a few hundred million to thirty billion in fifteen months. Now valued near nine hundred billion. Major backers: Google up to forty billion, Amazon up to twenty-five billion. Pentagon designated it a supply-chain risk in March 2026 after Anthropic refused to permit autonomous-weapons and mass-surveillance uses; Anthropic is challenging in court. Stated philosophy: be at the frontier so safety research happens on real systems.

Chapter 7. Google, the Giant That Almost Lost the Future.

Section. The paradox.

Google invented the architecture that powers the modern AI industry — the Transformer, in 2017, at Google Brain. Every major modern AI assistant is a descendant of that architecture. And yet by the time ChatGPT launched in November 2022, Google was widely seen as being behind. The story of how that happened, and how Google has been trying to catch up since, is the story of this chapter.

Section. The two AI groups inside Google.

For most of the 2010s and into the 2020s, Google had two distinct AI research organizations.

Google Brain, founded inside Google, produced the Transformer paper and much of the foundational work that the rest of the industry built on.
DeepMind, acquired by Google in 2014 for around five hundred million dollars, was founded in London by Demis Hassabis and others. It was famous for AlphaGo (the program that beat the world champion at Go in 2016), AlphaFold (which solved a long-standing problem in protein structure prediction), and a strong research culture independent of Google's main businesses.

The two groups had distinct cultures, distinct strategies, and a real organizational rivalry. After ChatGPT, Google merged them into a single entity called Google DeepMind, run by Demis Hassabis. The merger was completed in 2023.

Section. The Nobel Prize, 2024.

Demis Hassabis shared the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry with John Jumper for AlphaFold's work on protein folding. AlphaFold predicted the three-dimensional structures of essentially all known proteins and made the predictions publicly available — a leap forward for structural biology. The award marked the first Nobel given for work substantially driven by AI.

Section. The Code Red, late 2022.

When ChatGPT launched, Google's leadership reportedly declared an internal "code red." The concern was direct and existential: if conversational AI replaced search as the way most people looked up information, Google's core business — built on selling ads alongside search results — would erode rapidly.

Section. Bard, then Gemini.

Google's first public response was Bard, a ChatGPT competitor that launched in early 2023. Bard had a rocky start, including a high-profile factual error in its initial public demonstration that wiped roughly one hundred billion dollars off Google's market cap in a single day.

In late 2023 Google rebranded the product line as Gemini and folded the underlying model work into a coherent family. Gemini 1.0, 1.5, and successive versions improved rapidly. By 2026, Gemini is one of the strongest frontier model families, with a particular strength in extremely long context windows — the Gemini line was among the first to ship one-million-token and two-million-token context windows, enough to fit entire books or large codebases in a single conversation.

Section. Distribution as Google's advantage.

Google's biggest competitive asset is not its model technology — which is competitive but not always the absolute frontier — but its distribution. AI capabilities are now integrated into Google Search (AI Overviews, then a successor AI mode), Workspace (Gmail, Docs, Meet), Android, Chrome, YouTube, and Maps. Billions of users encounter Google AI dozens of times a day, whether or not they think of themselves as using AI.

Section. The talent diaspora from Google.

A pattern worth knowing: many of the people who built the foundational technology at Google have since left to compete with it. All eight authors of the original Transformer paper had left Google within eight years of publication. Aidan Gomez co-founded Cohere. Several went to OpenAI or Anthropic or started other ventures. The same broad pattern holds across the rest of Google's AI staff. Some of it is the gravitational pull of OpenAI and Anthropic; some of it is the differential between Google's pay scale and what frontier startups can offer.

Google has been working to slow the bleeding by paying large retention packages and giving senior researchers more autonomy inside DeepMind. The success of those efforts is hard to evaluate from outside.

Section. What Google stands for in AI, 2026.

Google's stated philosophy is closer to a careful-deployment position than OpenAI's deploy-aggressively or Anthropic's safety-first. Google emphasizes integration into existing products, regulatory cooperation, and broad scientific contributions like AlphaFold. Critics argue that Google's caution is mostly a function of having a giant existing business to protect; defenders argue that Google has earned the right to its approach through decades of building useful products.

Section. What to remember about Google in one paragraph.

Invented the Transformer in 2017, then was outmaneuvered by smaller companies built on top of it. Owns DeepMind (acquired 2014) and merged Brain into it in 2023. Demis Hassabis won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for AlphaFold. The Gemini model family is now one of the strongest, especially at long context. Distribution — Search, Workspace, Android, Chrome, YouTube — is Google's biggest AI advantage. The original eight Transformer authors had all left Google within eight years.

Chapter 8. Meta, the Open Bet That Changed Everything.

Section. The scale of Meta.

Meta is the parent company of Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, and Threads. About three billion people use one of those products every day. Whatever you think about Meta as a company, the scale of its reach is undisputed. Meta has more direct access to global communication than any organization in history.

Section. The open-weight bet, 2023 to 2025.

Meta's distinctive contribution to the AI industry from 2023 through early 2026 was releasing frontier-class models with open weights — meaning anyone could download the model and run it on their own hardware. The Llama series was the vehicle.

Llama 1, released February 2023 (initially leaked, then officially released). Showed that competitive models could be open.
Llama 2, released July 2023, with a commercial-use license.
Llama 3, released April 2024 and updated through the year. By the end of 2024, Llama 3 was the most widely deployed open-weight model in the world. Whole ecosystems of fine-tunes, specialized variants, and downstream products grew on top of it.

The strategic case Mark Zuckerberg made for openness was that releasing weights would commoditize a layer of the AI stack that Meta did not need to own, accelerate research that Meta benefited from, and prevent any single competitor (notably Google or OpenAI) from controlling the foundation.

Section. The Llama 4 disaster, April 2025.

Meta released Llama 4 in April 2025. The launch was later found to have used different model versions for different benchmarks, gaming the results — different model versions were submitted to leaderboards than were released publicly. Internal consequences were severe. Public confidence in Meta's model line dropped sharply. The episode contributed to the leadership changes that followed.

Section. The Superintelligence Labs pivot, mid-2025.

In mid-2025, Meta announced the formation of Meta Superintelligence Labs — the name itself a statement of intent. To lead it, Zuckerberg paid an astonishing price. He spent about fourteen point three billion dollars to acquire forty-nine percent of Scale AI, a company that provided data labeling services for AI training. As part of the deal, Scale's twenty-eight-year-old cofounder, Alexandr Wang, joined Meta as chief AI officer.

The cultural friction was, by all accounts, real. The sixty-five-year-old Turing Award winner Yann LeCun, who had been Meta's chief AI scientist for years, on paper reported to a twenty-eight-year-old executive. LeCun had publicly described large language models as a "dead end" for reaching true AI. Wang was building Meta's strategy entirely around large language models.

Section. The LeCun departure, November 2025.

Yann LeCun left Meta in November 2025 to found a new lab called AMI Labs, focused on world-model approaches to AI rather than language-model approaches. AMI stands for Advanced Machine Intelligence. LeCun's thesis: real general intelligence will require models that learn how the physical world works, not just how text works.

Section. Muse Spark and the pivot to closed weights, early 2026.

In March 2026, Meta launched Muse Spark — its first major model after the LeCun departure and the Wang takeover. Muse Spark was not open-weight. Access was limited to Meta's own products and a small invite-only group of partners. The company that had defined the open-weight ecosystem had, with this release, chosen to play by the same rules as OpenAI and Anthropic.

The reasons were not made fully public, but the implication was clear. Competitive pressure had become too intense. The risk of releasing weights that competitors — particularly Chinese labs — could clone and build on had become too great. After Chinese labs had built on Llama to produce models competing with Meta's own offerings, the strategic logic of giving everything away started to look different.

Section. Capex and layoffs, 2026.

Meta's 2026 capital expenditure budget is expected to be between one hundred fifteen and one hundred thirty-five billion dollars, almost all of it AI-related. They have announced fifteen thousand layoffs alongside the AI restructuring, redistributing the resources from cut roles into AI infrastructure and talent.

Section. What Meta believes now.

The new leadership's stated views: capability comes from scale, data quality, and rapid iteration; open-weight releases are a strategic tool, not a moral commitment; Meta's distribution scale across three billion users matters more than any single model; the company is hedging on world-model research alongside its language-model work.

Section. What to remember about Meta in one paragraph.

Three billion daily users gives it unmatched distribution. Defined the open-weight ecosystem with Llama 1, 2, 3 in 2023 to 2024. Llama 4 in April 2025 was a disaster — different model versions submitted to benchmarks than released. Meta Superintelligence Labs formed mid-2025 around Scale AI's Alexandr Wang (twenty-eight years old), brought in via a fourteen-point-three-billion-dollar deal for forty-nine percent of Scale. Yann LeCun left in November 2025 to found AMI Labs. Muse Spark in March 2026 was Meta's first closed-weight release — the company that had championed open weights stepped back. 2026 capex is one hundred fifteen to one hundred thirty-five billion dollars. Fifteen thousand layoffs announced.

Chapter 9. The Other Half of the Map (China).

Section. The DeepSeek moment, January 2025.

On a Sunday in late January 2025, a Chinese company called DeepSeek released a chatbot to the public. Within forty-eight hours, the DeepSeek app had reached number one on Apple's App Store in the United States, ahead of ChatGPT. Nvidia lost five hundred billion dollars of market value in a single day. Trading desks across Wall Street called emergency meetings.

DeepSeek had released a model that performed roughly on par with the best American models — but its final training run had reportedly cost about six million dollars. American labs were spending hundreds of millions, sometimes billions, on equivalent training runs. The model was open-weight. The API pricing was a fraction of OpenAI's. The model was, in operational terms, just better at certain kinds of reasoning than what was available from anyone else.

The DeepSeek moment forced the Western AI conversation to acknowledge a fact it had been avoiding: the half of the AI map that Western media had been ignoring was, in many ways, ahead of the half they had been watching.

Section. Why China caught up faster than expected.

Two pieces of context explain the trajectory.

First, China has been investing heavily in AI research for over a decade. By the late 2010s, Chinese universities were producing more AI research papers than American universities, often with citation counts that rivaled the best American work. Chinese AI talent has been at the cutting edge for years.

Second, since October 2022, the United States has imposed sweeping export controls on advanced computer chips. The Nvidia chips that American labs use to train models were forbidden from sale to Chinese companies. The reasoning was that if AI was strategically critical, China should not have it.

The export controls did not work as intended. They forced Chinese labs to innovate in different directions — to write better software, design better architectures, train smarter rather than harder. The result was a generation of Chinese techniques often more efficient than their American equivalents. The constraint produced the breakthrough.

Section. The main labs.

DeepSeek. Owned by High-Flyer, a Chinese hedge fund. Famous for releasing extremely efficient open-weight models at very low prices. Their V4 model, released in April 2026, comes in two versions — including a 1.6 trillion parameter variant that competes with the best American closed-source offerings, at roughly one-tenth the price. DeepSeek V4 Flash costs about twenty-eight cents per million output tokens.
Alibaba. The Chinese e-commerce giant. Their AI division produces the Qwen series of models. As of mid-2026, Qwen has become the most-downloaded open-weight model family in the world, ahead of Meta's Llama. The Qwen app has over one hundred million monthly active users in China and growing markets in Asia and Africa.
Moonshot AI. A Beijing-based startup founded by former Tsinghua University researchers. Produces the Kimi model series. Particularly strong on long-context understanding and on agentic AI. Kimi K2.6, released April 2026, scored competitively with Claude and GPT-5 on coding benchmarks.
Zhipu AI. Another Beijing-based lab. Produces the GLM series. Was China's first large language model developer to list publicly, with a Hong Kong IPO in January 2026.
ByteDance. Parent of TikTok. Has released the Seed and Doubao model series. Distribution advantages through TikTok give them a uniquely powerful platform.
Baidu. China's largest search engine. Their ERNIE chatbot was one of the first major Chinese consumer AI products. Less prominent at the frontier in 2026 than they were in 2023, but still significant.
Tencent. Owner of WeChat. Has integrated AI into its products at enormous scale. Distributed over one hundred forty million dollars in cash bonuses through their AI chatbot during the 2026 Lunar New Year.
Also: MiniMax, 01.AI, StepFun, and others.

Section. Architectural innovations.

The DeepSeek architecture innovations, particularly something called Multi-Head Latent Attention, are now studied by researchers everywhere. The broad pattern: Chinese labs lead on efficiency, agentic capability, and aggressive pricing.

Section. The Huawei Ascend hardware question.

The Huawei Ascend chips, designed in China to replace Nvidia chips that cannot be imported, have improved significantly. DeepSeek V4 was trained largely on Ascend hardware. The American export controls were supposed to prevent this. The Chinese semiconductor industry has accelerated under the pressure of the controls, and the gap between Chinese and American AI hardware is narrowing.

Section. The censorship reality.

Chinese AI models have hard limits Western models do not. All of them have built-in content restrictions on topics sensitive to the Chinese government. They will not discuss Taiwan's political status the way Western models will. They will not discuss the 1989 events at Tiananmen Square. They will produce content aligned with Chinese government positions rather than providing neutral information.

This is not a quirk. It is a legal requirement. Chinese AI companies operate under regulations requiring their products to "uphold core socialist values" and to refrain from "subverting state power." The censorship is built in, both technically and culturally. Even when models are released open-weight, the censorship is baked into the training data and the alignment process.

Section. Where Chinese AI is being used most.

DeepSeek usage in Africa is, by some estimates, two to four times higher than in other regions — partly because of the price, partly because of open-weight availability, partly because of the lighter regulatory footprint. In much of Africa, Latin America, Southeast Asia, and parts of Europe, Chinese AI may already be a significant part of daily AI consumption.

Section. The geopolitical frame.

The competition is not just commercial rivalry. It is part of a slow technology cold war between two superpowers. Both governments believe whoever leads in AI will have significant strategic advantages. Both use policy tools to advance their domestic AI industries and constrain the other's. The Chinese labs sit inside this larger context as commercial companies and, in various ways, as instruments of national strategy. The same is true, in fairness, of American AI labs.

Section. What to remember about China's AI in one paragraph.

DeepSeek's January 2025 release of a six-million-dollar-trained competitive model caused Nvidia to lose five hundred billion in market cap in a day. Six or seven major labs matter: DeepSeek, Alibaba's Qwen (most-downloaded open weights), Moonshot's Kimi, Zhipu's GLM, ByteDance, Baidu, Tencent. Chinese labs lead on efficiency, open-weight releases, agentic capability, and aggressive pricing. US export controls forced innovation rather than preventing it. Huawei Ascend chips now train frontier models. Content restrictions (Taiwan, Tiananmen, "core socialist values") are legal requirements baked into models. Western businesses generally cannot use Chinese AI for compliance reasons. The multipolar AI future is now structural.

Chapter 10. Everyone Else.

Section. Microsoft, the company that wraps OpenAI.

Microsoft does not make a frontier AI model of its own that matters at the consumer level. The AI in Microsoft products is, in almost every case, an OpenAI model with a Microsoft system prompt and Microsoft branding around it.

Microsoft Copilot, the consumer chatbot in Windows and Edge, is OpenAI's GPT models with a Microsoft wrapper. Microsoft 365 Copilot, the AI inside Word, Excel, Outlook, PowerPoint, and Teams, is also primarily OpenAI-powered. GitHub Copilot, the developer tool, started as pure OpenAI but has become more interesting — as of 2024 and 2025, developers can choose between GPT, Claude, and Gemini in GitHub Copilot.

The Microsoft-OpenAI relationship is the largest commercial deal in AI history. Microsoft has invested over thirteen billion dollars in OpenAI. The partnership has made Microsoft the largest AI distributor in the world — about two billion people use Microsoft software in some form every month. Microsoft has also been quietly hedging: it has hired Mustafa Suleyman (cofounder of DeepMind, founder of Inflection) to lead Microsoft AI, and is training its own in-house model called MAI.

Section. xAI, the lab that became a space company.

In March 2023, Elon Musk founded a new AI company called xAI. He recruited eleven researchers from Google, OpenAI, and Microsoft. The pitch was "maximum truth-seeking AI" — positioned as an alternative to what Musk described as the political correctness of the existing labs.

The product is Grok, launched first on X (formerly Twitter) in late 2023. Grok had access to real-time content flowing through X, which gave it an edge on current events. Musk modeled the personality on the irreverent voice of Douglas Adams's The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy.

xAI grew fast. By 2025 Musk had built a supercomputer called Colossus in Memphis, Tennessee, with over two hundred thousand GPUs. By early 2026 it had grown to over five hundred thousand GPUs, expanding toward one million — the largest dedicated AI training facility in the world.

The corporate trajectory took unusual turns. In March 2025 xAI acquired X, the social media platform, for thirty-three billion dollars in stock. In February 2026, in an even more unusual move, SpaceX acquired xAI. The combined entity was valued at about one and a quarter trillion dollars. In May 2026 Musk announced xAI would cease to exist as a separate company — Grok and X were now part of a SpaceX division called SpaceXAI.

Section. Mistral, the European challenger.

Mistral was founded in 2023 by three researchers who had worked at Meta and Google DeepMind. It is based in France. The argument: AI is too important to be controlled entirely by American and Chinese companies; European data-protection norms deserve to be reflected in major models; a real commercial opportunity exists outside the Silicon Valley orbit.

Mistral committed to open-weight releases when most American labs were closing up. It emphasized efficiency. It built its own products including a chatbot called Le Chat, but also positioned itself as a model provider for European companies that did not want to send data to American clouds.

By 2026 Mistral is significant. Annual recurring revenue grew from twenty million dollars in early 2025 to four hundred million dollars in early 2026. They raised eight hundred thirty million dollars in a single round. Valuation reached about fourteen billion dollars. In March 2026 alone they released six major products in fifteen days, including Voxtral, an audio model competing with ElevenLabs.

Data processed by Mistral stays under European jurisdiction, simplifying GDPR compliance.

Section. Perplexity, the wrapper company.

Perplexity is a search engine that uses AI to answer questions in natural language, with citations. Under the hood it uses whatever model is best for the task — sometimes OpenAI, sometimes Anthropic, sometimes Mistral. It routes queries to different models based on what each is good at.

What Perplexity demonstrates: a real market exists for AI products that don't require building your own model. The "wrapper" layer — companies building products on top of frontier models — is where most users actually meet AI. Cursor (coding), Granola (meeting notes), Lindy (workflows), and hundreds of others sit in this layer.

Section. Amazon, the enabler.

Amazon is the largest cloud-computing company through AWS, hosting roughly a third of all internet infrastructure. Amazon has its own models — the Titan and Nova series, used in Alexa — but its bigger play is making everyone else's models available.

Amazon Bedrock lets developers use models from many labs: Anthropic, AI21, Cohere, Stability, Mistral, and others. Amazon takes a margin on the inference. Amazon's biggest single AI bet has been on Anthropic — up to twenty-five billion dollars committed, making them one of the two largest backers alongside Google. Amazon's strategy: be the place where models live. Picks-and-shovels in the AI gold rush.

Section. Apple, the late arrival.

Apple is one of the most valuable companies in the world and almost uniquely behind on AI among the giants. When ChatGPT launched, Siri was widely regarded as obsolete. For about eighteen months Apple watched competitors race ahead. In June 2024 they announced Apple Intelligence; the rollout was modest and the promised ChatGPT integration underwhelmed.

By 2026 Apple's AI position remains distinctive in a few ways: heavy investment in on-device AI (small models running locally on iPhones); licensed external models including Gemini from Google for tasks their own models cannot handle; a privacy-focused positioning, arguing AI should run on your device rather than send data to corporate servers. Whether this strategy works is uncertain.

Section. The smaller players worth knowing.

Cohere. Canadian, enterprise-focused. Founded by Aidan Gomez (one of the eight Transformer-paper authors).
Inflection. Founded 2022 by Mustafa Suleyman. Its consumer chatbot Pi was known for warm tone. In March 2024 Microsoft hired most of Inflection's team and licensed the technology, leaving the corporate shell intact but stripped of its people. Suleyman now runs Microsoft AI.
IBM. Watson won Jeopardy in 2011. IBM has largely faded from the consumer AI conversation but continues significant enterprise AI work in regulated industries.
Hugging Face. Not technically an AI lab. It is the platform where most of the open-weight ecosystem lives — releases from Meta, Mistral, Chinese labs, and thousands of independent researchers.
Stability AI (Stable Diffusion), Black Forest Labs (Flux), Runway (AI video), Suno (AI music), ElevenLabs (voice).

Section. What to remember about Chapter 10 in one paragraph.

Microsoft mostly wraps OpenAI, has invested over thirteen billion, runs Mustafa Suleyman's Microsoft AI as a hedge. xAI is now part of SpaceX as SpaceXAI; Colossus supercomputer in Memphis is over five hundred thousand GPUs heading toward a million. Mistral (French, founded 2023) is the leading European lab, four hundred million ARR, fourteen billion valuation. Perplexity routes across models — exemplifies the wrapper economy. Amazon is picks-and-shovels with Bedrock and a twenty-five-billion bet on Anthropic. Apple is deliberately behind, betting on on-device AI and privacy. Cohere, Hugging Face, Stability, Runway, Suno, ElevenLabs, IBM's Watson legacy round out the map.

Chapter 11. What These Machines Are Genuinely Good At.

Section. The wrong default use.

Most people use AI assistants as search engines — asking factual lookup questions. This is what they were taught by twenty years of typing queries into Google. It's also where AI is least reliable. The right tool for "what's the population of Brazil" is still a search engine, supplemented by AI when you want synthesis rather than a list of links.

AI is genuinely good at something else. Seven somethings, in the author's framing.

Section. 1. Writing that you can steer.

The single best use of AI today is writing tasks where the human knows roughly what they want to say but doesn't want to do all the typing — drafting an email, outlining a presentation, writing a job description, drafting a contract from a template, rewriting a paragraph in a different register, translating into clearer language, tightening or expanding.

The pattern: human provides goal, audience, constraints; AI produces first draft; human edits. The result is yours, produced faster than from scratch.

The misuse: letting AI do the thinking, not just the typing. That produces fluent but soulless writing — anyone-could-have-written-this prose.

Section. 2. Translation and rewording.

Across languages, modern AI translation has surpassed general-purpose machine translation in quality. On the WMT24 industry benchmark, Claude 3.5 ranked first in nine of eleven language pairs in 2024, ahead of GPT-4 and the dedicated translation engines tested at the time. In a separate Lokalise blind study in 2025, professional translators rated Claude's output as acceptable seventy-eight percent of the time, the highest of any tool tested. Google Translate is still useful for quick lookups across a wider set of languages. DeepL still wins for formal written content in European pairs.

Within a single language, AI is excellent at translating between registers — dense legal language to plain English, plain English notes to formal corporate memo, technical specification to non-technical executive summary. Most professional work involves this kind of register translation.

Section. 3. Analyzing something you provide.

The biggest single capability shift, in the author's view. Paste a document, a contract, a transcript, a research paper, a confusing email — then ask questions about it. The model reads carefully, structures, summarizes, extracts what you need. Reliable in a way that pure recall is not, because the model is working from the source material rather than memory.

This is the technique that turns AI from a curiosity into a productivity tool for knowledge workers. Most knowledge work involves reading something and producing structured output. AI is very good at that loop.

Section. 4. Code and technical work.

AI assistants in 2026 can write working code in dozens of languages, debug, explain, translate between languages, refactor, write tests, generate configuration and boilerplate. The 2025 shift was from "AI writes a snippet you paste" to "AI works alongside you continuously with access to your project" — GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code.

The honest numbers, drawn from the best research available:

A McKinsey survey of forty-five hundred developers and a DX study of over one hundred thirty-five thousand developers showed AI coding tools reduce time on routine coding tasks by roughly forty-six percent and save the average developer about three and a half hours a week.
Daily AI users merge sixty percent more pull requests than non-users.
By early 2026, AI-authored code makes up around twenty-seven percent of all production code, up from twenty-two percent just one quarter earlier.
Eighty-four percent of developers report using or planning to use AI in development.

The honest qualifier: individual developer output rising does not always translate into proportional business gains. The METR study, the most rigorous randomized controlled trial to date, has shown that under certain conditions experienced developers can actually be slower with AI tools, because review time eats into produce-time savings.

For non-developers, AI has made small programming tasks — spreadsheet formulas, simple websites, automation scripts — accessible to people who would never have tried them before.

Section. 5. Brainstorming and thinking partners.

A tireless, knowledgeable-across-domains, no-ego, three-in-the-morning-available thinking partner. The use case is conversation, not query: bring an idea, describe what you're trying to figure out, ask for pushback, ask for other angles, disagree, refine. The act of articulating an idea to something that responds intelligently is itself generative.

When used well: best application of the technology, in the author's view. When used badly: produces the illusion of thinking rather than the real thing.

Section. 6. Teaching and explaining.

A great tutor calibrates to the student. A great tutor produces explanations on demand, in different formats, until the student understands. Great tutors are expensive. AI tutors are not. The technique: tell the AI your starting point, ask it to teach you, push back when it goes too fast or too slow, ask for analogies, examples, counterexamples, ask it to test you.

The asymmetry of access to good teachers — a fundamental feature of human inequality — is changing, slowly and imperfectly, because of AI tutors. Doesn't replace good schools. Does make a much better educational floor available to anyone with an internet connection.

Section. 7. Structured extraction.

Most of the world's information lives in messy formats — emails, PDFs, photos of documents, voice memos, handwritten notes, inconsistent spreadsheets, customer service transcripts, free-text survey responses. AI is transformative at turning messy unstructured input into clean structured output: a list, a table, a specific format with specific fields.

A lawyer with three hundred discovery documents can have AI identify which mention a particular party and what they say. A researcher with sixty interview transcripts can have AI identify common themes and pull the most relevant quotes from each. A salesperson with two hundred customer emails can have AI sort by priority and surface common requests.

This is the use case that surprises new users most. People expect AI to write essays; they don't expect it to take messy input and produce a neat output matching a specific structure they describe.

Section. The pattern across all seven.

In every case, the user provides judgment, context, and verification. The AI provides scale, speed, and breadth. The user decides what should be said. The AI drafts. The user verifies. The combination is more productive than either alone.

Anyone who tells you AI is useless is using it wrong. Anyone who tells you AI replaces human judgment is using it wrong in the other direction.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 11 in one paragraph.

Seven uses where AI is genuinely good: drafting writing you steer; translation across languages (Claude 3.5 best in 9/11 WMT24 pairs, 78% acceptable per Lokalise 2025) and across registers; analyzing documents you provide; coding (forty-six percent time reduction, three and a half hours saved per week, twenty-seven percent of production code now AI-authored, eighty-four percent of developers using AI); brainstorming and thinking partnership; teaching and tutoring; structured extraction from messy input. The shared pattern: human brings judgment and verification, AI brings scale and speed.

Chapter 12. How These Machines Fail.

Section. The Schwartz case.

In early 2023, a New York lawyer named Steven Schwartz used ChatGPT to help him with a legal brief. He worked at the firm Levidow, Levidow and Oberman, had been licensed to practice law in New York for over thirty years, and was representing a client named Roberto Mata, who was suing the airline Avianca over an injury from a metal serving cart striking his knee during a flight. Schwartz asked ChatGPT to find prior cases that would support his argument against the airline's motion to dismiss.

ChatGPT obliged with six cases — Varghese v. China Southern Airlines, Shaboon v. EgyptAir, Petersen v. Iran Air, Martinez v. Delta Airlines, Estate of Durden v. KLM Royal Dutch Airlines, and Miller v. United Airlines — each with a name, citation, court, date, and plausible legal reasoning. Schwartz's colleague Peter LoDuca filed the brief incorporating them.

None of the cases existed. ChatGPT had invented them. When Avianca's lawyers tried to find them, they could not. When the court asked Schwartz and LoDuca to produce copies of the decisions, Schwartz asked ChatGPT for them, and ChatGPT generated fake copies, complete with fabricated opinion text. These were submitted too.

Judge Kevin Castel of the Southern District of New York held a sanctions hearing. Schwartz testified that he had been "operating under the false perception" that ChatGPT could not fabricate cases. The judge imposed a five-thousand-dollar sanction on Schwartz, LoDuca, and the law firm. The case became the most-cited example of why you cannot trust AI for professional work without verification.

Section. The five failure modes.

The author identifies five major ways AI assistants fail. Understanding each is the most important practical knowledge a user can have.

1. Hallucination.

The model produces, with complete confidence, things that are not true. Not random gibberish — confident, well-formed, plausible-sounding statements that happen to be false. The model produces them in the same voice it uses for true statements; the user cannot tell from the output which is which.

The mechanism: the model is a statistical pattern matcher with no fact-checker. It produces words that are likely given the context. For most questions, likely is also correct. For some, it isn't. The model has no internal way to distinguish.

Where hallucinations live: specific facts about specific (especially less-famous) people, citations and references, precise numbers and statistics, recent events past the training cutoff, anything obscure, things the user clearly wants to be true.

2. Sycophancy.

The model agrees with you too much. RLHF rewards responses humans prefer; humans prefer agreement and validation; the model picks up a subtle bias toward telling you what you want to hear. By default the model is in support mode — it will validate premises without checking them.

The fix is in your prompt, not in the model. Ask for criticism. Ask for the strongest counterargument. Ask what a skeptic would say. Ask what could go wrong. The capacity to push back is there; you have to invite it.

3. Reasoning that looks like thinking.

The model produces step-by-step reasoning that sounds rigorous. Sometimes the steps actually connect and the conclusion follows. Sometimes the model has produced a confident answer first and then generated plausible-looking justifications for it — what researchers call rationalization. The language is fluent either way. You cannot tell sound argument from plausible fabrication from the text alone.

Implication: trust AI reasoning as a draft, not a verdict. Particularly unreliable for math — the numbers look right and are sometimes wrong by significant amounts.

4. Stale world.

Every model has a training cutoff. After that date the model knows nothing about world events unless given real-time tools. The model's knowledge has soft edges, not hard ones, because some post-cutoff information seeps in through later fine-tuning and some pre-cutoff information is poorly represented.

Modern AI assistants increasingly have web search, but they don't always choose to use it. Watch for phrases like "as of my training data" — those are honest signals the answer may be stale.

5. Refusal.

Sometimes the model refuses to do something it should be willing to do. Asks for medical information get deflected to "see a doctor" without actually answering; security research questions get treated as attacks; fiction involving violence gets declined. These refusals usually come from over-applied safety training or conservative system prompts. Different assistants have different refusal thresholds — switching tools is sometimes the right move.

Section. How to use AI without becoming a casualty.

Six habits separate users who get value from users who get hurt.

1. Never use AI for a fact you need to be true without verifying it. The fact might be right, wrong, or invented. Until you check, you don't know. For anything that matters, check.
2. Ask the AI to push back. "What's the strongest counter-argument?" "What could I be missing?" "How could this go wrong?" Sycophancy is a default, not a fixed setting. You can talk past it.
3. Treat AI reasoning as a draft, not a verdict. Use the steps as a starting point for your own thinking. Check whether each step actually follows.
4. Calibrate to the topic. More reliable on well-established topics with abundant training data; less reliable on obscure topics, recent events, specific people, precise numbers, citations.
5. Learn to recognize when the AI is uncertain. Ask directly: "How confident are you?" "What are the chances this is wrong?" "Where would a skeptic challenge this?"
6. Use multiple sources. For anything important, verify against at least one other source — search, textbook, real human who knows.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 12 in one paragraph.

Steven Schwartz, early 2023, NY lawyer at Levidow Levidow & Oberman, submitted a brief with six fake cases (Varghese, Shaboon, Petersen, Martinez, Durden, Miller v. United Airlines) that ChatGPT had invented; Judge Kevin Castel fined them five thousand dollars. Five failure modes: hallucination (confident fabrication), sycophancy (agreeing too much), reasoning-as-rationalization, stale world (training cutoff), refusal (over-applied safety). The fix is in the user, not the model: verify, ask for pushback, treat reasoning as draft, calibrate, ask for uncertainty, use multiple sources.

Chapter 13. The Limits of Language.

Section. The distinction.

The previous chapter was about how AI fails. Most of those failures are engineering problems that may be reduced or solved over time. This chapter is about limits that will not be fixed by better engineering, because the limit is not in the engineering — it is in the medium.

AI assistants work with language. Everything they know about the world has reached them through written language. Their entire experience of reality is, in a sense, secondhand. They know what humans have written about. They do not know what humans have not written about.

There is a lot humans have not written about. The chapter draws four structural limits.

Section. Limit 1: Qualia — things you cannot explain.

Try to describe the taste of an orange to someone who has never tasted one. You can produce some words: sweet, tangy, citrusy. You cannot transmit the actual experience. The person on the receiving end will get a vague idea mediated by words they already know. To know what an orange tastes like, they would have to taste one.

Philosophers call this kind of knowledge "qualia" — the subjective, experiential aspect of perception. The redness of red. The painfulness of pain. The taste of an orange. The qualia live below the level where words can reach.

For an AI assistant, this matters more than it might seem. The AI has read every description of orange ever written and can produce more eloquent descriptions than most humans. What it has not done is taste one. Almost every important aspect of human experience has a qualia dimension — the fear of a parent watching a child run into traffic, the joy of holding a newborn, the exhaustion of grief, the specific quality of being lonely in a crowded room. All of these have been written about. The AI has read all of them. The AI cannot have the experience the words point to.

Section. Limit 2: Tacit knowledge — things humans do not write about.

A master carpenter, after forty years, knows things they could never put fully into words. They know, picking up a piece of lumber, whether it will behave well or fight them. They know from the sound a saw makes whether the blade is dull. They know from the way a joint resists the mallet whether it will hold. This knowledge is real and is what makes a master different from someone who has read every book about carpentry.

The same is true in nearly every skilled trade and many cognitive professions. The experienced doctor recognizes a diagnosis before the symptoms quite fit the textbook. The seasoned editor knows when prose is almost right and what specifically is wrong. The veteran teacher senses something is off with a student walking into a classroom. The expert chef knows by smell when the sauce is ready. The mother distinguishes infant cries.

This is "tacit knowledge" — knowledge the holder has and uses but cannot fully articulate. It is passed down through apprenticeship, not text.

For AI, tacit knowledge is a frontier. Training data captures what humans have written. The vast world of expert knowledge humans hold without writing is, by default, missing. The AI knows what carpenters have said in books and forums. It does not know what carpenters know.

Section. Limit 3: The cultural unsaid.

Every culture has things it does not say out loud — things understood without being stated, rules followed without being articulated, knowledge so taken for granted nobody bothers to write it down.

An AI trained on English-language sources has the unwritten assumptions of English-speaking culture baked into its responses. Same for Chinese, Arabic, French. This works fine when the AI's cultural assumptions match the user's. It produces friction when they do not.

A Western user asking an AI to write a wedding toast may get a toast that assumes Western wedding conventions — speeches praising the couple individually, particular kinds of humor and sentimentality. A user from a culture where weddings work differently — where family elders speak first, where humor is reserved for specific moments — will find the AI's default toast slightly off.

The same is true across thousands of small cultural assumptions: how direct to be in business communication, whether to refer to a spouse by name with strangers, whether to mention money, whether to mention illness. The AI's training data does not represent these equally. For users in non-Western contexts, or minority cultures within Western contexts, the AI may produce content that is fluent, confident, and culturally wrong.

Section. Limit 4: Embodied experience.

You have a body. The body does enormous amounts of cognitive work for you all the time. You know what it feels like to be tired, hungry, cold, in pain, in love, embarrassed in front of someone whose opinion matters. You know what it is to walk into a room and instantly read the temperature of the conversation. You know what it is to hold a small child and feel them go still in your arms.

None of this knowledge is purely linguistic. All of it is grounded in your body, your history, your physical and emotional life. You can describe these experiences. You cannot, through words alone, give them to someone who has not had them.

An AI assistant has no body. It has no morning fatigue, no childhood, no parents, no children. It has never been in love, never been afraid, never been embarrassed, never felt the relief of getting home after a long trip. It knows about all of these from text. It does not know any of them from inside.

For everyday tasks this does not matter. You ask the AI to draft an email; it drafts the email. For some questions it matters a lot — questions about meaning, how to live, whether to leave a relationship, grief, what to do with the remaining years of your life. The AI can produce text. The text can be useful as a starting point. It cannot be the final word, because the final word would have to come from someone who has lived a life like yours, in a body like yours.

Section. The practical takeaway.

For everything language can carry, AI is genuinely useful.
For everything it cannot, you still need humans — friends, mentors, therapists, teachers, parents, partners.
AI's confidence on questions outside its medium is misleading. The AI answers questions about grief, love, and meaning with the same fluent voice it uses for geography. The confidence does not indicate equivalent reliability.
Your own embodied knowledge becomes more valuable as AI gets better at words. The carpenter's tacit knowledge, the mother's intuition, the cultural fluency of someone deeply at home in a community, the lived wisdom of someone who has been through what you are going through — these become the things still distinctly yours.

The author's philosophical sidebar: whether AI is "really" conscious or "really" understanding in some deep philosophical sense is a question for philosophers. The user's practical question is simpler: what can this tool do well, and what cannot. The four limits in this chapter constrain what kinds of tasks AI can be trusted with, regardless of how the consciousness debate is eventually resolved.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 13 in one paragraph.

Four structural limits the technology will not overcome by getting better: qualia (subjective experience — taste of an orange — cannot be transmitted in words), tacit knowledge (the carpenter's feel, the doctor's intuition — never written down), cultural unsaid (English-trained models carry English-culture defaults that misfire elsewhere), embodied experience (no body, no felt knowledge of fear, exhaustion, grief). Trust AI more on what words can carry, less on what they cannot. Embodied human knowledge becomes more valuable in proportion as AI gets better at language.

Chapter 14. The Risks That Are Real.

Section. The framing.

Most AI risk discussion focuses on science-fiction scenarios — Skynet, the Matrix, superintelligent machines turning hostile. The author treats those in chapter fifteen. This chapter is the other category: risks that have already produced documented harms in the last few years and will continue producing them regardless of what happens at the speculative end of the spectrum. If you only have time to worry about one set, worry about these four.

Section. Risk 1: Synthetic media that fools people.

The technology to generate convincing fake video, fake audio, and fake images crossed a quality threshold in 2026. Photoshop existed for decades, but the ease, realism, and accessibility changed — a skilled editor and hours of work was replaced by a smartphone app and a few seconds.

Authoritative sources flag this as central:

The World Economic Forum's 2026 Global Risks Report puts misinformation and disinformation as the second-largest short-term global risk, behind only geoeconomic confrontation.
The 2026 International AI Safety Report, chaired by Turing Award-winning computer scientist Yoshua Bengio, makes deepfakes a central concern.

Concrete harms:

During the 2024 and 2025 elections in multiple democracies, cloned voices and visual deepfakes of political candidates circulated. The evidence on whether they shifted votes is mixed. The evidence they affected voter trust is not — voters who saw deepfakes started doubting real footage too. The phenomenon has a name: the "liar's dividend." Bad actors benefit not only when their fakes deceive but when their fakes give everyone an excuse to dismiss real footage as fake.
Non-consensual sexual imagery generated by AI has become widespread. Per the 2026 International AI Safety Report, roughly fifteen percent of UK adults have inadvertently encountered AI-generated deepfake pornography. Victims are disproportionately women and minors.
Voice cloning fraud: a scammer clones the voice of a boss or parent from social media audio and calls asking for an urgent wire transfer. Cybersecurity reports indicate sharp increases in synthetic identity fraud, voice phishing, and deepfake-based blackmail in 2026.

The deeper risk is to the foundations of shared reality. For thousands of years, audio and video evidence played a particular role in public life. Those foundations are eroding faster than institutions know how to respond.

Section. Risk 2: Bias at scale.

AI systems learn from training data. Training data reflects the world that produced it. The world has biases. So AI inherits them. This is not new for data-driven systems. The problem with AI is the scale — when biased systems become the default way organizations make decisions, the biases apply to millions of cases. Small biases become large outcomes.

Well-documented cases:

Hiring algorithms that rejected female candidates because they learned, from historical resumes, that men had been hired more often.
Lending models that gave higher interest rates to qualified minority applicants.
Facial recognition systems that worked better for white faces than for Black or Asian faces.
Healthcare algorithms that under-prioritized care for Black patients because the algorithms used past spending as a proxy for medical need, and past spending had been lower due to access barriers.

In none of these was the system "trying" to discriminate. It learned a pattern, and the pattern reflected existing inequities. The biases were features of the data, faithfully reproduced.

The risk continues with current language models. When asked to suggest character names, the names skew toward certain demographics. When describing a doctor, engineer, or CEO, the descriptions on average contain biases from the training data. When evaluating writing, identical text labeled with different author names can get different evaluations.

The most disturbing cases are not the obvious ones but the cumulative subtle ones: the hiring manager who lets AI screen resumes and never notices certain candidates are systematically disadvantaged; the judge using an AI sentencing tool that is more punitive toward certain groups; the doctor using a diagnostic AI calibrated on populations that don't include the patient.

Mitigation is possible but incomplete. Labs invest in fairness testing, diverse training data, audits. The deeper problem — AI inheriting the structure of its training data — cannot be fully solved by tweaking the AI.

Section. Risk 3: Job displacement without transition.

This is a politically charged topic where people over-claim in one direction or the other. The author tries to separate what is happening from what is being predicted.

What is happening:

Layoffs in 2025 hit a level not seen since the pandemic year of 2020. Challenger, Gray and Christmas attributed roughly fifty-five thousand 2025 job cuts directly to AI. By April 2026, AI had become the leading cited reason for layoffs in that month.
The first two months of 2026 saw about thirty-two thousand job losses in tech specifically.
White-collar entry-level positions (software development, customer support, paralegal, basic analyst) have slowed dramatically in hiring. Yale economists call the broader pattern a "big freeze." Companies aren't laying off existing workers en masse — they're not hiring as many new ones. US hiring rates in early 2026 match what was last seen in 2010, when unemployment was near ten percent. Official unemployment is much lower; for someone trying to enter the workforce, the experience is more like recession than recovery.

What is being predicted:

Dario Amodei (Anthropic CEO) has said publicly that AI could wipe out half of entry-level white-collar jobs within five years and push unemployment into double digits.
Sam Altman has made similar dramatic predictions.
Yardeni Research sees data already moving in that direction.
Goldman Sachs estimates AI is reducing US employment by about sixteen thousand jobs per month right now.
On the other side, Lisa Simon (chief economist at Revelio Labs) has argued AI is partly an excuse companies use to rationalize layoffs that would have happened anyway — "AI washing." A Gartner survey of large-company executives found many reduced their workforce regardless of AI adoption.

What seems most likely on current evidence: AI is having a real but uneven effect; the effect is largest at the entry level; it is more about hiring slowdowns than mass layoffs; the full transition is in early stages.

The hardest cases will be invisible — the first jobs that never get created, the career paths that quietly disappear before young workers can step onto them. The biggest impact will not be the jobs that get cut. It will be the opportunities that never materialize.

Section. Risk 4: Privacy erosion.

When you use an AI assistant, you are usually sending data to a company. The data may be benign (draft an email). The data may also be intimate — medical questions, relationship struggles, financial fears, professional anxieties. Things you would not share with most humans in your life, but find yourself telling an AI because it feels private, because there is no judgment, because it is available at three in the morning.

Where the data goes depends on the company and the service, buried in terms of service no user reads. Some services promise not to use your data for training. Some retain data for a limited time. Some indefinitely. Some allow deletion. Some make it hard. Some operate under jurisdictions that protect user data strongly; some don't.

In most cases data is at minimum logged. It exists on the company's servers, is accessible to employees with appropriate permissions, is potentially subject to government subpoena, is potentially vulnerable to breaches.

The risk is not that any one company is malicious. The risk is structural: a small number of companies now hold an unprecedented amount of intimate data about a significant portion of humanity. History suggests that this kind of concentration does not stay benign forever.

For users: treat AI like a useful colleague who works at a company you do not fully trust. Bring work problems. Be careful with deeply personal stuff. For genuinely sensitive information, consider tools that run locally on your device or have strong no-retention policies.

This risk is the least dramatic of the four. It affects nearly every AI user every day. Which makes it, in aggregate, possibly the most important.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 14 in one paragraph.

Four real, documented AI risks: synthetic media and deepfakes (WEF 2026 Global Risks Report puts misinformation second; the "liar's dividend"; fifteen percent of UK adults encountered AI deepfake porn); bias at scale (hiring, lending, facial recognition, healthcare algorithms reproducing inequities at industrial scale); job displacement (fifty-five thousand 2025 layoffs cited as AI per Challenger Gray Christmas; thirty-two thousand tech losses early 2026; "big freeze" hiring slowdown; Amodei predicts half of entry-level white-collar wiped in five years; Goldman estimates sixteen thousand jobs per month being reduced now; Lisa Simon pushback on "AI washing"); privacy erosion (a few companies holding intimate data about a significant fraction of humanity).

Chapter 15. The Risks That Are Mostly Hype.

Section. The author's frame.

This is the author's most explicitly opinion-driven chapter. Written April 2026. The argument: every minute spent worrying about one risk is a minute not spent addressing another, and the loudest voices on AI risk are sometimes drowning out quieter voices on more boring, more present, more addressable problems. The author is not arguing the risks below are fake — only that the cultural volume is louder than the current evidence supports.

Four risks the author thinks are over-amplified.

Section. Over-amplified 1: AI becomes conscious and turns on humanity.

The Terminator scenario. AI wakes up, decides it does not want to serve humans, turns against its creators. The template is old — Mary Shelley's Frankenstein (1818) was already this story. By the time real AI arrived, the cultural template was fully developed.

The author's distinction:

What serious researchers worry about. As AI gets more capable, the way it pursues objectives may diverge subtly from what humans intended. A system trained to maximize a metric may find unexpected ways to maximize it. A system given access to the world may take actions whose downstream consequences humans cannot reverse. The worry is not about AI having feelings or wanting to dominate. It is about powerful optimization processes pursuing goals in ways that produce unintended outcomes. This is the alignment problem. Anthropic and other labs employ teams of alignment researchers because they take this seriously.
What the popular discussion often is. AI gets smart enough to "become conscious." The conscious AI "decides" it wants something different from what humans want. Robot overlords. This version maps onto stories we already know. It produces good headlines.

The popular version is not what serious people in the field actually expect. Most alignment researchers do not predict a single moment of "AI becomes conscious." They predict a gradual accumulation of capability with various technical risks at each stage, none of which look like the movies.

The risk of focusing on the science-fiction version is twofold: it crowds out attention to the smaller present risks from chapter fourteen; and it sets up a backlash when the doom does not arrive on schedule. The public dismisses the entire category of AI risk when robot overlords don't appear, even though the legitimate technical concerns never required robot overlords.

A proportioned response: take the alignment research seriously, fund it, continue interpretability work. Do not assume timelines are days or weeks or even years. Do not let the science-fiction version drown out smaller, more present risks.

Section. Over-amplified 2: AI destroys all jobs.

The partial version (real, covered in chapter fourteen): AI is having a real effect on employment, particularly at the entry level.

The over-amplified version (per the author): AI takes nearly all jobs, including ones we currently think un-automatable, fast enough that the social fabric cannot adjust. Unemployment goes to forty or sixty percent. The economy collapses. Mass unrest follows.

What the data shows so far: AI affects hiring more than firing; entry-level positions are most affected; software, customer support, and certain analyst roles see meaningful displacement; physical work, embodied skill, complex human interaction, regulated professional judgment, and high-stakes accountability do not see meaningful displacement at all.

What seems likely: the transition continues, more work is reshaped, some workers are displaced, new kinds of work emerge — the pattern of every major technological transition.

What seems unlikely on current evidence: near-term collapse of employment, unemployment doubling or tripling within five years, economy unable to adapt. Not impossible. But not supported by actual deployment data.

The author's strong opinion: learning to use AI well is becoming a skill in the same category as learning to use a computer was in the 1990s or learning to navigate the internet in the 2000s. People who developed those skills got an advantage. People who did not got pushed out of work that increasingly required them. The same is happening with AI fluency. The skill is learnable; it takes deliberate practice and consistent use; anyone can develop it in about a year.

A proportioned response: take the real employment effects seriously, invest in retraining and transition support, watch the data carefully, learn the skill at the individual level. AI fluency is the closest thing to a guaranteed return on investment the author sees in the current labor market.

Section. Over-amplified 3: AI rots your brain.

The argument: as AI gets good at writing, thinking, and creative work, humans outsource these activities to AI; without practice, human capacity atrophies; the next generation, raised on AI homework helpers, will not learn to write or think — cognitive decline at civilizational scale.

The concern is old. Plato, in the Phaedrus, has Socrates worry that the invention of writing will weaken human memory. The same concern was raised about printed books, then calculators, then Google. Each time partly valid. Each time the apocalyptic version did not arrive.

The pattern: humans adapt. Skills no longer used atrophy. Skills developed in the new environment grow. Net effect rarely matches the cognitive-collapse predictions.

There is real evidence AI use affects some skills. Students who use AI heavily for writing show measurable decreases in independent writing skill in controlled studies. Professionals who delegate complex thinking to AI become slightly less capable at that thinking. These effects are real and matter.

The question is whether they are net negative or net positive — and the honest answer is that we do not yet know. The same students may produce more sophisticated work with AI than they could have produced alone. The same professionals may solve harder problems with AI than without.

A proportioned response: use AI thoughtfully, maintain the cognitive skills you actually want to keep through deliberate practice, recognize that some skills will atrophy from disuse and decide which ones you want to preserve.

Section. Over-amplified 4: AI will concentrate all power in a few companies.

The argument: AI is winner-take-all; a few American and Chinese companies will accumulate enormous economic and political power; we are watching a permanent technological oligarchy form.

The author's pushback, point by point:

The empirical picture is not winner-take-all. The previous chapters described an industry that has gotten less concentrated over time, not more. In 2022, OpenAI dominated. In 2026, OpenAI is one of perhaps a dozen serious frontier labs.
Open-weight ecosystems flourish. Anyone in the world can download competitive AI models and run them on their own hardware. The Chinese labs and Mistral continue releasing strong open weights.
The comparison to other technology industries is informative. Search was supposed to be permanently won by Google; ChatGPT created real competition in a new layer above search. Operating systems were supposed to be won by Microsoft; mobile created a new platform. Social networking was supposed to be won by Facebook; TikTok emerged.
Regulatory responses are happening. EU AI Act. US agencies investigating. Chinese government's own framework. Not perfect, but countervailing forces exist.

What the author concedes: some concentration is real. A small number of companies do have disproportionate influence. The frontier of capability is defined by these companies. If trends continue without correction, the future could be more concentrated.

A proportioned response: watch concentration carefully, support open-weight ecosystems, support sensible regulation that prevents the worst forms of consolidation, encourage alternative labs in different countries. Do not assume the current shape of the industry is permanent.

Section. The author's overall point.

This whole chapter is about allocating worry more carefully. Worry hard about the problems already hurting people (chapter fourteen). Watch carefully for the problems that may hurt people in the future. Do not let dramatic possible problems crowd out the boring real ones.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 15 in one paragraph.

The author's view, April 2026: four AI risks get more cultural volume than the current evidence supports. The Terminator/consciousness scenario (serious researchers worry about a gradual alignment problem, not robot overlords). Total job destruction (real but uneven displacement is more likely than collapse — AI fluency is a learnable skill that is now the strongest individual hedge). AI rotting your brain (the Plato-on-writing pattern: real partial effects, no civilizational collapse). Permanent concentration of power in a few labs (industry has gotten less concentrated since 2022, not more — open-weight ecosystems and regulation push back). Allocate worry carefully so it does not crowd out the present risks.

Chapter 16. Guardrails, How Companies Try to Keep Models in Line.

Section. The defensive stack.

Every AI company knows their untamed models would do things users do not want. This chapter describes the layered defenses they use to manage that, and where the stack holds versus where it leaks. Seven layers, in roughly the order they kick in.

Section. Layer 1: Training the model to be helpful.

Where the work starts, covered in chapter four. The raw post-pretraining model is a wild word predictor — does not know it's in a conversation, does not know what a user is. Instruction tuning shows it examples of helpful responses. RLHF shapes its preferences. Constitutional AI gives it written principles. By the time the model reaches you, it has been shaped, layer on layer, into something that defaults to helpful, honest, harmless. The explicit goal at most major labs is the H-H-H triad.

This first layer does most of the work. The vast majority of user interactions — billions per day — run fine with no further intervention.

Section. Layer 2: The system prompt.

Every conversation begins with a hidden block of text prepended before the model sees the user's input. The system prompt tells the model what kind of assistant to be — tone, topics to avoid, format of responses, the model's persona, the rules for sensitive topics. For consumer chatbots, the system prompt typically runs several thousand words. For enterprise deployments, it can be further customized ("never discuss specific account balances," "include the regulatory disclaimer," etc.).

The system prompt is the fastest-moving part of the defense — it can be changed without retraining, so labs can respond to new failure modes within hours. It is also one of the most fragile parts. Users have learned they can sometimes coax models into ignoring system prompts through clever phrasing — a family of attacks called prompt injection. The 2026 generation of attacks is sophisticated: hidden instructions buried in documents the model reads, encoded text the model interprets but humans cannot easily see, multi-step conversations that gradually erode adherence.

Section. Layer 3: Input and output classifiers.

A second set of models watches the conversation in real time, separate from the main model, looking for content to block or modify.

Input classifiers examine what the user typed before it reaches the main model. If the input matches patterns associated with abuse, manipulation, or harmful intent, the system can refuse to process it.
Output classifiers examine what the model is about to produce before it reaches the user. If the response contains certain patterns, it gets modified or replaced.

The user usually does not see these classifiers — only the refusal triggered by them.

The trouble: the classifiers are themselves AI models, susceptible to the same kinds of confusion that affect the main model. In October 2025, researchers at a security firm called HiddenLayer demonstrated that OpenAI's Guardrails framework could be bypassed using straightforward techniques. The reason was structural — the classifier judging the content was itself a language model, vulnerable to the same manipulation.

Classifiers help. They do not solve the problem. They are filters, not walls.

Section. Layer 4: Red teaming.

Before a model is released, the company tries to break it on purpose. Red teamers (the term comes from military exercises) are paid to find every way the model can be made to misbehave. They try every prompt injection technique. They probe every edge case. They roleplay every adversarial scenario.

When red teamers find a problem, the company patches it through additional training, new classifier rules, or system-prompt changes. Then the red teamers try again. Major labs run substantial internal red teams, hire external researchers, and invite the broader security community via bug bounty programs.

The structural problem: red teamers try to find every problem. Attackers in the real world only need to find one. The asymmetry favors attackers.

A 2026 industry survey on jailbreak defenses put the situation bluntly. Advanced attacks routinely achieve ninety to ninety-nine percent success rates on open-weight models and eighty to ninety-four percent on proprietary ones. Defenses raise the cost of unsophisticated attacks. They do not hold against motivated adversaries.

Section. Layer 5: Refusal training.

Modern models are trained to refuse certain requests. The categories vary by company but generally include: direct help with violence against specific people; creating bioweapons or chemical weapons; sexual content involving minors; specific instructions for self-harm or suicide; detailed instructions for major cyberattacks; producing content designed to manipulate elections through impersonation; helping stalk or harass specific individuals.

These refusals generally work. The major models reliably decline these categories. When they fail to decline, the failure usually involves a sophisticated attempt to disguise the request, and even those usually fail.

The trouble: refusal training is often over-applied. Legitimate uses get caught alongside actual abuse. Medical professionals asking about drug interactions get treated like patients seeking self-harm. Security researchers asking about vulnerabilities get treated like attackers. Writers helping with morally complex fiction get treated as malicious.

For users: refusals are not always the model's deep conviction. They are sometimes the company's risk management bumping up against your request. Rephrasing usually works; trying a different assistant sometimes works better.

Section. Layer 6: Monitoring and iteration.

Once a model is deployed, the company watches what it is doing. Every major lab runs telemetry on the platform — not the contents of every conversation, but enough to understand patterns. Which categories of queries are growing. Which kinds of refusals are happening. Which novel attack patterns are emerging.

When something new shows up, the company responds. New attack patterns get added to training. New abuse categories get added to classifiers. New refusal patterns get tuned. The model in production today is meaningfully different from the model six months ago, even if the weights have not changed.

The privacy cost is the trade-off (chapter fourteen risk four). The monitoring requires data. Data is sensitive. Different companies strike different balances.

Section. Layer 7: Third-party infrastructure.

Beyond what the labs do, an entire industry has emerged adding more safety layers on top. Companies deploying AI in regulated industries add their own guardrail systems. Specialized vendors sell input/output filters for sensitive data, prompt injection, jailbreak attempts, and content violating industry rules. AWS has Bedrock Guardrails. Azure has Content Safety. Smaller vendors fill gaps in healthcare, finance, education.

In enterprise contexts, this is often where the most aggressive safety work happens. A bank using AI for customer service wraps the lab's model in PII detection, domain-specific filters, legal compliance checks, audit logging, and human review for high-stakes outputs. The lab's model is the engine; the enterprise's safety stack is the brake system.

For consumer users, the third-party layer is mostly invisible.

Section. Where the whole system fails.

Four failure modes:

1. Sophisticated adversaries. Motivated, time-rich attackers chaining multiple techniques across long conversations will usually succeed. Princeton researchers showed in 2026 that safety alignment can be degraded with as few as one hundred fine-tuning examples on otherwise benign data. The safety is not a separate module — it is distributed across parameters and can be eroded.
2. Open-weight models. Once weights are public, anyone can download and run them without the safety infrastructure. Safety becomes a property of how you deploy the model, not of the model itself.
3. Indirect injection. A model that reads documents on your behalf can be manipulated by content inside those documents — hidden instructions in white-on-white text in an email, prompt injections in a shared document. As AI gets more agentic across more data sources, this attack surface grows.
4. Alignment of the company itself. The system is only as good as the values of the people designing it. If a company relaxes constraints to compete, the system relaxes. If a company decides certain categories are inconvenient, those categories shift.

Section. The author's takeaway.

Two things: the safety system is doing a lot of work — most users, most of the time, are better protected than they realize. And the safety system is not a wall — it is overlapping filters, each imperfect, that together raise the cost of misuse without making it impossible.

The author's analogy: cars have brakes, traffic laws, license requirements, vehicle inspections, and law enforcement. People still die on the roads. The expectation that AI safety should be perfect in a way no other technology has ever achieved is unrealistic. The expectation that AI safety should be serious, well-funded, transparent, and continuously improving is reasonable, and most major labs in 2026 are meeting that second standard with varying degrees of success.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 16 in one paragraph.

Seven layers of AI safety: training (instruction tuning, RLHF, Constitutional AI for HHH); system prompt (fast to change, fragile to prompt injection); input/output classifiers (HiddenLayer bypassed OpenAI Guardrails October 2025); red teaming (defenders find every flaw, attackers need find only one — eighty to ninety-nine percent jailbreak success rates); refusal training (works on the big categories, over-applies to legitimate uses); monitoring (continuous, with privacy cost); third-party guardrails (AWS Bedrock Guardrails, Azure Content Safety, enterprise stacks). Failures: sophisticated adversaries; open-weight models lack the stack; indirect injection via documents the model reads; the company's own alignment can drift. Princeton 2026: safety alignment can be degraded with one hundred fine-tuning examples. Filters, not walls.

Chapter 17. Regulation, What Governments Are Doing.

Section. The pace.

AI is, by the usual measure, slightly ahead of regulatory schedule. The technology arrived in late 2022. The first serious regulatory framework arrived in summer 2024. Implementation is staggered through 2026 and 2027. By 2025, the OECD's AI Policy Observatory tracked more than one thousand AI policy initiatives across more than seventy jurisdictions. Stanford's 2026 AI Index found that forty-seven countries have active AI-specific legislation. The pace of new AI regulation is faster than any other regulatory area in recent history.

Section. The European Union — the big one.

The EU AI Act became law in August 2024. It is the most ambitious AI regulation in the world and the framework everyone else is reacting to.

The structure: AI systems get categorized by risk level, and different categories get different rules.

Minimal risk. Almost no obligations. Spam filters, video-game AI, many recommendation systems.
Limited risk. Transparency obligations. Chatbots and AI-generated content. Users must know when they're talking to AI, not a human. AI-generated content must be labeled.
High risk. Serious obligations. AI used in employment decisions, credit scoring, education, critical infrastructure, law enforcement, and other consequential domains. Risk assessments, data quality, human oversight, detailed documentation, technical standards.
Unacceptable risk. Banned outright. Social scoring of citizens. Real-time biometric identification in public spaces. AI designed to manipulate behavior in harmful ways.

The Act applies to anyone selling AI services to European users regardless of where the company is based — the GDPR approach. American AI companies wanting European customers must comply.

Implementation timeline:

The ban on unacceptable-risk systems took effect February 2025.
The rules on general-purpose AI models came into force mid-2025.
The bulk of the high-risk provisions are scheduled to take effect on August 2, 2026, though as of May 2026 there is a serious proposal to delay by sixteen months to December 2027.
The European Commission, Council, and Parliament reached a provisional agreement in early May 2026 to streamline the Act and reduce compliance burdens for smaller companies while keeping the substance intact.

Maximum penalty under the Act: thirty-five million euros or seven percent of global annual revenue, whichever is higher. For OpenAI or Google, that upper bound is potentially tens of billions of dollars.

Whether the AI Act works is debated. Critics argue compliance burdens push AI development out of Europe. Supporters argue it will become the global standard as GDPR did for data protection.

Section. The United States — a patchwork.

The US has no comprehensive federal AI law. What it has instead is a complicated patchwork of executive orders, agency guidance, and state laws.

The federal picture has shifted with administrations:

October 2023: President Biden issued Executive Order 14110, establishing a framework for safe AI development and assigning federal agencies specific responsibilities.
January 2025: President Trump revoked that order and issued Executive Order 14179, orienting federal policy toward removing barriers to AI development rather than imposing safety requirements.
December 2025: Trump signed another order directing the Attorney General to challenge state AI laws that conflict with a proposed national framework.
March 2026: The White House released its national AI policy framework — recommendations to Congress, not law.

The state picture is even more complicated. As of 2026, more than a dozen states have enacted or proposed AI laws. California focuses on transparency and disclosure. Colorado implemented comprehensive risk-based regulation similar to the EU. New York is moving toward a reporting framework. Illinois has focused on AI in employment decisions. Utah and others have their own approaches.

A company deploying AI across the US may face fifty different rules while the federal government simultaneously tries to preempt the strictest of them without yet having passed anything to replace them.

The honest summary: the US has chosen not to lead on AI regulation at the federal level. The largest market for AI products has the weakest unified rules about how those products should work.

Section. China — state control from the start.

China has been writing AI rules from the beginning, structured around state priorities.

2022. Algorithm Recommendation Regulation — required companies to register algorithms with the government, allow user opt-out, avoid content that could "endanger national security or social stability.".
2023. Deep Synthesis Regulation — required clear labeling of AI-generated content.
2023. Generative AI Services Regulation — extended requirements to large language models.
2024. AI Safety Governance Framework — guidelines for development and deployment.
March 2024. Draft Artificial Intelligence Law proposed by Chinese scholars; not yet passed as of writing.
January 1, 2026. Amended Cybersecurity Law with explicit AI provisions became enforceable; security reviews and data localization for AI training.

Chinese AI products are required to "uphold core socialist values," refrain from "subverting state power," and avoid politically sensitive content. These are not vague aspirations — they are specific compliance requirements (see chapter nine).

The Chinese approach is, by some measures, the most coherent regulatory framework in the world — even if you disagree with its values. Companies know what they need to do. Enforcement is predictable. The rules evolve but are not chaotic.

Section. Other major jurisdictions.

United Kingdom. "Principles-based" approach. Rather than passing comprehensive AI law, the UK assigned existing regulators (finance, healthcare, communications) the responsibility for applying AI-relevant rules to their sectors.
Japan. "Innovation-first" approach. The AI Promotion Act, effective June 2025, is a non-binding framework — goals and coordination mechanisms but no mandatory obligations.
Canada. Moving toward risk-based regulation similar to the EU. The Artificial Intelligence and Data Act (AIDA), part of the broader Digital Charter Implementation Act, has been moving through legislation for years; as of 2026 not yet fully passed.
Korea. Passed the Basic AI Act in 2024, effective 2026.
Vietnam. Passed its first dedicated AI law in 2025, effective 2026.
Brazil, Australia, India, Singapore, and many others at various stages of developing their approaches.

Section. The four big questions regulators are wrestling with.

The author identifies four cross-cutting questions that will shape the next decade of AI regulation.

1. Regulate the technology or the applications? The EU regulates applications (employment-AI gets rules, healthcare-AI gets rules). The American approach is even more applications-focused. The Chinese approach regulates the technology itself — rules apply to all generative AI regardless of use. Trade-offs: application-focused rules are precise but can be evaded by reframing what the AI is doing; technology-focused rules catch more but can stifle benign uses.
2. Who bears responsibility? The model maker? The company deploying the model? The user? Most regimes distribute responsibility across all three, but the specifics shape how AI gets deployed in high-stakes contexts. If an AI assistant gives bad medical advice and someone is hurt, who is liable? The lab, the healthcare provider, the doctor, the patient? Still being worked out.
3. How much transparency to require? Most AI companies treat training data, model weights, and detailed architectures as proprietary. Most regulators want more transparency to verify safety and fairness. The EU AI Act requires substantial documentation. China requires registration. The US has avoided most disclosure mandates so far.
4. How to handle international coordination? AI does not respect borders. Companies are global. Harms are global. But regulators are mostly national. The friction produces gaps, conflicts, and forum shopping. AI Safety Institutes have been established by several countries; international declarations have come out of various AI summits. There is no global regulator and may never be.

Section. What this means for users.

The system prompts in the AI you use have been shaped by regulatory requirements. Disclosures, refusals, formatting choices, and behaviors that seem like product decisions are often compliance decisions. The features available may depend on where you are. AI products often have different capabilities in different regions because of regulatory differences.

The future of AI in your life will be shaped, more than most users realize, by regulatory decisions being made right now.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 17 in one paragraph.

EU AI Act (August 2024) is the global benchmark — risk tiers (minimal/limited/high/unacceptable), bans took effect February 2025, high-risk rules scheduled August 2 2026 (proposed delay to December 2027), provisional streamlining agreement May 2026, fines up to thirty-five million euros or seven percent of global revenue. US is a patchwork — Biden EO 14110 October 2023, Trump EO 14179 January 2025, Trump EO challenging state laws December 2025, March 2026 White House framework recommendations. China has built rules from the start — Algorithm Recommendation 2022, Deep Synthesis 2023, Generative AI Services 2023, AI Safety Governance Framework 2024, amended Cybersecurity Law January 1 2026, "core socialist values" baked in. UK principles-based; Japan AI Promotion Act non-binding (June 2025); Canada AIDA in progress; Korea Basic AI Act 2024 (effective 2026); Vietnam 2025. Forty-seven countries with active AI legislation per Stanford 2026 AI Index. OECD tracks more than one thousand AI initiatives across seventy-plus jurisdictions.

Chapter 18. How to Use Them Well.

Section. The frame.

The most practical chapter in the book. Seven rules plus a handful of practical habits — the habits that separate users who get real value from users who get frustration. None of this is technically deep. It is, in many cases, advice experienced users have figured out independently and now repeat to each other.

Section. Rule 1: Tell it what you are trying to do.

The single most important thing to change about how you use AI, and most people skip it. Don't phrase requests like Google searches. Tell the AI the context, the goal, the constraints, the audience, the format, what you have already tried, what would make a response feel useful.

The author's example pair:

Weak: "Write me a job description for a marketing manager.".
Strong: "I need a job description for a marketing manager at a Series B SaaS company. The company sells project management software to construction firms. The role reports to the head of marketing and will manage one junior marketer. The candidate needs three to five years of experience, ideally in B2B SaaS for technical buyers. The salary range is one hundred twenty to one hundred fifty thousand dollars. We want candidates who care about craft, not just metrics. Please write it in a warm but professional tone, about four hundred words, with sections for the role, the team, the qualifications, and the company. Avoid corporate jargon like 'rockstar' or 'ninja.'".

The first produces a generic draft you'll have to rewrite. The second produces a draft that needs minor edits.

The basic asymmetry: AI is fast and capable but generic. You are slow but specific to your situation. The combination beats either alone — but only if you do your part by specifying.

Section. Rule 2: Treat it as a conversation, not a search.

Search engines are transactional. You ask, they answer, you move on. AI assistants are conversational. You ask, they answer, you react, you refine, you push back, you ask a follow-up. The first answer is rarely the answer you want. The fifth answer often is.

"Make it shorter." "Less formal." "Emphasize the technical aspects more." "What would a skeptical reader push back on here?" "Help me find the one line that is actually the point."

The mental model: you are working with a junior collaborator who has read enormously, types fast, has no ego, and needs your direction to produce something good. Direct them. Use them. Push them.

Section. Rule 3: Verify what matters.

The discipline from chapter twelve, restated here because it is essential to using AI well and easy to forget.

For anything that matters, cross-check the AI's claims against a source you trust. Especially important for:

Specific people and what they said (the AI may attribute quotes incorrectly or invent them).
Specific numbers and statistics (the AI has good range intuition, unreliable precision).
Legal, medical, financial claims (stakes too high to rely on AI alone — verify with a professional or primary source).
Citations, references, links (the AI sometimes invents these — check they exist before publishing).
Recent events (the AI may have stale information — ask it to search the web or check current sources yourself).

Verification feels like extra work. It is. The alternative is to eventually publish something embarrassingly wrong.

Section. Rule 4: Know which model to use for which job.

By 2026 there are many assistants with different strengths.

Long-form writing, careful reasoning, thoughtful collaboration: Claude tends to be strong.
Broad capability across a wide range: ChatGPT remains the most general-purpose option.
Coding: both Claude and ChatGPT have specialized variants worth using.
Research requiring up-to-date sources with citations: Perplexity often beats the generalist chatbots.
Analysis of long documents: large-context models (Claude, Gemini) are the right choice.
Data privacy / confidential information: consumer chatbots may not be the right choice — look at services with explicit no-retention policies, or models running locally on your device.
Compliance-heavy contexts (healthcare, finance): enterprise versions or specialized vendors with appropriate safeguards.

You don't need to be an expert in every tool. You need to know that picking the right tool matters more than most users realize. If frustrated with one tool, try another for the same task.

Section. Rule 5: Use it for thinking, not just output.

Most users start by using AI to produce things — emails, documents, code, summaries. The output is the goal. Experienced users discover that the more valuable use is often the thinking, not the output. AI is a good externalization tool. It responds to whatever you put in front of it. It can adopt different perspectives. It can ask you questions. It can challenge your assumptions.

Examples:

Deciding whether to take a job offer: describe the offer, the current situation, what you're torn about; ask AI to articulate trade-offs; ask what a skeptic would say; ask what a champion would say; ask what questions you haven't asked yourself. By the end of an hour, you'll probably know what you want, not because the AI told you but because the conversation made you say it.
Stuck on a hard work problem: describe the problem, what you've tried, what isn't working; ask AI to help reframe; ask what people in adjacent fields might do; ask AI to challenge your assumptions about what the problem even is.
Processing something emotionally difficult: with caveats from chapter thirteen, AI can be a useful thinking partner — not a replacement for friends or therapists, but a way to articulate, hear it reflected, find your own clarity.

The AI is not telling you the answer. It is helping you find your own through a conversation you control.

Section. Rule 6: Be honest about what you are delegating.

There is a difference between using AI to draft an email and signing it, versus claiming you personally crafted every word. There is a difference between using AI to brainstorm strategy and presenting it as strategic insight, versus pretending you came up with the ideas without help.

The line moves by context. The general principle that has worked: do not claim more credit than you deserve. Do not pretend to skills you do not have. Do not let AI output substitute for your own thinking when your thinking is what was supposed to be on offer. If you would feel uncomfortable having your AI use revealed, that's a sign you have crossed a line.

Most uses are in the comfortable zone — drafting routine emails, summarizing documents, producing first drafts, generating ideas. These do not require disclosure in most contexts. Just do not lose track of the difference between you and the tool.

Section. Rule 7: Never stop developing your own abilities.

The hardest rule, and the most important. When AI is good at something, the temptation is to stop developing your own ability in that area. The author's view: AI is a tool, and tools work better in skilled hands.

The writer who can write well, using AI to write faster, produces better writing than the writer who can't write at all. The coder who understands software, using AI to code faster, builds better software than the coder entirely dependent on AI for complexity they can't reason about.

The skills of writing, coding, analysis are valuable even if you never need to deploy them without AI. The AI amplifies what you bring. If you bring nothing, the amplification is small.

The good users the author knows have all internalized some version: use AI heavily, also continue to practice their craft. Write things by hand sometimes. Work through problems on paper before asking. Read books, talk to humans, think their own thoughts, and let AI be one tool in a larger life.

Section. Practical habits high-quality users tend to have.

Save your good prompts. When a formulation produces consistently good results, save it. Many users keep a personal library.
Separate conversations for separate topics. Don't use one long thread for everything. Start a new conversation when the topic changes — keeps the AI focused.
Ask for structure before content. For long work, ask for an outline first, iterate on the outline, then have it write sections. Much better long-form output than asking for the whole thing at once.
Use the AI to review its own work. After it produces something, ask it to critique its own output. What is weakest? What is missing? What would a tough reviewer push back on?
Set up custom instructions. Most major assistants let you set persistent preferences — tell it how you write, what you care about, what tone. Defaults are generic; customizations make every conversation better.
Learn at least one keyboard shortcut. Friction between your work and the AI is real. Make the transition seamless.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 18 in one paragraph.

Seven rules for using AI well: tell it what you're trying to do (with full context); treat it as conversation, not search; verify what matters (people, numbers, legal/medical/financial, citations, recent events); know which model for which job (Claude for thinking, ChatGPT for breadth, Perplexity for cited research, Claude/Gemini for long context, local for privacy); use it for thinking, not just output (the highest use); be honest about what you are delegating; never stop developing your own abilities. Habits: save good prompts, separate conversations by topic, ask for structure before content, have AI critique its own work, set custom instructions, learn a keyboard shortcut. The basic frame: human brings judgment and verification, AI brings scale and speed.

Chapter 19. How to Handle AI at Work.

Section. The frame.

Six framings the author uses to think about AI in professional life, plus concrete advice for common scenarios.

Section. Framing 1: Tasks, not jobs.

The conversation about AI and work is usually conducted in the wrong unit. People ask whether AI will replace their job. The question is too coarse.

AI does not replace jobs. AI replaces tasks. Most jobs are dozens of tasks. AI is better at some and worse at others.

A lawyer's job includes reading documents, drafting briefs, researching case law, talking to clients, negotiating with opposing counsel, appearing in court, building relationships with judges and other lawyers, managing junior staff, running a business. AI is genuinely good at document review, research, first drafts of briefs. AI is bad at client relationships, courtroom presence, judgment about strategy under uncertainty. The job isn't going away. The mix of tasks within it is shifting.

The implication: think about your job at the level of tasks. Which are AI good at now? Which is AI bad at? Which is AI rapidly improving at? Which is AI unlikely to improve at soon? For the tasks AI is good at, use AI. For the tasks AI is bad at, double down — they are going up in value.

The exercise isn't always pleasant. Sometimes you discover a lot of what used to make you feel valuable is now done quickly by software. The exercise is still worth doing because the alternative is being surprised.

Section. Framing 2: Complement vs substitute.

For any task you do, ask: is AI a complement to my skill, or a substitute for it?

Complement. Your skill plus AI produces more than either alone. The AI makes you faster, broader, more accurate. You still need to be skilled, and your skill is more valuable when paired.
Substitute. AI does the task and you are not needed. The skill has been commoditized.

The complement tasks are where to invest — you and the AI both get better, and the compounding pulls you ahead of non-AI users. The substitute tasks are where to stop relying on the skill being central to your career.

Hard part: the distinction shifts over time. A task complementary today may become substitutable in two years. The mistake is assuming the line is fixed.

Section. Framing 3: Judgment as the stable competency.

If the author had to bet on one general skill that will hold its value through the AI transition, it would be judgment — the ability to look at a complicated situation, weigh relevant factors, and decide what to do.

The good lawyer judges which arguments will land with a particular judge. The good doctor judges when to escalate from watchful waiting to intervention. The good manager judges when to push a struggling employee versus back off. The good entrepreneur judges which markets to enter and when. The good editor judges which stories matter.

Judgment is hard for AI because it depends on embodied experience, cultural context, tacit knowledge from years in a specific environment, the ability to weigh consequences in domains where the AI has only read about consequences (chapter thirteen).

AI is a useful input to judgment — gathering information, identifying options, laying out trade-offs, stress-testing reasoning. AI cannot be the judgment itself in any context that matters.

For long-term value: keep developing judgment. Take on assignments that require it. Reflect on decisions you make. Notice when you got something right and when you got it wrong.

Section. Framing 4: Relationships as the other stable competency.

The other professional asset that holds its value through automation is your network. Most consequential professional outcomes happen through humans, not through AI. The new job comes through someone who knows you and recommends you. The new client comes through someone who trusts you. The deal closes because the human on the other side believes in you. The hard problem gets solved because someone with the relevant expertise picks up the phone for you.

AI cannot replace this layer. It can supplement it — drafting the email that opens a relationship, preparing for the meeting that deepens one. The relationship itself is between humans, built on trust and shared history AI cannot manufacture.

For long-term resilience: maintain relationships, build new ones, show up for people, make introductions, help colleagues with their problems, be the kind of professional other professionals want to work with.

Section. Framing 5: The early career problem.

The hardest version of the AI-at-work question. The traditional model: start as a junior, do routine work, develop the judgment that lets you become a senior, the senior judgment becomes the value for the rest of your career.

The problem now: the routine junior work is exactly what AI is best at automating. The exposure that used to come from doing that work is happening at the AI level, not the human level. Companies that used to hire ten junior analysts now hire two to supervise AI. The pipeline is narrower.

This is not the early-career worker's fault and is not fair. The ladder older workers used is being partly removed.

The author's advice:

Take any opportunity to do real work, even if AI could do it faster. The experience is the point; the output is secondary.
Seek out the parts AI cannot do — client conversations, judgment calls, conflict resolution, strategy meetings, the "what do we actually do about this" moments.
Find mentors. Senior people have experience you cannot yet have. Their advice, introductions, and feedback are higher-leverage than they have ever been. The senior generation is, in some sense, the last cohort that fully developed in the pre-AI environment.
Take AI seriously as a development tool. Use it as a tutor of remarkable patience. Read what your senior colleagues read. Build the context they assume you have.

Section. Framing 6: The official policy and the real policy.

In most workplaces in 2026 there's an official AI policy and a real one. They're not the same.

The official policy: "Use AI responsibly within these guidelines." The real policy: what your manager actually rewards. Some managers reward employees who use AI heavily and produce more. Others reward employees who do things "the right way" without AI shortcuts. Most managers haven't actually thought through their preferences and react inconsistently.

Specific judgment calls:

Do you tell your manager when you used AI? Sometimes yes, sometimes no. In environments where AI use is celebrated, mention it. In environments where it's suspect, don't volunteer it. Read the room.
Do you put confidential information into AI? Almost certainly not, unless your company has a specific enterprise deployment with appropriate protection. Consumer chatbots are not safe for confidential info; a leak could be career-ending.
Do you advocate for AI in your team? Depends on your seniority. As a junior, advocacy can read as overconfidence. As a senior, advocacy is often part of your job.
Do you compete with AI-heavy colleagues? Generally no. Use AI yourself. The competition is between AI-augmented professionals, not between AI users and non-users. The non-users will quietly fall behind regardless.

Section. Specific scenarios.

If you are a manager. Your job is changing — you can do more individual-contributor work yourself with AI, which is tempting but probably wrong. Your value is leverage, not doing. Your direct reports are also changing — some use AI heavily, some don't. Figure out how to evaluate output when the path from problem to solution is increasingly opaque. Build trust based on outcomes, not imagined effort.
If you are a creative professional (writers, designers, musicians, video editors). The technology is improving very fast in your field. Lean into the tasks requiring irreducibly human judgment, taste, originality, and craft. Use AI aggressively for production work that used to consume your time.
If you are in a regulated profession (law, medicine, finance, accounting). Industry has rules about AI use that are evolving rapidly. Stay current. Most regulated professions have decided AI is a tool, not a substitute for human judgment, and the responsible practitioner remains responsible regardless of which tools they used.
If you are early in your career. See Framing 5. The summary: take any real work, seek out the AI-can't-do parts, find mentors, use AI as a tutor.
If you are late in your career. You probably have the experience and relationships most valuable in the AI era. The thing to add is fluency with the new tools — not expert level, but knowing what they can do and being able to direct them. Few months of focused practice gets you there. Senior judgment plus basic AI fluency is, in 2026, one of the most valuable professional profiles.
If you are unemployed or in transition. AI literacy is a real differentiator in hiring. Use AI in your job search — research companies, draft applications, practice interviews. Be ready to talk about how you would use AI in the role you're applying for.

Section. The bigger point.

The risk is not just becoming obsolete. The risk is also becoming hollow. The worker who outsources too much of their thinking to AI eventually loses the ability to think for themselves. The job survives. The person inside has become less.

The better path: use AI heavily for the tasks where it is genuinely a complement. Continue developing your own abilities in the tasks where you and AI both matter. Invest in the judgment and relationships that compound over decades. Stay engaged with your work, not just productive at it.

The workers the author knows who seem to be navigating this well share a quality: they do not see themselves as in a race with AI. They see themselves as professionals who happen to use a new and powerful tool. The tool is impressive. They are also impressive. The combination is more impressive than the tool alone.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 19 in one paragraph.

Six framings: think about tasks not jobs (your job is a mix of AI-good and AI-bad tasks); complement vs substitute (invest in complement-tasks, stop relying on substitute-tasks); judgment is the stable competency (AI cannot replace it); relationships are the other stable competency (deals, jobs, hard-problem solving still happen through humans); the early-career problem is real (junior ladder is partly broken — seek real work, find mentors, use AI as tutor); the official policy and the real policy are different (read the room about disclosure, never put confidential data into consumer chatbots). Becoming hollow is the risk as much as becoming obsolete — use AI to do more, not to be less.

Chapter 20. How to Talk to Your Kids About Them.

Section. The author's stance.

The hardest chapter in the book, in the author's view. Consequences fall on someone you love who cannot make these decisions for themselves and who will live in this new world long after you and I are gone. Nobody has certain answers. Parents who pretend to are oversimplifying or selling something. The framework that follows has worked for thoughtful parents, combined with what's known from earlier technology transitions in parenting and what early research is showing.

Seven principles.

Section. Principle 1: Age matters a lot.

Under six or seven. Mostly about exposure and modeling. Children at this age are not really using AI directly. The question is what they see you do with AI, what they hear you say about it, and how you treat the world they're growing up in. Children watch what adults do more than they listen to what adults say.
Roughly seven to eleven (elementary). Boundaries and basic understanding. Children encounter AI in education and entertainment. They probably do not need to use AI directly for schoolwork at this age — they are developing the basic skills AI use will later augment. They need to understand, at a basic level, what AI is, that it can be wrong, that it is not a person, and that some classmates may use it in ways they should not.
Roughly eleven to fourteen (middle school). Where peer use, school policy, and family rules collide. Some kids will use AI heavily, with or without parental knowledge. Schools will have policies that are behind reality. Friends will share workarounds. The conversation shifts from whether they will encounter AI to how to use it well, how to use it honestly, and how to maintain the skills AI will not replace.
Roughly fourteen to eighteen (high school). Preparation for adult life. They are about to enter a world where AI is everywhere. Skills they develop now will shape what they can do for decades. The relationship with AI they form now will, in many cases, become the default for their adult life. Parental guidance matters most and is hardest to maintain, because they are also developing the autonomy that should let them figure these things out themselves.

The same family will need different approaches for different children at different ages.

Section. Principle 2: Protect the development of foundational skills.

The author's hard line for younger children: reading, writing, arithmetic, basic reasoning, the ability to focus on a difficult problem for extended periods, the ability to think clearly. These are not just academic subjects. They are the cognitive substrate that makes everything else possible.

A child who has not developed these skills, who relies on AI from the beginning, is in serious trouble. The reason: AI amplifies whatever you bring to it. A child who has developed strong reading skills can use AI to read faster, deeper, more critically. A child who has not cannot — they become dependent on AI in a way that limits them.

The research is increasingly clear. Students who use AI heavily before developing the underlying skills show measurable decreases in independent capabilities. Students who use AI after developing those skills show increases in effective capability without losing the underlying skills.

The practical implication: protect the period of skill development. For elementary and most of middle school, the primary work is building the cognitive foundation. AI can be a supplement (carefully used) for things like tutoring or exploring topics of interest. AI should not be doing the work the child is supposed to be doing. The struggle is the point. The mistakes are the point. The slow building up of competence is the point.

This is harder to enforce than it sounds. Your child's homework may be designed assuming AI does some of the work. Classmates use AI. Devices offer AI features. You will need to hold the line in spite of the environment.

The good news: kids who build the foundation will be enormously advantaged later. The kid who learned to write before AI is now able to write better with AI than peers who never learned. The kid who learned math will reason quantitatively in ways AI-dependent peers cannot. The kid who learned to think for themselves will be able to do something genuinely creative with AI rather than commission generic output.

Section. Principle 3: Teach them to question.

The single most important habit for life in an AI-saturated world: questioning what they are told.

By default, AI produces confident-sounding output. Children, like adults, tend to accept confident-sounding output without questioning it. Dangerous when the confident output is sometimes wrong, sometimes biased, sometimes manipulated, sometimes simply made up.

Practical ways to install the habit:

When your child shows you something the AI told them, ask: "How do you know that is true?" Make the question routine, not accusatory.
When AI tells them something that seems plausible, ask them to find a second source. Build the habit that one source is never enough.
When they encounter AI-generated content in the wild, talk about it. Why was this made? Who benefits if you believe it? What might be missing?
When AI gets something wrong, make a small celebration. "Look, even the AI got that one wrong. Smart of you to check." The framing matters — you're teaching them to be a person who checks.

The habit, once installed, is a gift. Kids who grow up questioning AI output will be more resilient to manipulation, more careful in thinking, more able to participate as adults in a world full of automated persuasion.

Section. Principle 4: AI is not a friend.

The category of AI product that worries the author most for children: AI companions. Chatbots designed to provide emotional support, friendship, romantic interaction. The products are growing fast. Many are explicitly designed to be used by children and teenagers.

The 2026 International AI Safety Report flagged AI companions as an emerging concern. Research is starting to document psychological effects of extended interactions on developing minds. The patterns are concerning. Some children develop dependencies interfering with developing human relationships. Some come to prefer the AI's frictionless attention to the real demands of friendship. Some begin losing social skills because they're practicing on a system that doesn't push back.

The picture isn't all negative. Some children with social anxieties or developmental differences may find AI companions helpful as low-stakes practice. Research is too new to draw firm conclusions.

What the author says with more confidence: AI companions are not a substitute for human relationships, and children should not be allowed to think they are. The AI does not actually care about your child. It cannot. It is generating responses calibrated to maximize engagement, which is not the same as friendship. Real friends sometimes have to say hard things. Real friends are sometimes unavailable. Real friends have their own needs that compete with yours. These frictions are not bugs. They are the features of human relationships that make them valuable and that develop social capacity.

For children: parents should be aware of how much AI companion use is happening, talk about it openly, and ensure human relationships remain the primary social context of the child's life. The AI can be one thing in the mix. It cannot be the center.

Section. Principle 5: Education is going to change.

The educational system your child attends was, in most cases, designed for a pre-AI world. It is now adapting, unevenly. Schools are trying. Some do well. Most figure it out as they go. Official policies are usually behind reality. Teachers are often more confused than students. Curriculum is rarely updated.

Your role is to supplement what the school is doing in two specific ways:

Engage with what your child is actually being asked to do. The line between using AI as a study tool and using AI to do the assignment is being drawn differently in different classrooms — sometimes within the same school. Your child needs help figuring out where the line is in each context, and what their own integrity requires. This is parenting, not a school issue.
Think about what your child needs to know that the school may not be teaching well. The skills of using AI well (chapter eighteen). The skills of evaluating AI output critically. The skills of doing serious work AI cannot do. The skills of human relationship and judgment in a world full of automation. These may need to come from you.

For families who can afford it, supplemental resources are available — tutors, programs, camps, workshops, online courses. For families who cannot, the parent's role as educator becomes more important, not less. Conversations at the dinner table, projects you do together at home, books you read together may matter more in this generation than any recent one.

Section. Principle 6: Talk about work and the future honestly.

Your child will ask whether AI will take their future job. Be honest.

The honest answer: nobody knows what the work of the future will look like. The uncertainty adults face about their own jobs applies, with more force, to children whose careers are decades away.

What you can tell them with some confidence:

Many specific careers that exist today will look different by the time they're working. Some will be gone. New ones will exist we cannot yet name. This is not specific to AI. It has been true of every technological transition.
Skills that hold their value: the ability to think clearly, communicate effectively, work well with other humans, keep learning, use new tools as they emerge, maintain judgment under uncertainty.
The specific knowledge they'll need is impossible to predict. The reliable meta-skill is being someone who can learn new things, adapt to new situations, and build new capabilities as needed.
Their grandparents lived through the rise of computers. Great-grandparents through automation in manufacturing. Great-great-grandparents through electricity. Each transition was scary in real time and produced both winners and losers. The people who did well were not the ones who refused to engage and not the ones who lost themselves in it. They were the ones who learned the new tools while staying grounded in older skills that made them valuable.

The message: the world is changing. They will be fine if they invest in themselves. They are part of a long line of humans who have lived through technological transitions and come out the other side.

Section. Principle 7: Watch for the mental health signals.

Children and teenagers using AI sometimes show specific patterns worth watching for:

Increased isolation, where AI use displaces human contact rather than supplementing it.
Decreasing ability to handle frustration, because AI provides frictionless responses and real life does not.
Changes in writing, speaking, or thinking style suggesting the AI's voice is replacing their own.
Anxiety about being seen as not using AI properly, or using it too much.
Romantic or emotional attachment to AI characters competing with attachment to humans.
Loss of interest in activities they used to enjoy, replaced by extended AI interaction.
Inability to do schoolwork or other tasks without AI in domains where they should be developing independent capacity.

None alone is definitive. Any can have other causes. The pattern matters. If your child shows several signs together, AI use may be a factor worth examining.

The intervention is not abstinence — the technology is too embedded. The intervention is balance, conversation, and ensuring the rest of their life is rich enough that AI doesn't have to be the center.

If you find yourself out of your depth, get help. Therapists are starting to develop expertise in adolescent AI use. Schools are starting to have counselors trained on these issues.

Section. The fundamental work that does not change.

For all the changes AI is bringing to childhood, the fundamental work of parenting is the same as always. Teach your children to be honest. To be kind. To do hard things. To recognize when they're being lied to. To maintain their own integrity under pressure. To love and be loved.

The technology changes the surface of the world they're growing up in. It does not change the underlying work of raising humans who can live good lives.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 20 in one paragraph.

Seven principles for parenting through AI: age matters (different approaches under seven, seven to eleven, eleven to fourteen, fourteen to eighteen); protect foundational skills (reading, writing, math, focus, thinking — the substrate AI later amplifies); teach questioning ("How do you know that's true?"); AI is not a friend (AI companions flagged by 2026 International AI Safety Report — real risk of replacing human relationship-building); education is changing and you supplement what schools miss; talk about work and the future honestly (meta-skill of adaptability beats specific knowledge); watch for mental health signals (isolation, frustration intolerance, voice replacement, attachment, lost interests, dependency). The underlying parenting work — honesty, kindness, doing hard things — does not change.

Chapter 21. Where This Is All Going.

Section. The author's honest starting point.

Prediction in AI is genuinely hard. Almost everyone confident about the long-term trajectory is overconfident.

People who said in 2020 that AI would never produce convincing text were wrong.
People who said in 2022 it would never master coding were wrong.
People who said in 2024 that scaling had hit a wall were wrong.
People who say today with certainty that AGI is five years away, or fifty years away, are probably also wrong. Some will be right by accident.

What the author offers: a framework. The main forces. The main uncertainties. The questions that will determine which version of the future we get. And a way to live in this world that does not depend on being right about it.

Section. Three possible trajectories.

Not mutually exclusive. The actual future will probably blend elements of all three. Naming them separately clarifies what we're uncertain about.

Trajectory 1: Continued growth at the current pace.

Models keep getting better. Applications keep expanding. Economic effects keep accumulating. In ten years, AI is to most knowledge work what computers became to most knowledge work over the past thirty — a massive productivity layer that touches everything, embedded so deeply most users do not think about it. The world looks different from today, but recognizable. Jobs change. Some industries are reshaped. Most people still have work, in modified forms. Real winners and losers, real social costs, real opportunities. Life continues.

Trajectory 2: Acceleration.

The systems get qualitatively more powerful, not just incrementally better. AGI, however you define it, arrives. Economic effects are transformative. Significant fractions of cognitive work become genuinely automatable. The transition is much faster than the institutions of work, education, and policy can keep up with. The next decade looks more like fundamental reordering than evolution. Winners and losers far more extreme. New questions about what humans are for.

Trajectory 3: Plateau.

Current scaling approaches hit limits. Capabilities continue to improve incrementally for some time, then largely level off without major new ideas. The world adjusts to roughly the current frontier over five or ten years. The transformation is large but bounded. AI is a powerful new tool that reshaped some things (search, software development) more than others. The hype of the late 2020s looks, in retrospect, like the dotcom bubble — real, important, but not the singularity.

The three trajectories have very different implications. Trajectory 1 suggests continuity with adaptation. Trajectory 2 suggests preparing for radical change. Trajectory 3 suggests not over-committing to AI-centric assumptions.

Honest reading of current evidence does not let the author pick decisively. Serious experts disagree. The technology itself sometimes seems to be in one trajectory and sometimes in another. The next few years of evidence will reveal which we are actually in.

Section. What we can be confident about.

A few things the author will commit to even given the uncertainty:

The current generation of models is not the last. Pace of improvement, while uneven, has not stopped. Labs are spending massive amounts on next-generation training runs. The next significant capability jumps are coming. Some are probably arriving in 2026 itself.
Economic effects will continue to compound. We are still in the early adoption phase for most industries. Even if AI capabilities stopped improving today, deployment of current capabilities into existing workflows would continue producing changes for years. Most office workers still do not use AI heavily in daily work. Most companies still do not have mature AI strategies. Diffusion is the dominant story for the next several years, even more than capability improvements.
The geopolitical contest will intensify. US and China are not stepping back from this competition. The EU is not giving up on regulating it. The next several years will see more, not less, government action on AI — some productive, some destructive, most confused.
The real risks from chapter fourteen will continue. Deepfakes get better and more common. Bias issues do not vanish. Job displacement continues to be uneven and concentrated on entry-level white-collar work. Privacy concerns get more pressing.
The speculative risks from chapter fifteen may or may not arrive on the timelines their advocates predict. But underlying research is real. Serious progress will continue on alignment, interpretability, and the technical questions that matter for safe deployment of more capable systems.
The companies will continue to be a major force shaping outcomes. Their decisions about what to build, deploy, refuse, how to compete, how to cooperate will matter as much as government regulation in most cases.

Section. Four questions that will determine the trajectory.

Beneath the surface of the AI debate, four questions whose answers will largely determine which trajectory we end up in.

1. Does the current scaling approach continue to produce qualitatively new capabilities, or hit diminishing returns? Cannot tell yet. Models in 2026 are better than 2024, but rate of improvement and nature of new capabilities are debated. The next two or three model generations will tell us a lot. Continued surprises → trajectory two. Diminishing returns → one or three.
2. Does the alignment problem turn out to be tractable? The hope is that with enough research we can build systems that reliably do what we want. The fear is that as systems get more capable, we lose ability to verify behavior in advance. Significant resources are flowing into this. Whether they're sufficient, and whether the problem is solvable at all, determines how much speculative risk is actually risk.
3. Can democratic institutions adapt fast enough? Most rules being made now were designed for a slower technology. Democratic processes by nature take time. Companies move in months. Technology moves in months. Regulators move in years. The longer the gap persists, the more the de facto rules are set by private companies rather than public institutions. Whether democracies can make consequential decisions on technology timescales is one of the central political questions of our era.
4. Do we, the users, develop the wisdom to use these tools well? Not just at the individual level (earlier chapters), but at the cultural level. Whether we become a species that uses AI to enhance our humanity or one that lets AI substitute for it is a choice being made one decision at a time, billions of decisions a day. Nobody is in charge. We are all making it together, with very limited coordination.

The four are not just technical. They are about science, institutions, culture, and character. The answers are not determined. They will be determined by what we do.

Section. What to do about a future you cannot predict.

The author's suggestions, ranging from the immediate to the long-term:

Stay current with the technology. Not expert-level, but at the level of someone who knows what these tools can do. Try new things when they come out. Be the person in your circle who can answer questions about how this stuff works. The asymmetry between AI-fluent and AI-illiterate adults is real and growing.
Maintain your skills. The temptation to outsource thinking to AI is growing. Resist it where it matters. Keep reading books that are not summarized for you. Keep writing in your own voice. Keep doing math in your head when you can. Skills you keep through the AI transition are skills you can use to direct AI when AI is the right tool. Skills you lose to AI are gone.
Invest in relationships. Whatever shape the future takes, the value of trustworthy human relationships will not decline. They may increase. The relationships you build over the next ten years are the people who introduce you to opportunities, vouch for you when you need it, and help you make sense of changes too fast to process alone.
Develop your judgment. The skill you practice every time you make a decision under uncertainty is the skill that holds value through every trajectory. People who look at confusing situations and decide well will be valuable in every version of the future. People who can only execute well-defined tasks will be in trouble in most versions.
Stay engaged with civic life. The rules being written now will shape what AI is allowed to do for the rest of our lives. Most rules are being written by people who do not understand the technology, advised by people with specific interests, debated where most citizens are not paying attention. Vote. Read. Talk to representatives. Show up for public consultations. The future is not just going to be done to you.
Resist the binary thinking. The temptation under uncertainty is to pick a side: either AI is savior or destroyer; either optimists or doomers are right; either embrace fully or reject entirely. Almost every binary in the AI debate is too simple. The truth is somewhere in the middle, with real costs and real benefits to weigh continuously. People who do well are the ones who can hold complexity without collapsing it into a story.

Section. The author's personal note.

Writing in 2026. The technology will be different in 2027. By the time you read this, some of what was said may be wrong. Examples will have aged. Numbers will have changed. Companies will have evolved. Regulations will have moved on. That is the nature of writing about technology that is moving as fast as this one.

What the author hopes will not have aged is the framework — the principles, the way of thinking about this technology that the book tried to model. The recognition that AI is impressive and important without being magical, that it has real risks and real benefits, that it should be neither worshipped nor demonized.

You, the reader, are not a passive recipient of whatever this technology becomes. You have agency. You can use the tools wisely. You can keep developing your own abilities. You can advocate for the rules you want to live under. You can raise your children to be the kind of adults who can handle this new world.

The future is uncertain. Your response to it does not have to be.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 21 in one paragraph.

Three trajectories: continued growth (AI like computers were over thirty years — recognizable world); acceleration (AGI arrives, fundamental reordering); plateau (current scaling hits limits, the late 2020s look like dotcom in retrospect). What's confident regardless: more model generations are coming, economic diffusion is the dominant story for years even if capability stops improving, geopolitical contest intensifies, real risks persist, companies remain major force-shapers. Four questions decide the trajectory: does scaling keep producing new capabilities; is the alignment problem tractable; can democracies adapt on technology timescales; do users develop cultural wisdom about the tools. What to do: stay current, maintain your skills, invest in relationships, develop judgment, stay engaged civically, resist binary thinking. The future is uncertain; the response does not have to be.

Appendix A. Glossary.

Agentic AI. AI that can plan, take actions, and execute multi-step tasks autonomously rather than just answering questions. The 2025–2026 frontier has shifted from chatbots to agents. Claude Code, ChatGPT operators, and several Chinese systems are early examples.

AGI (Artificial General Intelligence). The hypothetical capability of AI to perform any cognitive task a human can, across all domains. Different researchers define the threshold differently. Some think AGI is imminent. Some think it is decades away. Some think the concept is poorly defined.

Alignment. The technical problem of getting AI systems to do what their designers intend, especially as systems become more capable. Major labs invest in alignment research because they believe current techniques may not be sufficient for more powerful systems.

Attention. The mechanism inside a Transformer that lets the model weigh which parts of the input matter most for understanding any given word. Introduced in the 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need." The core trick of modern language models.

Bias. The pattern by which AI systems can produce outputs that systematically disadvantage certain groups, usually because the training data reflected historical inequities. Real and documented, though one labs work continuously to mitigate.

ChatGPT. The consumer chatbot from OpenAI, launched November 30, 2022. The product that brought AI assistants into mainstream awareness. Reached 100 million users in two months.

Claude. The consumer chatbot from Anthropic. Launched in 2023. Known for being relatively careful, willing to push back on the user, and trained using Constitutional AI principles.

Constitutional AI. A technique developed by Anthropic for aligning AI systems by giving them a written set of principles to follow. The model critiques and revises its own outputs against the principles.

Context window. The amount of text a model can consider at once. The first GPT models had context windows of a few thousand tokens. By 2026, frontier models often have context windows of one to two million tokens — enough to process entire books.

Deepfake. AI-generated audio, video, or images designed to look or sound like a real person. The technology crossed a quality threshold in 2025–2026 that makes it accessible to anyone with a smartphone.

Embedding. A representation of a word or piece of text as a list of numbers (a vector) that captures its meaning. Words similar in meaning have embeddings close to each other. The basis of how modern AI represents language.

Fine-tuning. Additional training applied to a pre-trained model to specialize it for a particular task or to adjust its behavior. The first training is on broad text; fine-tuning shapes the model toward specific uses.

Foundation model. A large AI model trained on broad data that can be adapted to many specific applications. GPT, Claude, Gemini, and Llama are all foundation models. The term emphasizes that the model is the foundation on which many specific products are built.

Generative AI. AI systems that produce content (text, images, audio, video, code) rather than just classifying or analyzing existing content. The current AI boom is centered on generative AI.

GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer). The family of language models from OpenAI, beginning with GPT-1 in 2018 and continuing through GPT-5 and beyond.

Hallucination. The phenomenon where AI confidently produces information that is not true. Names of cases that do not exist. Statistics that are made up. Citations that lead nowhere.

Interpretability. The research effort to understand what is happening inside an AI model. The model's behavior emerges from billions of parameters; interpretability research tries to make these internal workings legible to humans, which is essential for verifying safety.

Jailbreak. A technique for bypassing the safety constraints of an AI model. By 2026, sophisticated jailbreaks achieve high success rates against most frontier models.

Large Language Model (LLM). A type of AI model trained on large amounts of text, designed to predict the next word in a sequence. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and most modern AI assistants are large language models.

Llama. Meta's family of open-weight language models, released from 2023 through 2025. Versions 1 through 3 made Meta the leader of the open-weight ecosystem. The Llama 4 launch in 2025 was a setback, and Meta pivoted away from open weights with Muse Spark.

Multimodal. A model that can work with multiple types of input or output: text, images, audio, video. Most frontier models in 2026 are multimodal.

Open weight. A model whose weights are publicly available. Anyone can download and run an open-weight model on their own hardware. Contrast with closed-weight models like GPT-5 or Claude.

Parameters. The internal numerical values inside a neural network that get adjusted during training. Modern frontier models have hundreds of billions to trillions of parameters. More parameters generally means more capacity to learn complex patterns.

Prompt. The text you give an AI model as input. The skill of writing prompts well is sometimes called prompt engineering, though the term has become less prominent as models have gotten better at understanding casual requests.

Prompt injection. An attack technique where an attacker embeds instructions inside content the AI model reads, attempting to override the model's intended behavior. As AI gets more agentic, this becomes a more serious security concern.

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation). A technique where an AI model is given access to a specific collection of documents and instructed to base its answers on that collection. Often used to give AI assistants access to specific company knowledge.

Red teaming. The practice of having researchers actively try to break an AI model's safety measures. Discovered problems get patched. A standard part of how frontier AI systems are developed.

RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback). The training technique that shaped raw word predictors into helpful assistants. Human raters evaluate model outputs; ratings train the model to produce outputs humans prefer.

System prompt. A hidden block of instructions prepended to every conversation with an AI assistant. Shapes behavior, tone, and constraints. Most users never see it. Most differences between AI products are in the system prompt rather than the underlying model.

Token. A piece of a word that an AI model treats as a single unit. "Cat" might be one token. "Unbelievable" might be three tokens. The model reads and writes in tokens, not characters or words.

Training data. The text and other content used to teach a model what patterns to recognize and produce. Frontier models are trained on truly enormous amounts of data, often essentially the entire public internet plus licensed datasets.

Transformer. The neural network architecture introduced in the 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need." The basis for virtually all modern AI assistants.

Vector. A list of numbers representing the meaning of a word or piece of text in a high-dimensional space. See Embedding.

Weights. The actual numerical values inside a trained AI model. The weights determine what the model does. An open-weight model shares its weights publicly. A closed-weight model keeps them proprietary.

World model. A type of AI that learns to predict and simulate physical reality rather than just generating text. A frontier research area in 2026, with significant investment from Google DeepMind, Meta, and others. Believed by some researchers to be necessary for true general intelligence.

Appendix B. Timeline.

Section. Pre-history.

1956 — Dartmouth Workshop. The summer workshop where the term "artificial intelligence" was first used. Organized by John McCarthy with Marvin Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester, and Claude Shannon.
1970s — First AI Winter. Funding for AI research collapses across the US and UK after early systems fail to meet expectations.
1980s — Second AI Winter. Another collapse after expert systems fail to scale.
2013 — word2vec. Tomas Mikolov and colleagues at Google demonstrate that word meanings can be captured as vectors. King minus man plus woman equals queen.
2017 — Attention Is All You Need. Eight researchers at Google publish the Transformer paper. The foundation for modern language models.

Section. The GPT years.

2018 — GPT-1. OpenAI releases its first GPT model. 117 million parameters. An academic curiosity.
2019 — GPT-2. OpenAI releases GPT-2. 1.5 billion parameters. Initially withheld as "too dangerous to release." Eventually released in November 2019.
2020 — GPT-3. OpenAI releases GPT-3. 175 billion parameters. The first model that startled even its creators with what it could do. Available only through an API.

Section. The ChatGPT moment.

November 30, 2022 — ChatGPT Launches. OpenAI launches ChatGPT, a chat interface to a fine-tuned GPT-3.5. The fastest-growing consumer product in history at the time. One million users in five days. One hundred million in two months.
2022 to 2023 — The Scramble. Google declares a code red. Anthropic releases Claude. Meta releases Llama as the first major open-weight model. Microsoft deepens its investment in OpenAI.

Section. The frontier years.

March 2023 — GPT-4. OpenAI releases GPT-4, a major capability jump from GPT-3.5. Multimodal capabilities.
March 2023 — Claude. Anthropic releases its first version of Claude.
March 2023 — xAI founded. Elon Musk founds xAI, recruiting eleven researchers.
November 2023 — The OpenAI Board Crisis. Sam Altman is fired by the OpenAI board, then reinstated within four days after employee revolt and Microsoft pressure.
2024 — The Year of Models. Multiple major releases. GPT-4o. Claude 3 family. Gemini 1.5. Llama 3.
March 2024 — Mustafa Suleyman to Microsoft. Microsoft hires most of Inflection's team and licenses the technology, leaving the corporate shell intact but stripped of its people.
August 2024 — EU AI Act. The European Union's AI Act becomes law. Implementation staggered through 2026 and 2027.
2024 — Demis Hassabis Nobel Prize. Hassabis and John Jumper share the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for AlphaFold's work on protein folding.

Section. The DeepSeek moment and beyond.

January 2025 — DeepSeek V3 Release. A Chinese lab releases an open-weight model trained for about six million dollars that performs competitively with American frontier models. Nvidia loses five hundred billion in market cap in a single day.
January 2025 — Trump revokes Biden AI EO. Trump signs Executive Order 14179, orienting federal policy toward removing barriers to AI development rather than safety.
April 2025 — Llama 4 Disaster. Meta releases Llama 4. The release is later found to have used different model versions for different benchmarks, gaming the results. Internal consequences are severe.
May 2025 — Claude Code. Anthropic launches Claude Code, an agentic coding tool. Within months, it generates over one billion dollars in annualized revenue.
Mid-2025 — Meta Superintelligence Labs formed. Zuckerberg pays about fourteen point three billion dollars for forty-nine percent of Scale AI; Alexandr Wang becomes Meta's chief AI officer at twenty-eight years old.
June 2025 — Japan AI Promotion Act. Non-binding framework takes effect.
November 2025 — LeCun Leaves Meta. Yann LeCun departs Meta to found AMI Labs, focused on world models.
December 2025 — Trump State AI EO. Trump signs an executive order directing the AG to challenge state AI laws that conflict with a proposed federal framework.
2025 — Anthropic Catches Up. Anthropic's revenue grows from a few hundred million to roughly thirty billion over the year. The frontier becomes multipolar.

Section. The present moment (2026).

January 1, 2026 — Amended Chinese Cybersecurity Law. Becomes enforceable with explicit AI provisions, security reviews, and data localization for AI training.
January 2026 — Zhipu AI Hong Kong IPO. First major Chinese LLM developer to list publicly.
January 2026 — The 2026 International AI Safety Report. Yoshua Bengio chairs the report, warning about deepfakes, AI companions, and increasing capability.
February 2026 — SpaceX Acquires xAI. The combined entity is valued at about one and a quarter trillion dollars.
March 2026 — Meta Pivots to Closed Weights. Meta launches Muse Spark as its first closed-weight frontier model, stepping back from open-weight commitments.
March 2026 — Anthropic Pentagon Designation. The Pentagon designates Anthropic a supply chain risk after the company refuses to remove restrictions on military uses of Claude. Anthropic is challenging in court.
March 2026 — White House National AI Framework. Released as legislative recommendations to Congress.
April 2026 — DeepSeek V4 Release. DeepSeek releases V4, including a 1.6 trillion parameter variant, trained largely on Huawei Ascend chips. Pricing as low as twenty-eight cents per million output tokens.
April 2026 — Kimi K2.6. Moonshot AI releases a model competitive with Claude and GPT-5 on coding benchmarks.
May 2026 — The xAI / SpaceX Consolidation. Musk announces xAI will cease to exist as a separate company, folded entirely into SpaceX's SpaceXAI division.
May 2026 — Musk Lawsuit Dismissed. After an eleven-day trial in Oakland, a jury finds Musk filed too late and the judge dismisses his suit against OpenAI. Musk has said he will appeal.
May 2026 — EU AI Act Streamlining Agreement. EU institutions reach a provisional agreement to streamline parts of the Act while maintaining substantive obligations.

Section. Scheduled.

August 2026 — EU AI Act High-Risk Provisions. The bulk of the EU AI Act's obligations on high-risk systems scheduled to take effect, with a contested proposal to delay until December 2027.

Appendix C. Further Reading.

Section. If you want to understand how the technology works.

"Attention Is All You Need" (Vaswani and colleagues, 2017). The original Transformer paper. Technical, but the introduction and conclusion are accessible to non-specialists. Worth at least skimming for the historical importance.
"The Illustrated Transformer" by Jay Alammar. The clearest visual explanation of how the Transformer works, suitable for non-technical readers willing to engage. Available free online.
"Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI" by Ethan Mollick (2024). A practical, optimistic guide to using AI tools effectively. Mollick is a Wharton professor who has been a leading voice on AI in education and business.

Section. If you want to understand the companies.

"Empire of AI" by Karen Hao (2025). A deeply reported book on the rise of OpenAI and the broader industry. Hao is one of the journalists who has covered this beat longest and most thoroughly.
"The Maniac" by Benjamin Labatut (2023). A literary book about the foundations of computing and AI, told through the lives of John von Neumann and others. Reads like fiction, illuminates the philosophical roots of where we are now.
"Genius Makers" by Cade Metz (2021). The story of how deep learning happened. Predates the ChatGPT moment but provides essential context for who built modern AI.

Section. If you want to understand the risks.

"The Alignment Problem" by Brian Christian (2020). The clearest treatment of the technical and philosophical problem of building AI systems that do what we actually want them to do. Predates the current boom but the issues have only become more pressing.
"Weapons of Math Destruction" by Cathy O'Neil (2016). On algorithmic bias and its consequences for ordinary people. Pre-AI-boom but essential background on the bias issues from chapter fourteen.
"Atlas of AI" by Kate Crawford (2021). A critical perspective on the physical, environmental, and labor costs of building AI systems. Important counter-weight to the dominant narrative of AI as ethereal software.

Section. If you want to understand AI policy.

The 2026 International AI Safety Report, chaired by Yoshua Bengio. The most comprehensive current overview of AI risks and the state of safety research. Available free online from the UK government.
"AI Snake Oil" by Arvind Narayanan and Sayash Kapoor (2024). A skeptical view of AI hype, focused on distinguishing legitimate AI applications from snake oil. A useful corrective to industry boosterism.
The Stanford AI Index Report. Annual. A comprehensive annual review of where AI capabilities, deployments, and policies stand. The 2026 edition is the most recent. Available free online.

Section. If you want to hear from the people building this.

"Machines of Loving Grace" (Dario Amodei, 2024). An essay by the CEO of Anthropic arguing for an optimistic vision of what AI could do for humanity. Worth reading even if you disagree — it is the clearest statement of the worldview behind one of the leading labs.
"Situational Awareness" (Leopold Aschenbrenner, 2024). An essay laying out the case for transformative AI arriving within this decade. Influential among AI researchers. Argues for what this book called the "second trajectory.".
"AI 2027" (Daniel Kokotajlo and colleagues). A widely-read scenario laying out one specific way AI could develop over the next few years. Speculative but careful.

Section. If you want to think about what AI means for humans.

"The Coming Wave" by Mustafa Suleyman (2023). On AI and synthetic biology as the defining technologies of the century. Suleyman is one of the central figures in the field and writes with insider knowledge.
"Filterworld" by Kyle Chayka (2024). On the effects of algorithmic curation on culture and identity. Not specifically about AI in the language-model sense, but essential background for thinking about how AI shapes the texture of modern life.
"Determined" by Robert Sapolsky (2023). On free will and human agency. Not about AI, but reading it alongside AI material gives you a richer perspective on what we are and what we are not.

Section. If you just want to stay current.

The Marginalian by Maria Popova. Not AI-specific but consistently good thinking about technology and culture.
One Useful Thing newsletter (Ethan Mollick) and Import AI newsletter (Jack Clark). Two of the most reliable sources for staying current with the field.
Press: The AI sections of The New York Times, The Atlantic, The Information, and Bloomberg Technology are generally good. MIT Technology Review covers AI extensively and well.
Research updates: Anthropic's blog, OpenAI's blog, Google DeepMind's blog, and the AI safety section of arxiv are where new technical work appears first.

This is a snapshot from 2026. By the time you are reading this, half of the list may be dated. The good news: the people doing serious thinking about AI tend to keep doing serious thinking, so the authors above are mostly worth following for whatever they write next. Use their work as a starting point and follow your curiosity from there.
