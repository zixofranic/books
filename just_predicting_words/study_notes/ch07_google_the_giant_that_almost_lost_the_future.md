# Chapter 7 — Google, the Giant That Almost Lost the Future

**Version:** v1 · **Last updated:** 2026-05-30 · **Source:** *Just Predicting Words*, Ch7

---

## The paradox

Google invented the architecture that powers the modern AI industry — the Transformer, in 2017, at Google Brain. Every major modern AI assistant is a descendant of that architecture. And yet by the time ChatGPT launched in November 2022, Google was widely seen as being behind. The story of how that happened, and how Google has been trying to catch up since, is the story of this chapter.

## The two AI groups inside Google

For most of the 2010s and into the 2020s, Google had two distinct AI research organizations.

- **Google Brain**, founded inside Google, produced the Transformer paper and much of the foundational work that the rest of the industry built on.
- **DeepMind**, acquired by Google in 2014 for around five hundred million dollars, was founded in London by Demis Hassabis and others. It was famous for AlphaGo (the program that beat the world champion at Go in 2016), AlphaFold (which solved a long-standing problem in protein structure prediction), and a strong research culture independent of Google's main businesses.

The two groups had distinct cultures, distinct strategies, and a real organizational rivalry. After ChatGPT, Google merged them into a single entity called Google DeepMind, run by Demis Hassabis. The merger was completed in 2023.

## The Nobel Prize, 2024

Demis Hassabis shared the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry with John Jumper for AlphaFold's work on protein folding. AlphaFold predicted the three-dimensional structures of essentially all known proteins and made the predictions publicly available — a leap forward for structural biology. The award marked the first Nobel given for work substantially driven by AI.

## The Code Red, late 2022

When ChatGPT launched, Google's leadership reportedly declared an internal "code red." The concern was direct and existential: if conversational AI replaced search as the way most people looked up information, Google's core business — built on selling ads alongside search results — would erode rapidly.

## Bard, then Gemini

Google's first public response was Bard, a ChatGPT competitor that launched in early 2023. Bard had a rocky start, including a high-profile factual error in its initial public demonstration that wiped roughly one hundred billion dollars off Google's market cap in a single day.

In late 2023 Google rebranded the product line as Gemini and folded the underlying model work into a coherent family. Gemini 1.0, 1.5, and successive versions improved rapidly. By 2026, Gemini is one of the strongest frontier model families, with a particular strength in extremely long context windows — the Gemini line was among the first to ship one-million-token and two-million-token context windows, enough to fit entire books or large codebases in a single conversation.

## Distribution as Google's advantage

Google's biggest competitive asset is not its model technology — which is competitive but not always the absolute frontier — but its distribution. AI capabilities are now integrated into Google Search (AI Overviews, then a successor AI mode), Workspace (Gmail, Docs, Meet), Android, Chrome, YouTube, and Maps. Billions of users encounter Google AI dozens of times a day, whether or not they think of themselves as using AI.

## The talent diaspora from Google

A pattern worth knowing: many of the people who built the foundational technology at Google have since left to compete with it. All eight authors of the original Transformer paper had left Google within eight years of publication. Aidan Gomez co-founded Cohere. Several went to OpenAI or Anthropic or started other ventures. The same broad pattern holds across the rest of Google's AI staff. Some of it is the gravitational pull of OpenAI and Anthropic; some of it is the differential between Google's pay scale and what frontier startups can offer.

Google has been working to slow the bleeding by paying large retention packages and giving senior researchers more autonomy inside DeepMind. The success of those efforts is hard to evaluate from outside.

## What Google stands for in AI, 2026

Google's stated philosophy is closer to a careful-deployment position than OpenAI's deploy-aggressively or Anthropic's safety-first. Google emphasizes integration into existing products, regulatory cooperation, and broad scientific contributions like AlphaFold. Critics argue that Google's caution is mostly a function of having a giant existing business to protect; defenders argue that Google has earned the right to its approach through decades of building useful products.

## What to remember about Google in one paragraph

Invented the Transformer in 2017, then was outmaneuvered by smaller companies built on top of it. Owns DeepMind (acquired 2014) and merged Brain into it in 2023. Demis Hassabis won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for AlphaFold. The Gemini model family is now one of the strongest, especially at long context. Distribution — Search, Workspace, Android, Chrome, YouTube — is Google's biggest AI advantage. The original eight Transformer authors had all left Google within eight years.
