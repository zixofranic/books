# Chapter 9 — The Other Half of the Map (China)

**Version:** v1 · **Last updated:** 2026-05-30 · **Source:** *Just Predicting Words*, Ch9

## The DeepSeek moment, January 2025

On a Sunday in late January 2025, a Chinese company called DeepSeek released a chatbot to the public. Within forty-eight hours, the DeepSeek app had reached number one on Apple's App Store in the United States, ahead of ChatGPT. Nvidia lost five hundred billion dollars of market value in a single day. Trading desks across Wall Street called emergency meetings.

DeepSeek had released a model that performed roughly on par with the best American models — but its final training run had reportedly cost about six million dollars. American labs were spending hundreds of millions, sometimes billions, on equivalent training runs. The model was open-weight. The API pricing was a fraction of OpenAI's. The model was, in operational terms, just better at certain kinds of reasoning than what was available from anyone else.

The DeepSeek moment forced the Western AI conversation to acknowledge a fact it had been avoiding: the half of the AI map that Western media had been ignoring was, in many ways, ahead of the half they had been watching.

## Why China caught up faster than expected

Two pieces of context explain the trajectory.

First, China has been investing heavily in AI research for over a decade. By the late 2010s, Chinese universities were producing more AI research papers than American universities, often with citation counts that rivaled the best American work. Chinese AI talent has been at the cutting edge for years.

Second, since October 2022, the United States has imposed sweeping export controls on advanced computer chips. The Nvidia chips that American labs use to train models were forbidden from sale to Chinese companies. The reasoning was that if AI was strategically critical, China should not have it.

The export controls did not work as intended. They forced Chinese labs to innovate in different directions — to write better software, design better architectures, train smarter rather than harder. The result was a generation of Chinese techniques often more efficient than their American equivalents. The constraint produced the breakthrough.

## The main labs

- **DeepSeek.** Owned by High-Flyer, a Chinese hedge fund. Famous for releasing extremely efficient open-weight models at very low prices. Their V4 model, released in April 2026, comes in two versions — including a 1.6 trillion parameter variant that competes with the best American closed-source offerings, at roughly one-tenth the price. DeepSeek V4 Flash costs about twenty-eight cents per million output tokens.
- **Alibaba.** The Chinese e-commerce giant. Their AI division produces the Qwen series of models. As of mid-2026, Qwen has become the most-downloaded open-weight model family in the world, ahead of Meta's Llama. The Qwen app has over one hundred million monthly active users in China and growing markets in Asia and Africa.
- **Moonshot AI.** A Beijing-based startup founded by former Tsinghua University researchers. Produces the Kimi model series. Particularly strong on long-context understanding and on agentic AI. Kimi K2.6, released April 2026, scored competitively with Claude and GPT-5 on coding benchmarks.
- **Zhipu AI.** Another Beijing-based lab. Produces the GLM series. Was China's first large language model developer to list publicly, with a Hong Kong IPO in January 2026.
- **ByteDance.** Parent of TikTok. Has released the Seed and Doubao model series. Distribution advantages through TikTok give them a uniquely powerful platform.
- **Baidu.** China's largest search engine. Their ERNIE chatbot was one of the first major Chinese consumer AI products. Less prominent at the frontier in 2026 than they were in 2023, but still significant.
- **Tencent.** Owner of WeChat. Has integrated AI into its products at enormous scale. Distributed over one hundred forty million dollars in cash bonuses through their AI chatbot during the 2026 Lunar New Year.
- Also: MiniMax, 01.AI, StepFun, and others.

## Architectural innovations

The DeepSeek architecture innovations, particularly something called Multi-Head Latent Attention, are now studied by researchers everywhere. The broad pattern: Chinese labs lead on efficiency, agentic capability, and aggressive pricing.

## The Huawei Ascend hardware question

The Huawei Ascend chips, designed in China to replace Nvidia chips that cannot be imported, have improved significantly. DeepSeek V4 was trained largely on Ascend hardware. The American export controls were supposed to prevent this. The Chinese semiconductor industry has accelerated under the pressure of the controls, and the gap between Chinese and American AI hardware is narrowing.

## The censorship reality

Chinese AI models have hard limits Western models do not. All of them have built-in content restrictions on topics sensitive to the Chinese government. They will not discuss Taiwan's political status the way Western models will. They will not discuss the 1989 events at Tiananmen Square. They will produce content aligned with Chinese government positions rather than providing neutral information.

This is not a quirk. It is a legal requirement. Chinese AI companies operate under regulations requiring their products to "uphold core socialist values" and to refrain from "subverting state power." The censorship is built in, both technically and culturally. Even when models are released open-weight, the censorship is baked into the training data and the alignment process.

## Where Chinese AI is being used most

DeepSeek usage in Africa is, by some estimates, two to four times higher than in other regions — partly because of the price, partly because of open-weight availability, partly because of the lighter regulatory footprint. In much of Africa, Latin America, Southeast Asia, and parts of Europe, Chinese AI may already be a significant part of daily AI consumption.

## The geopolitical frame

The competition is not just commercial rivalry. It is part of a slow technology cold war between two superpowers. Both governments believe whoever leads in AI will have significant strategic advantages. Both use policy tools to advance their domestic AI industries and constrain the other's. The Chinese labs sit inside this larger context as commercial companies and, in various ways, as instruments of national strategy. The same is true, in fairness, of American AI labs.

## What to remember about China's AI in one paragraph

DeepSeek's January 2025 release of a six-million-dollar-trained competitive model caused Nvidia to lose five hundred billion in market cap in a day. Six or seven major labs matter: DeepSeek, Alibaba's Qwen (most-downloaded open weights), Moonshot's Kimi, Zhipu's GLM, ByteDance, Baidu, Tencent. Chinese labs lead on efficiency, open-weight releases, agentic capability, and aggressive pricing. US export controls forced innovation rather than preventing it. Huawei Ascend chips now train frontier models. Content restrictions (Taiwan, Tiananmen, "core socialist values") are legal requirements baked into models. Western businesses generally cannot use Chinese AI for compliance reasons. The multipolar AI future is now structural.
