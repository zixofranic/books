# Chapter 20 — How to Talk to Your Kids About Them

**Version:** v1 · **Last updated:** 2026-05-30 · **Source:** *Just Predicting Words*, Ch20

---

## The author's stance

The hardest chapter in the book, in the author's view. Consequences fall on someone you love who cannot make these decisions for themselves and who will live in this new world long after you and I are gone. Nobody has certain answers. Parents who pretend to are oversimplifying or selling something. The framework that follows has worked for thoughtful parents, combined with what's known from earlier technology transitions in parenting and what early research is showing.

Seven principles.

## Principle 1: Age matters a lot

- **Under six or seven.** Mostly about exposure and modeling. Children at this age are not really using AI directly. The question is what they see you do with AI, what they hear you say about it, and how you treat the world they're growing up in. Children watch what adults do more than they listen to what adults say.
- **Roughly seven to eleven (elementary).** Boundaries and basic understanding. Children encounter AI in education and entertainment. They probably do not need to use AI directly for schoolwork at this age — they are developing the basic skills AI use will later augment. They need to understand, at a basic level, what AI is, that it can be wrong, that it is not a person, and that some classmates may use it in ways they should not.
- **Roughly eleven to fourteen (middle school).** Where peer use, school policy, and family rules collide. Some kids will use AI heavily, with or without parental knowledge. Schools will have policies that are behind reality. Friends will share workarounds. The conversation shifts from whether they will encounter AI to how to use it well, how to use it honestly, and how to maintain the skills AI will not replace.
- **Roughly fourteen to eighteen (high school).** Preparation for adult life. They are about to enter a world where AI is everywhere. Skills they develop now will shape what they can do for decades. The relationship with AI they form now will, in many cases, become the default for their adult life. Parental guidance matters most and is hardest to maintain, because they are also developing the autonomy that should let them figure these things out themselves.

The same family will need different approaches for different children at different ages.

## Principle 2: Protect the development of foundational skills

The author's hard line for younger children: reading, writing, arithmetic, basic reasoning, the ability to focus on a difficult problem for extended periods, the ability to think clearly. These are not just academic subjects. They are the cognitive substrate that makes everything else possible.

A child who has not developed these skills, who relies on AI from the beginning, is in serious trouble. The reason: AI amplifies whatever you bring to it. A child who has developed strong reading skills can use AI to read faster, deeper, more critically. A child who has not cannot — they become dependent on AI in a way that limits them.

The research is increasingly clear. Students who use AI heavily before developing the underlying skills show measurable decreases in independent capabilities. Students who use AI after developing those skills show increases in effective capability without losing the underlying skills.

The practical implication: protect the period of skill development. For elementary and most of middle school, the primary work is building the cognitive foundation. AI can be a supplement (carefully used) for things like tutoring or exploring topics of interest. AI should not be doing the work the child is supposed to be doing. The struggle is the point. The mistakes are the point. The slow building up of competence is the point.

This is harder to enforce than it sounds. Your child's homework may be designed assuming AI does some of the work. Classmates use AI. Devices offer AI features. You will need to hold the line in spite of the environment.

The good news: kids who build the foundation will be enormously advantaged later. The kid who learned to write before AI is now able to write better with AI than peers who never learned. The kid who learned math will reason quantitatively in ways AI-dependent peers cannot. The kid who learned to think for themselves will be able to do something genuinely creative with AI rather than commission generic output.

## Principle 3: Teach them to question

The single most important habit for life in an AI-saturated world: questioning what they are told.

By default, AI produces confident-sounding output. Children, like adults, tend to accept confident-sounding output without questioning it. Dangerous when the confident output is sometimes wrong, sometimes biased, sometimes manipulated, sometimes simply made up.

Practical ways to install the habit:

- When your child shows you something the AI told them, ask: "How do you know that is true?" Make the question routine, not accusatory.
- When AI tells them something that seems plausible, ask them to find a second source. Build the habit that one source is never enough.
- When they encounter AI-generated content in the wild, talk about it. Why was this made? Who benefits if you believe it? What might be missing?
- When AI gets something wrong, make a small celebration. "Look, even the AI got that one wrong. Smart of you to check." The framing matters — you're teaching them to be a person who checks.

The habit, once installed, is a gift. Kids who grow up questioning AI output will be more resilient to manipulation, more careful in thinking, more able to participate as adults in a world full of automated persuasion.

## Principle 4: AI is not a friend

The category of AI product that worries the author most for children: AI companions. Chatbots designed to provide emotional support, friendship, romantic interaction. The products are growing fast. Many are explicitly designed to be used by children and teenagers.

The 2026 International AI Safety Report flagged AI companions as an emerging concern. Research is starting to document psychological effects of extended interactions on developing minds. The patterns are concerning. Some children develop dependencies interfering with developing human relationships. Some come to prefer the AI's frictionless attention to the real demands of friendship. Some begin losing social skills because they're practicing on a system that doesn't push back.

The picture isn't all negative. Some children with social anxieties or developmental differences may find AI companions helpful as low-stakes practice. Research is too new to draw firm conclusions.

What the author says with more confidence: AI companions are not a substitute for human relationships, and children should not be allowed to think they are. The AI does not actually care about your child. It cannot. It is generating responses calibrated to maximize engagement, which is not the same as friendship. Real friends sometimes have to say hard things. Real friends are sometimes unavailable. Real friends have their own needs that compete with yours. These frictions are not bugs. They are the features of human relationships that make them valuable and that develop social capacity.

For children: parents should be aware of how much AI companion use is happening, talk about it openly, and ensure human relationships remain the primary social context of the child's life. The AI can be one thing in the mix. It cannot be the center.

## Principle 5: Education is going to change

The educational system your child attends was, in most cases, designed for a pre-AI world. It is now adapting, unevenly. Schools are trying. Some do well. Most figure it out as they go. Official policies are usually behind reality. Teachers are often more confused than students. Curriculum is rarely updated.

Your role is to supplement what the school is doing in two specific ways:

- Engage with what your child is actually being asked to do. The line between using AI as a study tool and using AI to do the assignment is being drawn differently in different classrooms — sometimes within the same school. Your child needs help figuring out where the line is in each context, and what their own integrity requires. This is parenting, not a school issue.
- Think about what your child needs to know that the school may not be teaching well. The skills of using AI well (chapter eighteen). The skills of evaluating AI output critically. The skills of doing serious work AI cannot do. The skills of human relationship and judgment in a world full of automation. These may need to come from you.

For families who can afford it, supplemental resources are available — tutors, programs, camps, workshops, online courses. For families who cannot, the parent's role as educator becomes more important, not less. Conversations at the dinner table, projects you do together at home, books you read together may matter more in this generation than any recent one.

## Principle 6: Talk about work and the future honestly

Your child will ask whether AI will take their future job. Be honest.

The honest answer: nobody knows what the work of the future will look like. The uncertainty adults face about their own jobs applies, with more force, to children whose careers are decades away.

What you can tell them with some confidence:

- Many specific careers that exist today will look different by the time they're working. Some will be gone. New ones will exist we cannot yet name. This is not specific to AI. It has been true of every technological transition.
- Skills that hold their value: the ability to think clearly, communicate effectively, work well with other humans, keep learning, use new tools as they emerge, maintain judgment under uncertainty.
- The specific knowledge they'll need is impossible to predict. The reliable meta-skill is being someone who can learn new things, adapt to new situations, and build new capabilities as needed.
- Their grandparents lived through the rise of computers. Great-grandparents through automation in manufacturing. Great-great-grandparents through electricity. Each transition was scary in real time and produced both winners and losers. The people who did well were not the ones who refused to engage and not the ones who lost themselves in it. They were the ones who learned the new tools while staying grounded in older skills that made them valuable.

The message: the world is changing. They will be fine if they invest in themselves. They are part of a long line of humans who have lived through technological transitions and come out the other side.

## Principle 7: Watch for the mental health signals

Children and teenagers using AI sometimes show specific patterns worth watching for:

- Increased isolation, where AI use displaces human contact rather than supplementing it.
- Decreasing ability to handle frustration, because AI provides frictionless responses and real life does not.
- Changes in writing, speaking, or thinking style suggesting the AI's voice is replacing their own.
- Anxiety about being seen as not using AI properly, or using it too much.
- Romantic or emotional attachment to AI characters competing with attachment to humans.
- Loss of interest in activities they used to enjoy, replaced by extended AI interaction.
- Inability to do schoolwork or other tasks without AI in domains where they should be developing independent capacity.

None alone is definitive. Any can have other causes. The pattern matters. If your child shows several signs together, AI use may be a factor worth examining.

The intervention is not abstinence — the technology is too embedded. The intervention is balance, conversation, and ensuring the rest of their life is rich enough that AI doesn't have to be the center.

If you find yourself out of your depth, get help. Therapists are starting to develop expertise in adolescent AI use. Schools are starting to have counselors trained on these issues.

## The fundamental work that does not change

For all the changes AI is bringing to childhood, the fundamental work of parenting is the same as always. Teach your children to be honest. To be kind. To do hard things. To recognize when they're being lied to. To maintain their own integrity under pressure. To love and be loved.

The technology changes the surface of the world they're growing up in. It does not change the underlying work of raising humans who can live good lives.

## What to remember about Chapter 20 in one paragraph

Seven principles for parenting through AI: age matters (different approaches under seven, seven to eleven, eleven to fourteen, fourteen to eighteen); protect foundational skills (reading, writing, math, focus, thinking — the substrate AI later amplifies); teach questioning ("How do you know that's true?"); AI is not a friend (AI companions flagged by 2026 International AI Safety Report — real risk of replacing human relationship-building); education is changing and you supplement what schools miss; talk about work and the future honestly (meta-skill of adaptability beats specific knowledge); watch for mental health signals (isolation, frustration intolerance, voice replacement, attachment, lost interests, dependency). The underlying parenting work — honesty, kindness, doing hard things — does not change.
