# Appendix C — Further Reading

**Version:** v1 · **Last updated:** 2026-05-30 · **Source:** *Just Predicting Words*, Appendix C

An opinionated list of sources for going deeper than this book did. Organized by topic.

---

## If you want to understand how the technology works

- **"Attention Is All You Need"** (Vaswani and colleagues, 2017). The original Transformer paper. Technical, but the introduction and conclusion are accessible to non-specialists. Worth at least skimming for the historical importance.
- **"The Illustrated Transformer"** by Jay Alammar. The clearest visual explanation of how the Transformer works, suitable for non-technical readers willing to engage. Available free online.
- **"Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI"** by Ethan Mollick (2024). A practical, optimistic guide to using AI tools effectively. Mollick is a Wharton professor who has been a leading voice on AI in education and business.

## If you want to understand the companies

- **"Empire of AI"** by Karen Hao (2025). A deeply reported book on the rise of OpenAI and the broader industry. Hao is one of the journalists who has covered this beat longest and most thoroughly.
- **"The Maniac"** by Benjamin Labatut (2023). A literary book about the foundations of computing and AI, told through the lives of John von Neumann and others. Reads like fiction, illuminates the philosophical roots of where we are now.
- **"Genius Makers"** by Cade Metz (2021). The story of how deep learning happened. Predates the ChatGPT moment but provides essential context for who built modern AI.

## If you want to understand the risks

- **"The Alignment Problem"** by Brian Christian (2020). The clearest treatment of the technical and philosophical problem of building AI systems that do what we actually want them to do. Predates the current boom but the issues have only become more pressing.
- **"Weapons of Math Destruction"** by Cathy O'Neil (2016). On algorithmic bias and its consequences for ordinary people. Pre-AI-boom but essential background on the bias issues from chapter fourteen.
- **"Atlas of AI"** by Kate Crawford (2021). A critical perspective on the physical, environmental, and labor costs of building AI systems. Important counter-weight to the dominant narrative of AI as ethereal software.

## If you want to understand AI policy

- **The 2026 International AI Safety Report**, chaired by Yoshua Bengio. The most comprehensive current overview of AI risks and the state of safety research. Available free online from the UK government.
- **"AI Snake Oil"** by Arvind Narayanan and Sayash Kapoor (2024). A skeptical view of AI hype, focused on distinguishing legitimate AI applications from snake oil. A useful corrective to industry boosterism.
- **The Stanford AI Index Report.** Annual. A comprehensive annual review of where AI capabilities, deployments, and policies stand. The 2026 edition is the most recent. Available free online.

## If you want to hear from the people building this

- **"Machines of Loving Grace"** (Dario Amodei, 2024). An essay by the CEO of Anthropic arguing for an optimistic vision of what AI could do for humanity. Worth reading even if you disagree — it is the clearest statement of the worldview behind one of the leading labs.
- **"Situational Awareness"** (Leopold Aschenbrenner, 2024). An essay laying out the case for transformative AI arriving within this decade. Influential among AI researchers. Argues for what this book called the "second trajectory."
- **"AI 2027"** (Daniel Kokotajlo and colleagues). A widely-read scenario laying out one specific way AI could develop over the next few years. Speculative but careful.

## If you want to think about what AI means for humans

- **"The Coming Wave"** by Mustafa Suleyman (2023). On AI and synthetic biology as the defining technologies of the century. Suleyman is one of the central figures in the field and writes with insider knowledge.
- **"Filterworld"** by Kyle Chayka (2024). On the effects of algorithmic curation on culture and identity. Not specifically about AI in the language-model sense, but essential background for thinking about how AI shapes the texture of modern life.
- **"Determined"** by Robert Sapolsky (2023). On free will and human agency. Not about AI, but reading it alongside AI material gives you a richer perspective on what we are and what we are not.

## If you just want to stay current

- **The Marginalian** by Maria Popova. Not AI-specific but consistently good thinking about technology and culture.
- **One Useful Thing** newsletter (Ethan Mollick) and **Import AI** newsletter (Jack Clark). Two of the most reliable sources for staying current with the field.
- **Press:** The AI sections of The New York Times, The Atlantic, The Information, and Bloomberg Technology are generally good. MIT Technology Review covers AI extensively and well.
- **Research updates:** Anthropic's blog, OpenAI's blog, Google DeepMind's blog, and the AI safety section of arxiv are where new technical work appears first.

## The author's final note

This is a snapshot from 2026. By the time you are reading this, half of the list may be dated. The good news: the people doing serious thinking about AI tend to keep doing serious thinking, so the authors above are mostly worth following for whatever they write next. Use their work as a starting point and follow your curiosity from there.
