## Plain text (Spotify for Authors, INaudio, podcast feeds, anywhere markup-free)

```
ChatGPT launched on a Wednesday in late 2022. Within five days, a million people had signed up. By February, a hundred million. It was, at the time, the fastest-growing consumer product in history.

Three years later, AI is in your phone, your search, your job, and the conversation at every dinner table. Most of what you read about it is either breathless or terrified. Neither tells you what's actually going on.

Just Predicting Words is a clear, honest, plain-language guide to how modern AI actually works, written for ordinary adults who want to understand the technology shaping their lives without needing a computer science degree.

In twenty-one chapters, Ziad El Feghali walks you through:

The "trick" that powers ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini, and why it produces results that look like far more than a trick.

The companies racing at the frontier: OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, and the Chinese labs Western media has been ignoring.

What AI is genuinely good at, where it fails (sometimes catastrophically), and the structural limits of what language alone can ever know.

Which risks deserve your worry, which are mostly hype, and what governments around the world are actually doing about it.

How to use these tools well at work, how to talk to your kids about them, and how to stay grounded while everything moves faster than the rules can keep up.

Read this if you've been nodding along in AI conversations without quite following them, if your work is being reshaped by tools you don't fully understand, or if you simply want a calm, careful map of where we actually are, and where we're actually going.

Written by a working professional, not an evangelist or a doomer.

The trick is small. The world it built is not.
```

## House style for any future copy edits

- **No em-dashes.** Use commas, periods, or parentheses.
- Straight quotes for words used as labels (e.g. *the "trick"*), not curly.
- Numbers in prose spelled out under a hundred (one million, a hundred million).
- "AI" stays uppercase, never spelled out as "artificial intelligence" in marketing copy.
