  Chapter Five

  OpenAI, the Company That Started the Wave

  A non-profit that became one of the most valuable companies in human history, in under a decade.

In December 2015, eleven people sat down to dinner at the Rosewood Hotel on Sand Hill Road in Menlo Park, California. The Rosewood is the kind of place where Silicon Valley deal-making happens. White tablecloths. Quiet servers. A wine list designed to impress people who have been impressed by everything.

The eleven people at the table that night were going to start a company. They had been talking about it for months. They were worried about something specific. They believed that artificial intelligence, the kind that did not yet exist in any serious form, was coming. They believed it would be the most consequential technology humanity had ever built. And they believed that if it was built by the wrong people, with the wrong intentions, in the wrong way, the consequences could be catastrophic.

So they were going to build it themselves. Carefully. With safety as the priority. And, in a move that was unusual for Silicon Valley, they were going to do it as a non-profit. No shareholders. No equity. No pressure to make money. Just a research mission, funded by donations, aimed at making sure that when transformative AI arrived, it arrived in ways that benefited humanity.

They pledged a billion dollars to fund the venture. The names were a who's who of the technology world. Elon Musk. Sam Altman. Reid Hoffman. Peter Thiel. Jessica Livingston. Greg Brockman. Ilya Sutskever, who would become their chief scientist. And a few others. Two companies, Amazon Web Services and Infosys, also pledged support, much of it in the form of compute credits rather than cash.

They called the company OpenAI.

That was December 2015.

What has happened to OpenAI since is one of the strangest stories in the history of business. The non-profit became a for-profit, in a structure so unusual that it generated lawsuits — the most prominent of which, brought by Elon Musk, went all the way to a jury trial in 2026. The founders fell out with each other in spectacular ways. The CEO was fired by his board, then re-hired four days later, in an episode that briefly looked like it might end the company. The company released a product that became the fastest-growing consumer application in history. And the same company, the one that started as a non-profit dedicated to safe AI for humanity, is now in talks to go public at a valuation that could reach one trillion dollars.

This chapter is the story of how that happened. We're going to walk through it because OpenAI is not just one company. It is, in a real sense, the company that defined what the AI industry looks like in 2026. The choices OpenAI made, both good and bad, have shaped how every other company in this space operates. To understand modern AI, you have to understand OpenAI.

Let's start at the beginning.

The founding idea.

The original premise of OpenAI was an unusual one. Most technology companies are started by people who want to build a product, sell it, and make money. OpenAI was started by people who were worried.

The worry, in 2015, was that artificial general intelligence, the kind of AI that could do everything a human can do and possibly more, was coming. The founders disagreed about when. Some thought decades. Some thought sooner. But they agreed it was coming, and they agreed that it would be the most powerful technology ever built, and they agreed that if a single company controlled it, the consequences for everyone else could be very bad.

Their solution was to make it open. Or at least, that was the idea behind the name. "OpenAI." Open research. Open papers. Open code. Anyone could see what they were doing. Anyone could build on top of it. The goal was to make sure that the development of advanced AI was a collaborative human project, not a proprietary corporate one.

You can already see, knowing what happened next, that this was going to be hard to maintain.

Within a few years, OpenAI had run into a basic problem. AI research turns out to be expensive. The kind of compute power needed to train cutting-edge models costs hundreds of millions, then billions, of dollars. A non-profit funded by donations could not keep up.

So in 2019, OpenAI did something almost unprecedented. They created a for-profit subsidiary, OpenAI LP, that would operate inside the non-profit shell. Investors could put money in. Returns to those investors would be capped at one hundred times their investment (an arrangement that seemed ridiculous at the time and now, in 2026, looks remarkably prescient about how much money was actually about to be made). The non-profit board would still govern the for-profit, in theory ensuring that the mission remained the priority. The cap was later removed in the October 2025 restructuring, when the for-profit arm became a public benefit corporation, but for most of the company's history it was the defining feature of OpenAI's commercial structure.

Elon Musk, who had been one of the largest funders of the original non-profit, did not approve of this transition. He had wanted OpenAI to remain a charity. Or, by some accounts that surfaced in his later lawsuit, he had wanted to acquire it outright. In testimony from May 2026, Sam Altman would tell a jury that Musk had at one point asked for ninety percent of the equity. Whatever the truth, Musk left the board in 2018, citing conflicts of interest with his other AI work at Tesla. The breach would never fully heal. Eight years later, the two men ended up on opposite sides of a federal lawsuit in Oakland, California. In May 2026, a jury rejected Musk's claims, finding he had waited too long to sue, and the judge dismissed the case. Musk has said he will appeal.

The for-profit conversion solved OpenAI's money problem. Microsoft invested one billion dollars in 2019. Then more. Then more. By 2026, Microsoft had directly committed over thirteen billion dollars to OpenAI, in exchange for various rights to use OpenAI's technology in its own products.

Around the same time, OpenAI made the second big choice that defined its trajectory. They bet on scale.

The scale bet.

We met this story in chapter two, but it's worth revisiting from OpenAI's perspective.

In 2018, the consensus view in AI research was that you would not get qualitatively new capabilities by just making models bigger. You needed clever new architectures, clever new training tricks. Just making the Transformer bigger and feeding it more data was supposed to hit diminishing returns.

OpenAI's leadership disagreed. They believed that if you simply scaled up the existing approach, qualitatively new behaviors would emerge. The model would not just be slightly better. It would be different in kind.

This was a contrarian bet. It required betting hundreds of millions of dollars on an idea that most of the field thought was wrong. And it worked.

GPT-2, the 2019 model, was bigger than anything that had come before and could write coherent paragraphs. GPT-3, in 2020, was bigger still, and could do things that startled even its creators. By the time GPT-4 arrived in 2023, the scale bet was no longer contrarian. Every major lab was scaling. The question was no longer whether scaling worked. It was whether it would ever stop working.

That question is still open. We will come back to it.

The point for our story is that OpenAI's two foundational bets, the bet on a unique structure and the bet on scale, both paid off. The structure let them raise the money to do the scaling. The scaling produced the breakthroughs that justified the structure. By late 2022, they had a model so capable that they decided to put it in front of the public.

The november night.

On November 30, 2022, OpenAI launched ChatGPT.

We've covered this moment in chapter one and chapter two. What I want to add here is what it looked like from inside the company.

The launch was, by most accounts, not supposed to be a big deal. The team expected modest interest. They had a backup plan in case interest was higher than expected. They did not have a backup plan for what actually happened, which was that the product went viral within hours.

In the first few days, the team scrambled to keep the servers running. The model was not designed to handle that level of demand. Costs spiraled. Engineers worked through the holiday season. Sam Altman would later tell people that the company "ran out of GPUs every day" during that period.

But the implications, beyond the operational chaos, were enormous. OpenAI had accidentally discovered that there was a massive consumer market for AI assistants. The product was free at first. They added a paid tier, ChatGPT Plus, at twenty dollars a month. People paid. Tens of millions of them.

The revenue curve, from late 2022 forward, looks like one of those charts they show you in business school to illustrate exponential growth. By early 2026, OpenAI was reportedly generating around twenty-four billion dollars a year in revenue, and rising. The company had become, in three years, one of the fastest-growing businesses in history.

The valuation followed. From a few hundred million dollars in 2019, to twenty billion in 2022, to eighty billion in 2023, to over eight hundred and fifty billion in 2026. As I write this, the company is reportedly preparing for an initial public offering at a target valuation around one trillion dollars.

If that number lands, OpenAI will be worth more than nearly any company on the planet that was not also one of the giants of the previous technology era. They will have done it in roughly a decade. From a dinner table at the Rosewood to one of the most valuable companies on Earth.

THE FIRING

In November 2023, something extraordinary happened.

The board of OpenAI, which still notionally controlled the for-profit subsidiary in the name of the non-profit mission, fired Sam Altman. They issued a statement saying he had not been "consistently candid" in his communications with them. They did not specify what they meant.

The reaction was immediate and overwhelming. Within hours, OpenAI's senior leadership had threatened to resign en masse. Microsoft, which had invested over ten billion dollars in the company by that point, was apparently caught completely off guard. Greg Brockman, the company's president, resigned in solidarity with Altman.

The next day, Microsoft offered Altman a job to come work for them and bring the entire OpenAI staff with him. Hundreds of OpenAI employees signed a letter saying they would follow.

Within four days, the board had reversed itself. Altman was reinstated. The board members who had fired him were themselves replaced. Altman returned to his job, with a much stronger position than he had held before.

To this day, the public still does not know with certainty what exactly the board had been concerned about. Various theories have circulated. Concerns about safety. Concerns about Altman's personal financial dealings. Concerns about communication and transparency with the board. The board members who pushed for the firing have mostly remained silent about their reasoning.

What we know is what came out of the episode. The board was reconstituted. The non-profit structure remained in place but the practical power of the non-profit board to constrain the for-profit operation seemed substantially diminished. Sam Altman emerged with a stronger position and a clearer mandate.

The structural concerns that the November 2023 firing was meant to address, whatever they were, did not get resolved. They merely got postponed.

The present tension.

As of May 2026, OpenAI sits at the center of a remarkable set of pressures.

There is the lawsuit from Elon Musk. Musk argued that OpenAI's transition from non-profit to for-profit was a betrayal of the original founding mission. He asked the court to undo the for-profit conversion, and sought up to one hundred and fifty billion dollars in damages. In May 2026, after an eleven-day trial in Oakland, a jury found that he had filed too late, and the judge dismissed the case. Musk's lawyers have said they will appeal.

There is the congressional investigation. The House Oversight Committee has opened an investigation into Sam Altman's personal investments and whether they have created conflicts of interest with OpenAI's strategic decisions. The investigation centers on companies Altman has personally invested in, including a fusion energy startup called Helion, which OpenAI has reportedly been steered toward as a partner. Ten state attorneys general have asked the SEC to scrutinize OpenAI's governance before any public offering.

There is the financial pressure. OpenAI is reportedly spending more than it earns, building data centers and signing compute contracts of historic scale, on the bet that the revenue curve will keep going up. The chief financial officer and the CEO are, according to reports, sometimes at odds about spending discipline.

There is the competitive pressure. Anthropic, the rival lab founded by former OpenAI employees, has caught up. By some measures, surpassed. Google has invested heavily in its own models. Chinese labs have produced open-weight models that match or beat OpenAI's flagship offerings on many benchmarks at a fraction of the cost. The market lead OpenAI held in 2023 has narrowed.

And there is the political pressure. AI is now a policy issue at the highest levels of every major government. OpenAI, as the most visible American AI company, is at the center of conversations about export controls, military partnerships, AI safety regulations, antitrust concerns, and economic competition with China.

For a company that started ten years ago with a dinner and a billion-dollar pledge, this is an extraordinary amount of weight to carry.

What OPENAI actually believes.

Behind all the corporate drama is a set of actual beliefs about AI that the company has held, consistently, for most of its history. Whether you trust the people stating these beliefs is another question. But the stated beliefs are worth understanding, because they shape how OpenAI builds its products.

The first belief is that artificial general intelligence is coming, possibly soon. Sam Altman has been on record predicting that human-level AI may arrive within the current decade. Many people inside OpenAI hold similar views. The company's strategic decisions, including the willingness to spend astronomical amounts on compute, only make sense if you take this belief seriously.

The second belief is that whoever builds AGI first will have an enormous amount of power. OpenAI's response to this concern, originally, was to make AI development open and collaborative. The current response is to try to be that builder themselves, while operating under what they argue are stronger safety constraints than the alternatives would have.

The third belief is that the benefits of AI should be widely distributed. OpenAI talks frequently about "broadly beneficial AGI." How exactly the benefits get distributed, given that the company is structured to return substantial profits to investors, is a question critics raise often.

The fourth belief is that the right pace is fast. OpenAI's leadership has consistently argued that the responsible approach is to deploy AI capabilities to the public quickly, learn from the deployment, and iterate. The opposing view, held by some others in the field, is that the right approach is to delay deployment until safety research has matured. OpenAI has chosen speed. The choice is consequential.

You can hold these beliefs and still draw different conclusions about what OpenAI should do. You can also doubt the beliefs themselves. Many serious people do both. But the company has been remarkably consistent in stating them, and the products you use from OpenAI are the products that beliefs like these produce.

What this means for you.

If you use ChatGPT, you are using a product made by a company with all of the features we just described. Brilliant. Driven. Conflicted. Caught between original ideals and commercial realities. Run by people who genuinely believe they are doing something historically important, who may or may not be right about that, and whose incentives are complicated by an unusual corporate structure and enormous amounts of money.

This is not a criticism of OpenAI specifically. It is just an honest description of the situation. Every major AI lab is doing some version of this dance. The details differ. The shape is similar.

What does it mean for you, the user?

It means that when ChatGPT does something that surprises you, helpful or not, the reasons behind that behavior are not just technical. They reflect product choices made by specific people, under specific pressures, with specific beliefs. The model is impressive. The product is a product.

It means that when you hear OpenAI announce a new model, or a new policy, or a new safety initiative, you are watching a company that is simultaneously trying to advance the science, satisfy investors, navigate regulators, fend off competitors, and maintain some version of its original mission. The announcement will be true. The full story will be more complicated.

It means that if the company succeeds, you and your children will live in a world significantly shaped by their choices. If it fails, the consequences for you, in the short term, will be modest. The technology will continue to exist. Other companies will pick up where they left off. But the specific texture of what we end up with, the personality of the AI we live alongside, will be different.

Either way, OpenAI is not the only player. To see the rest of the picture, we need to meet the company that broke off from OpenAI because they thought the pace was wrong.

We need to meet Anthropic.
