A short, opinionated list of things to read if you want to go
deeper than this book did. Organized by topic. Some books, some
papers, some essays. The list is short on purpose. You can find
many more recommendations elsewhere. These are the ones I think
are worth your time.

If You Want To Understand How The Technology Works...

"Attention Is All You Need" (Vaswani and colleagues, twenty seventeen)
The original Transformer paper. Technical, but the introduction
and conclusion are accessible to non-specialists. Worth at least
skimming for the historical importance.

"The Illustrated Transformer" (Jay Alammar)
The clearest visual explanation of how the Transformer works,
suitable for non-technical readers willing to engage. Available
free online.

"Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI" by Ethan Mollick
(twenty twenty-four)
A practical, optimistic guide to using AI tools effectively.
Mollick is a Wharton professor who has been a leading voice on
AI in education and business.

If You Want To Understand The Companies...

"Empire of AI" by Karen Hao (twenty twenty-five)
A deeply reported book on the rise of Open AI and the broader
industry. Hao is one of the journalists who has covered this
beat longest and most thoroughly.

"The Maniac" by Benjamin Labatut (twenty twenty-three)
A literary book about the foundations of computing and AI, told
through the lives of John von Neumann and others. Reads like
fiction, illuminates the philosophical roots of where we are now.

"Genius Makers" by Cade Metz (twenty twenty-one)
The story of how deep learning happened. Predates the Chat GPT
moment but provides essential context for who the people are
who built modern AI.

If You Want To Understand The Risks...

"The Alignment Problem" by Brian Christian (twenty twenty)
The clearest treatment of the technical and philosophical
problem of building AI systems that do what we actually want
them to do. Predates the current AI boom but the issues have
only become more pressing.

"Weapons of Math Destruction" by Cathy O'Neil (twenty sixteen)
On algorithmic bias and its consequences for ordinary people.
Pre-AI-boom but essential background on the bias issues we
covered in Chapter Fourteen.

"Atlas of AI" by Kate Crawford (twenty twenty-one)
A critical perspective on the physical, environmental, and
labor costs of building AI systems. Important counter-weight
to the dominant narrative of AI as ethereal software.

If You Want To Understand AI Policy...

"The twenty twenty-six International AI Safety Report" (Yoshua Bengio, chair)
The most comprehensive current overview of AI risks and the
state of safety research. Available free online from the UK
government.

"AI Snake Oil" by Arvind Narayanan and Sayash Kapoor (twenty twenty-four)
A skeptical view of AI hype, focused on distinguishing legitimate
AI applications from snake oil. A useful corrective to industry
boosterism.

The Stanford AI Index Report (Annual)
A comprehensive annual review of where AI capabilities,
deployments, and policies stand. The twenty twenty-six edition is the most
recent. Available free online.

If You Want To Hear From The People Building This...

"Machines of Loving Grace" (Dario Amodei, twenty twenty-four)
An essay by the CEO of Anthropic arguing for an optimistic
vision of what AI could do for humanity. Worth reading even if
you disagree, because it is the clearest statement of the
worldview behind one of the leading labs.

"Situational Awareness" (Leopold Aschenbrenner, twenty twenty-four)
An essay laying out the case for transformative AI arriving
within this decade. Influential among AI researchers. Argues
for what this book called the "second trajectory."

"AI twenty twenty-seven" (Daniel Kokotajlo and colleagues)
A widely-read scenario laying out one specific way AI could
develop over the next few years. Speculative but careful.

If You Want To Think About What AI Means For Humans...

"The Coming Wave" by Mustafa Suleyman (twenty twenty-three)
On AI and synthetic biology as the defining technologies of the
century. Suleyman is one of the central figures in the field
and writes with insider knowledge.

"Filterworld" by Kyle Chayka (twenty twenty-four)
On the effects of algorithmic curation on culture and identity.
Not specifically about AI in the LLM sense, but essential
background for thinking about how AI shapes the texture of
modern life.

"Determined" by Robert Sapolsky (twenty twenty-three)
On free will and human agency. Not about AI, but reading it
alongside AI material gives you a much richer perspective on
what we are and what we are not.

If You Just Want To Stay Current...

The Marginalian, by Maria Popova
Not AI-specific but consistently good thinking about technology
and culture.

The newsletters One Useful Thing (Ethan Mollick) and Import AI
(Jack Clark) are two of the most reliable sources for staying
current with the field.

For deeper coverage, the AI sections of The New York Times,
The Atlantic, The Information, and Bloomberg Technology are
generally good. The MIT Technology Review covers AI extensively
and well.

For research updates, Anthropic's blog, Open AI's blog, Google
Deep Mind's blog, and the AI safety section of arxiv are where
new technical work appears first.

A Final Note...

The list above is a snapshot from twenty twenty-six. By the time you are
reading this, half of it may be dated. The good news is that
the people doing serious thinking about AI tend to keep doing
serious thinking, so the authors above are mostly worth following
for whatever they write next. Use their work as a starting point
and follow your curiosity from there.

Reading widely and thinking carefully is, in my view, the best
preparation for what is coming. This book has tried to give you
a foundation. The rest is up to you.

Thank you for reading.
