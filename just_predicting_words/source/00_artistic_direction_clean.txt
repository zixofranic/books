ARTISTIC DIRECTION

Research-based design system for the book, the audiobook,
and the visual identity. Built from analysis of:

- Stripe Press (the gold standard for tech-books-as-objects)
- Tim Urban / Wait But Why (the gold standard for visual
  explanation of complex ideas to mass audiences)
- Algorithms to Live By (Brian Christian — example-driven prose
  that translated computer science to general readers)
- Penguin Composition Rules (Jan Tschichold's century-old
  rules that still define what a beautiful book looks like)
- Tufte / Bringhurst / Lupton (the design canon)
- Current Kindle bestseller analysis for AI books

Part one. what the research actually says.

The single most important finding from this research is something
simple. Books that successfully translate complex technical ideas
to mass audiences share four traits, in this order of importance:

1. THE WRITING DOES THE WORK. Visuals support, never substitute.
   Tim Urban's stick figures work not because they're beautiful
   but because they're attached to clear writing. Brian Christian's
   Algorithms to Live By has almost no illustrations — it succeeds
   on the strength of analogies in the prose.

2. VISUAL SIMPLICITY IS NON-NEGOTIABLE. Urban explicitly says:
   "I don't try to pretend to do fancy, well-produced visuals. I
   do it like I'd teach a friend if I had a napkin at a restaurant."
   Tufte calls this the same thing in different words: no chartjunk.
   Strip out everything that doesn't carry information.

3. CONSISTENCY BEATS BRILLIANCE. Stripe Press books look like
   Stripe Press books across 30 different titles. The system
   matters more than any individual cover. A coherent visual
   identity across all chapters of a single book matters more
   than any individual illustration being a masterpiece.

4. THE OBJECT MATTERS. In an age of disposable content, a
   beautifully-made physical book is "imbued with a sense of
   sacredness." (That's a direct quote from the Stripe Press
   analysis.) People keep beautiful books. They display them.
   They give them as gifts. This matters commercially.

A fifth finding worth naming: the best books in this category
don't have a single visual style — they have a SYSTEM. A visual
language with rules, applied consistently, that the reader
unconsciously learns. By chapter four, a reader of Stripe Press
or Wait But Why has internalized the rules and any deviation
from them registers as meaningful.

Part two. locked artistic direction for the book.

Here is the locked direction. Each piece below is a decision
that flows from the research above and from the voice we
already calibrated (conversational, example-rich, warm,
aha-driven, audio-friendly).

The overall posture.

The book sits at the intersection of:

- Stripe Press (designed-as-an-object, considered, intellectual)
- Wait But Why (warmth, accessibility, hand-drawn feel)
- Penguin Modern Classics (typographic restraint, classical
  proportions, trust)

It is NOT:
- A textbook (no chartjunk, no boxes around things, no "Chapter
  Objectives" lists)
- A children's book (no condescension, no oversimplified visuals)
- A coffee-table book (no glossy spreads, no full-bleed photos)
- A blog post in a binding (it has the weight and considered
  pacing of a real book)

COLOR PALETTE

Based on warm-but-restrained mid-century modern palettes,
adjusted for both screen and print, working in both light
backgrounds and Kindle grayscale.

PRIMARY PALETTE (use these for almost everything)

   CREAM            #F5EFE3   off-white background, page color
   INK              #2C2A26   primary text, primary lines
   TERRACOTTA       #D85A30   accent, key moments, emphasis
   DEEP CLAY        #9C3818   secondary accent, subhead color

SUPPORT PALETTE (use sparingly, for diagrams that need
distinction)

   DUSTY BLUE       #6B8C9E   technical / structural diagrams
   OLIVE            #7A8450   data and process diagrams
   AGED PAPER       #E8DDC3   subtle background for callouts
   CHARCOAL         #4A4742   secondary text, captions

That's it. Eight colors. Two warm accents, two cool support
colors, three neutrals, one ink. Any illustration in the book
uses at most three of these per composition. Most use just
two: ink on cream, with terracotta for emphasis.

Why this palette works.

The cream (not pure white) reduces eye strain and signals
"considered book object" rather than "PDF." The terracotta and
deep clay are warm without being cute, accessible without being
childish. They carry a faint Mediterranean / mid-century
sensibility that distinguishes the book on a shelf full of
flat-color tech book covers.

In grayscale on a standard Kindle, the cream goes to a very
light gray (~95% white), the ink stays deep, and the terracotta
becomes a medium-dark gray that still reads as "accent." All
diagrams remain legible. This is verified by converting the
palette through grayscale simulation.

In color Kindle (Colorsoft), the warm palette reproduces
better than cool blues — which is one of the few honest
advantages we get from designing for color e-ink.

TYPOGRAPHY

The research is clear: serif body text, in a tradition reaching
back to Tschichold's Penguin Composition Rules. We follow it.

BODY TEXT
   EB Garamond, 11pt, 1.4 line spacing

   Why: free, open-source, optically equivalent to commercial
   Garamond. Narrow set means lower page counts for print
   (saves money on KDP) without sacrificing readability.
   Recommended by every serious typography source for
   nonfiction. Reads beautifully on Kindle.

Chapter numbers and titles.
   Fraunces, weight 500, varied sizes

   Why: contemporary serif with personality, free on Google
   Fonts, looks deliberate without looking trendy. The slight
   warmth in its letterforms matches the warm palette.

SECTION HEADINGS (the small ones inside chapters)
   EB Garamond, small caps, letter-spaced

   Why: avoiding sans-serif for headings keeps the book
   tonally consistent. Small caps with extra letter-spacing
   look elegant and don't shout.

Captions and diagram labels.
   Inter, 9-10pt

   Why: this is the one place sans-serif is right. Captions
   need to feel like they belong to a different register than
   the main prose. Inter is free, modern, and reads well at
   small sizes.

Monospace for code and tokens.
   JetBrains Mono, 10pt

   Why: when we show tokens or any "machine-y" element, we
   want it to feel like machine output. JetBrains Mono is
   free, readable, and doesn't have the dated feel of Courier.

That's it. four typefaces total.

This is intentional. More typefaces = more decisions = more
chances for the book to look amateur. Four typefaces, each
with a single job, applied consistently, beats fifteen
typefaces every time.

ILLUSTRATION DIRECTION

exploration. The research confirms this hybrid is right but
sharpens it as follows:

Direction 3 (blueprint-architectural).
   Used for: technical diagrams inside chapters. Architecture,
   anatomy, system structure. Anything that requires labels
   and precision.

   Style rules: dusty blue lines on cream. Single thickness
   stroke (0.75pt). Labels in EB Garamond small caps for
   parts, Inter for measurements/IDs. No fills except for
   emphasis. Treat each diagram like a 1960s engineering
   manual page.

   Reference: Bauhaus design manuals, NASA technical
   schematics, mid-century architectural drawings.

Direction 4 (illustrative-metaphorical).
   Used for: chapter opener illustrations. The "feeling" of
   each chapter. Concepts shown as spatial metaphors that
   teach by experience.

   Style rules: terracotta and ink on cream. Hand-drawn feel
   in the line work (slight wobble, varied line weight).
   Composition is asymmetric, generous white space, single
   focal point. Think of these as the visual equivalent of
   a haiku — one strong image, room to breathe.

   Reference: New Yorker spot illustrations, Penguin Classic
   covers from the 1960s, Saul Bass minimalism.

Direction 5 (lebanese-modernist).
   Used for: part-opening pages (the 6 part dividers). One
   composition per part. These pages do nothing but exist
   beautifully.

   Style rules: warmer palette (cream, terracotta, deep clay,
   ink). Geometric composition with a single asymmetric
   element. May include a small Arabic kufic motif if it
   serves the part's theme. Otherwise pure modernist geometry.

   Reference: Massimo Vignelli book design, the Bauhaus
   masters, Lebanese mid-century graphic design.

The shared rules across all three directions.

- Cream background. Never pure white.
- Ink for primary strokes and text.
- Terracotta as the only "warm accent" — used sparingly,
  3-5 times max per illustration.
- No gradients. Solid fills only.
- No drop shadows.
- No 3D effects.
- Hand-drawn feel preferred over machine-perfect.
- Generous negative space. White space is the most important
  element on every page.
- Maximum 3 colors per illustration.
- Every illustration must work in grayscale.

PAGE LAYOUT

Trim size: 6 × 9 inches (Amazon KDP standard for nonfiction)

Margins:
   Top: 0.75"
   Bottom: 0.75"
   Outside: 0.75"
   Gutter (inside): 0.875"

Line length: ~65 characters per line at 11pt EB Garamond.
This is the classical optimum for sustained reading.

Running heads:
   Even pages: book title in small caps, 9pt, centered
   Odd pages: chapter title in small caps, 9pt, centered
   No running heads on chapter opener pages

Page numbers:
   Bottom outside corner, 10pt Inter
   No page numbers on chapter opener pages

Chapter openers (every chapter starts on a right-hand page):
   composition (Direction 5)
   epigraph
   letter

This is the rhythm of a real book. The reader feels the
ceremony of each new chapter starting.

COVER DESIGN

The cover is its own decision, made closer to publication.
But the artistic direction should guide it.

Principles for the cover.

- Typographic-led, not illustration-led. The title is the
  hero. This is the Stripe Press / Penguin Modern Classics
  move.

- Use the same palette as the interior. Cream background,
  ink type, terracotta accent.

- Title set in Fraunces, large, taking most of the cover.

- One small visual element — possibly a single Direction 5
  composition in miniature — as the visual signature.

- Author name in EB Garamond small caps at the top or bottom.

- No subtitles in giant type. The subtitle "How ChatGPT,
  Claude, and the AI Revolution Actually Work" goes in
  modest sans-serif under the title.

REFERENCE COVERS (for inspiration only, not imitation):
- Stripe Press, "The Dream Machine" by M. Mitchell Waldrop
- Stripe Press, "Working in Public" by Nadia Eghbal
- Penguin Modern Classics covers from 2015-2020
- Persephone Books interior covers

What the cover must not do.

- Show a brain made of circuits
- Show a robot hand reaching out of a screen
- Show a hooded figure in front of binary code
- Show ChatGPT or Claude logos
- Use neon blue, electric purple, or "tech gradient"
- Look like every other AI book on Amazon right now

These are the cliches that have flooded the AI book market
in 2024-2026. Avoiding all of them is, by itself, a strong
positioning move.

KINDLE-SPECIFIC ADJUSTMENTS

Kindle reflows text, so the print design doesn't fully
translate. Specific adjustments:

- Body text: render in user's choice (we don't control this
  on Kindle, only print)
- Illustrations: shipped as PNG at 1200x1800 resolution,
  optimized for both color and grayscale display
- Chapter openers: the illustration becomes a single image,
  followed by a clean title page
- Drop caps: render as an embedded image rather than CSS,
  for consistency across devices
- Section breaks: three centered dots (· · ·) work well in
  both formats
- Tables: avoid them, use prose instead
- Footnotes: convert to endnotes per chapter, linked back

Font embedding on kindle.

We CAN embed EB Garamond and Fraunces in the EPUB. We
SHOULD. Kindle then respects our type choice unless the
reader overrides it (most don't). This is what makes a
Kindle book feel "designed" versus generic.

The audiobook visual identity.

Even an audiobook has visual elements: the cover that
appears in Audible, Spotify, Apple Books. These should
match the print cover exactly. No separate "audiobook cover."

Additional audiobook visual element: a 10-second intro/outro
animation for the chapter beginnings (if we choose to do
this). Simple geometric composition in the book's palette,
typographic. Reference: the visual identity of "Revisionist
History" or "Cautionary Tales" podcasts.

But honestly: most audiobook listeners never see the cover
after the first time. The audiobook's "design" is its sound
design — pacing, pauses, music. That's its own conversation
when we get to production.

Part three. how the writing and the visuals work together.

This is the rule we should never break:

The writing teaches. the visuals reinforce.

If a chapter's idea isn't fully understandable from the text
alone, the text isn't done. Visuals are a reward, not a
crutch. This is the Tim Urban discipline. This is also the
Brian Christian discipline (he has almost no visuals at all,
and his book is a #1 bestseller).

For every illustration, there should be a moment in the
prose where you can imagine the reader pausing, having an
aha, and then turning the page to see the illustration that
confirms what they just figured out. The illustration is the
applause. Not the explanation.

This means our visual budget can be smaller than I initially
sketched. Instead of 40-55 illustrations across the book,
we may end up with closer to 25-30:

   6 part-opening compositions (Direction 5)
   23 chapter-opening illustrations (Direction 4)
   5-8 interior technical diagrams (Direction 3) — only
       where the prose genuinely needs them

That's 34-37 illustrations total. Less than I projected, but
each one earning its place. This is actually the right
number for a serious nonfiction book at this scale. More
would dilute, fewer would feel undesigned.

Part four. the production pipeline.

Once we move to Claude Code on the ASUS, here's what the
pipeline looks like for visuals specifically:

Source of truth.
   /book/illustrations/
       part_01.svg
       chapter_01_opener.svg
       chapter_03_attention.svg
       ...

   All illustrations live as SVG files. Single source.

BUILD OUTPUTS
   For print: SVG → PDF (embedded vector, prints at any
   resolution)
   For Kindle EPUB: SVG → PNG at 1200x1800px, optimized
   For audiobook cover: SVG → JPG at 3000x3000px

The build script.
   A small Node or Python script that reads the SVG
   library and produces all required output formats. Run
   it whenever an illustration changes. Run it once at the
   end to produce final assets.

We build this together on the ASUS when the writing is
further along. For now, we generate SVGs in Claude.ai and
save them.

Part five. what this commits us to.

decision should be tested against it:

- New illustration concept? Does it use the locked palette
  and one of the three directions? If not, redesign it.
- New chapter? Does it follow the chapter opener rhythm
  (illustration page, title page, body)?
- New typographic choice? Does it use one of the four
  locked typefaces?
- New visual idea? Does it pass the Tim Urban test — would
  it work as a napkin sketch?

This is what "designed" means. Not that every visual is
brilliant, but that every visual fits a system the reader
has unconsciously learned to trust.
