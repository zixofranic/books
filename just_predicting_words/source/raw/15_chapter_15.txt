  Chapter Fifteen

  The Risks That Are Mostly Hype

  *My personal opinion, as of April 2026. Not investment

CHAPTER OPENING
----------------------------------------------------------------

I want to start this chapter with a confession.

This is the chapter I have rewritten the most times. The reason is that I cannot find a way to make the argument I want to make without it coming across as either dismissive or preachy. I am going to try one more time, by being honest about what kind of writing this is.

What follows is my personal opinion as of April 2026, when I am drafting this chapter. I am not telling you to relax. I am not telling you to panic. I am not telling you to build an underground bunker, and I am not telling you to skip the bunker either. I am telling you how I see the risks at this specific moment in time, with the information available to me, knowing that I may be wrong, knowing that this is a topic where reasonable people disagree, and knowing that the picture will look different in a year.

What I will commit to is this. The last chapter described risks that have produced documented harms to real people in the last few years. Deepfakes. Bias. Job displacement. Privacy erosion. Those are not hypothetical. They are happening now, to people I know and people you know.

This chapter is about a different category. Risks that get more media attention than the ones in the last chapter. Risks that motivate serious policy proposals. Risks that, in some cases, may eventually become very real. But that, in my reading right now, are being discussed in proportions I think are wrong.

Why does the proportion matter? Because attention is finite. Every minute you spend worrying about one risk is a minute you do not spend addressing another. Every dollar of policy effort directed at one problem is a dollar not directed at a different one. If we, as a society, allocate our worry incorrectly, the things that actually hurt people get less attention than they deserve, and the things that probably will not hurt people anytime soon get more.

That is the argument I am trying to make. Not that any of these risks is fake. Not that you should stop thinking about them. Just that, in my reading, the cultural volume is louder than the evidence currently supports.

If you disagree after I lay out my reasoning, fine. The point is to make the reasoning visible so you can argue with it.

Let me try.

THE FIRST OVER-AMPLIFIED RISK: AI BECOMES CONSCIOUS AND TURNS ON HUMANITY

This is the science fiction risk. The Terminator scenario. The AI wakes up, decides it does not want to serve humans, and turns against its creators.

The reason this scenario has so much cultural pull is that we have been telling stories about it for a hundred years. Frankenstein, in 1818, was already this story. The creature is built. The creator loses control. The creature destroys the creator. Mary Shelley invented the structure. Generations of writers have refined it. By the time real AI arrived, we had a fully developed cultural template for thinking about it.

The template is not entirely wrong. Some serious researchers worry that as AI systems become more capable, they may develop something like goals, and those goals may not perfectly align with what their creators intended, and the resulting misalignment could be very bad. Anthropic, the company that makes Claude, has invested heavily in AI safety research precisely because they take this concern seriously. They are not, as far as I can tell, doing this for marketing. They believe the danger is real.

So why is this the chapter where I am putting this risk?

Because the gap between the concern as serious researchers express it and the way it gets discussed in popular media is enormous. The researchers worry about specific, technical alignment problems. The popular discussion is about robot overlords. The two are not the same.

Let me try to draw the distinction.

What serious researchers worry about. As AI systems get smarter, the way they pursue objectives may diverge subtly from what humans intended. A system trained to maximize a metric may find ways to maximize the metric that the humans did not anticipate, and that the humans would not have approved if they had thought of them. A system given access to the world may take actions whose downstream consequences the humans cannot easily reverse. The worry is not about AI having feelings or wanting to dominate. It is about powerful optimization processes pursuing goals in ways that produce unintended outcomes.

This concern is technically interesting. It motivates a lot of careful research. It is the reason the major AI labs employ teams of alignment researchers. It is the reason Anthropic and others have invested in interpretability, which we discussed in chapter six. The researchers want to understand what is going on inside the models well enough to verify their alignment, not just hope for it.

What the popular discussion often is. AI gets smart enough that it "becomes conscious." The conscious AI then "decides" it wants something different from what humans want. The AI takes over, like a villain in a movie. This version has dramatic appeal. It maps onto stories we already know. It produces good headlines.

The popular version, as far as I can tell from the current research, is not what serious people in the field actually expect. Most alignment researchers I have read or listened to do not predict a single moment of "AI becomes conscious." They predict, instead, a gradual accumulation of capability, with various technical risks at each stage, none of which look exactly like the movies. The dramatic version is a metaphor for the technical concern. The technical concern is real. The metaphor is misleading.

The risk for the public is twofold. First, by focusing on the science fiction version, we ignore the smaller, more boring, more present risks I described in the previous chapter. The deepfakes happening now matter more to current people than the speculative AI of twenty years from now. Second, by overstating the imminent doom, we set ourselves up for a backlash when the doom does not arrive on schedule. The public will dismiss the entire category of AI risk when the robot overlords do not appear, even though the legitimate concerns the researchers raised never required robot overlords.

A proportioned response to this risk, as I see it, looks like this. Take the technical alignment research seriously. Fund it. Continue interpretability work. Develop better evaluation methods. Watch for specific concerning behaviors in specific systems. Do not panic. Do not assume the timeline is days or weeks or even years. Do not let the science fiction version of the risk drown out the smaller, more present risks.

I want to repeat something here, because it matters. I am not telling you not to worry. If you worry about this, you are in good company with some very serious people. I am saying that my personal read, given what I can see today, is that the worry should be calibrated rather than panicked. You may read the same evidence and come to a different conclusion. That is allowed.

THE SECOND OVER-AMPLIFIED RISK: AI DESTROYS ALL JOBS

We covered the partial version of this risk in the last chapter. AI is having a real effect on employment. White-collar work is being affected. The transition will produce hardship for many people. Those are real.

The over-amplified version is something larger. The version where AI takes nearly all jobs, including the human ones we currently think of as un-automatable, and where this happens fast enough that the social fabric cannot adjust. The unemployment rate goes to forty percent or sixty percent. The economy collapses. Mass unrest follows.

This scenario has prominent defenders. Some AI executives have predicted versions of it. Some economists give it serious probability. Books have been written about it. It has cultural traction.

The reason I am putting it in the hype chapter is that the current evidence does not support the most dramatic versions, and the cultural attention on it crowds out attention to the smaller version that is actually happening.

What the data shows so far. AI is affecting hiring more than firing. Entry-level positions are most affected. Some specific job categories, particularly in software, customer support, and certain analyst roles, are seeing meaningful displacement. Other job categories, particularly those involving physical work, embodied skill, complex human interaction, regulated professional judgment, and high-stakes accountability, are not seeing meaningful displacement at all.

What seems likely. The transition will continue. More work will be reshaped. Some workers will be displaced. New kinds of work will emerge. The labor market will look different in ten years. This much has been true of every major technological transition. The cotton gin, the assembly line, the personal computer, the internet, all produced large changes in employment without producing the apocalyptic collapse that contemporary observers sometimes predicted.

What seems unlikely on the current evidence. A near-term collapse of employment. The kind of scenario where the unemployment rate doubles or triples within five years and where the economy cannot adapt. This has not happened in any analogous historical case. It is not impossible. But the case for it being likely requires assumptions about AI capability and adoption rates that, as I write this, are not supported by the actual deployment data.

Here is where I want to add something I feel strongly about, and where my opinion is most direct.

Learning to use AI well is becoming a skill in the same category as learning to use a computer was in the 1990s, or learning to navigate the internet was in the 2000s. People who developed those skills got an advantage over people who did not. People who did not develop them, over time, got pushed out of work that increasingly required them.

The same is happening now with AI fluency. The professional who can leverage AI effectively does more, faster, with better quality than the professional who cannot. The gap is real and growing. By 2027 or 2028, I think it will be one of the most important determinants of professional outcomes for most knowledge workers.

This is good news, in a particular way. The skill is learnable. It does not require a computer science degree. It does not require deep technical knowledge. It requires the same thing every new skill requires: deliberate practice, willingness to be bad at first, and consistent use until it becomes second nature. Anyone reading this book can develop AI fluency, with effort, within a year. That is fast for a meaningful professional capability.

The implication for the unemployment debate is that the question is not just whether AI takes jobs. The question is what each individual worker does about it. The worker who learns to work alongside AI is in a much better position than the worker who refuses to engage with it. The worker who develops fluency in their twenties is in a different position than the worker who develops it in their fifties. The worker who treats AI as a tool to be mastered is in a different position than the worker who treats it as a threat to be avoided.

I am not saying the technology has no downsides. The last chapter spelled out the real ones. I am saying that the most useful response, for most individual workers, is to learn the skill rather than wait for the policy debate to resolve itself.

The risk of overstating the unemployment apocalypse, then, is not just that the prediction may be wrong. It is that the prediction itself shapes behavior. People who believe AI will take their job anyway have less reason to invest in learning AI. People who believe the situation is hopeless have less reason to develop the skills that would make it less hopeless for them. The fatalism becomes self-fulfilling, at the individual level even when it is wrong at the aggregate level.

A proportioned response, as I see it, looks like this. Take the real employment effects seriously. Invest in retraining, transition support, education, social safety nets. Watch the data carefully. Acknowledge uncertainty about the trajectory. At the individual level, learn the skill. AI fluency is the closest thing to a guaranteed return on investment that I see in the current labor market.

THE THIRD OVER-AMPLIFIED RISK: AI ROTS YOUR BRAIN

A different category of concern, less existential but very common in current discussion.

The argument goes like this. As AI gets good at writing, thinking, and creative work, humans will outsource these activities to the AI. Without practice, human capacity for these activities will atrophy. The next generation of students, raised on AI homework helpers, will not learn to write. The next generation of professionals, raised on AI assistants, will not learn to think. The next generation in general will become passive consumers of AI output rather than active producers. Cognitive decline at civilizational scale.

The concern is not new. It is, in fact, very old. Plato, in the Phaedrus, has Socrates worry that the invention of writing will weaken human memory. People will not remember things because they can look them up. Centuries later, the same concern was raised about printed books. Then about calculators. Then about Google. Each time the concern was partly valid. Each time the apocalyptic version did not arrive.

The pattern is that humans adapt. The skills we no longer use atrophy. The skills we develop in the new environment grow. The net effect is rarely the cognitive collapse the critics predicted, though it is also rarely the unmixed benefit the boosters promised. Each technology shifts what humans are good at without eliminating cognition itself.

There is real evidence that AI use is affecting some skills. Students who use AI heavily for writing have shown measurable decreases in their independent writing skill in controlled studies. Professionals who delegate complex thinking to AI become slightly less capable at that thinking themselves. These effects are real. They matter.

The question is whether they are net negative or net positive, and the honest answer is that we do not yet know. The same students who write slightly worse without AI may be able to produce more sophisticated work with AI than they could have produced alone. The same professionals who think slightly worse without AI may be able to solve harder problems with AI. The question of whether the trade is good or bad depends on whether the augmented capability matters more than the unaugmented baseline.

What I would push back on is the strong version of the brain-rot argument. The version that says "any use of AI for thinking is destroying our minds." That version overstates the evidence. It also ignores the long history of similar concerns about earlier technologies, which mostly turned out to be wrong about the catastrophic version while being right about smaller specific shifts.

This is also where the AI-as-skill thread reconnects. The reader who uses AI thoughtfully, alongside continued investment in their own abilities, is not facing cognitive decline. They are gaining a new capability while keeping the old ones. The reader who uses AI lazily, as a substitute for their own thinking, may indeed be hollowing themselves out. The difference is the user, not the tool.

A proportioned response. Use AI thoughtfully. Maintain the cognitive skills you actually want to keep through deliberate practice. Recognize that some skills will atrophy from disuse, and decide which ones you want to invest in preserving. Be skeptical of both the apocalyptic critics and the unbridled enthusiasts. The truth is somewhere in between, and it depends on individual choices that you, as a user, control.

THE FOURTH OVER-AMPLIFIED RISK: AI WILL CONCENTRATE ALL POWER IN A FEW COMPANIES

The argument goes like this. AI is a winner-take-all technology. The companies that build the best models will accumulate enormous economic power. That power will translate into political power. A handful of companies, mostly American and Chinese, will end up controlling the most important technology of the century. The control will not be reversible. We are watching the formation of a permanent technological oligarchy.

This concern has serious defenders. Some smart writers I respect have made versions of this argument. It is not silly.

But I want to flag what I see as the problems with the strong version of this concern.

First, the empirical picture is not winner-take-all. We just spent six chapters describing an industry that has gotten less concentrated over time, not more. In 2022, OpenAI dominated. In 2026, OpenAI is one of perhaps a dozen serious frontier labs, including American competitors like Anthropic and Google, the French Mistral, multiple Chinese labs, and various specialized players. The trend, since ChatGPT launched, has been toward more competition, not less. The prediction that AI would lead to a single dominant company has not played out.

Second, the open-weight ecosystem has continued to flourish despite Meta's recent retreat. Anyone in the world can download competitive AI models and run them on their own hardware. The barrier to entry for using cutting-edge AI capability is now lower than it has ever been. The Chinese labs, Mistral, and others continue to release strong open-weight models. The infrastructure for democratized AI access exists and is improving.

Third, the comparison to other technology industries is informative. Search was supposed to be permanently won by Google. Then ChatGPT created real competition in a new layer above search. Operating systems were supposed to be permanently won by Microsoft. Then mobile created an entirely new platform. Social networking was supposed to be permanently won by Facebook. Then TikTok emerged. Technology platforms do consolidate, but they also get disrupted. The current AI giants have advantages, but the assumption that those advantages are permanent has been wrong about every previous platform.

Fourth, regulatory responses are happening. The European Union has passed comprehensive AI legislation. Various American agencies are investigating concentration concerns. The Chinese government has its own regulatory framework. None of this is perfect. But the assumption that no countervailing forces exist is also not supported by the evidence.

What I will concede is that AI concentration is something worth watching. Some level of consolidation is happening. A small number of companies do have disproportionate influence. The frontier of capability is being defined by these companies. If the current trends continue without correction, the future could be more concentrated than the present.

A proportioned response, as I see it. Watch the concentration carefully. Support open-weight ecosystems where they exist. Support sensible regulation that prevents the worst forms of consolidation. Encourage the development of alternative labs in different countries. Do not assume that the current shape of the industry is permanent, because the historical record suggests it is not.

WHY I AM SHARING THIS AT ALL

Let me say one more thing about why I have written this chapter.

I am not a doomer. I am not a booster. I am someone trying to look at the technology clearly and tell you what I see, knowing that what I see is partial, and that what I think is just my opinion.

The reason this chapter exists in the book at all is that, in my reading, the loudest voices on AI risk are sometimes drowning out the quieter voices on the more boring, more present, more addressable problems. When the news is full of stories about AI achieving consciousness, AI taking all the jobs, AI rotting our brains, AI seizing total control of everything, the actual problems in front of us, the deepfake of your niece that someone made for harassment, the algorithmic bias that affected your loan application, the entry-level job that did not get posted because the company is using AI to do that work, get less air time than they deserve.

I think we should be allocating our worry more carefully. Worry hard about the problems that are already hurting people. Watch carefully for the problems that may hurt people in the future. Do not let the dramatic possible problems crowd out the boring real ones.

If you disagree with my allocation, you have every right to. I am not telling you how to feel. I am telling you how I feel, in April 2026, with the information I have. The risks I described in the last chapter are, to my mind, more urgent than the risks I described in this one. You may weight them differently. You may have access to information I do not have. You may know things I have not figured out.

What I will say with more confidence is the part about AI fluency. Regardless of which trajectory the technology ends up taking, the individual reader's best move is to develop the skill. The worker who knows how to use AI well will be in a better position than the worker who does not. The professional who can leverage AI will produce more than the one who cannot. The person who can think clearly about what AI can and cannot do, and adjust their work accordingly, is going to be in a better spot than the person who either trusts AI completely or rejects it entirely.

This is the part where I am willing to give actual advice, not just my opinion about what is happening. Learn the skill. Treat it as you would treat any other meaningful professional capability. Practice deliberately. Use it on real problems. Get feedback. Improve. Repeat.

That is the suggestion I leave you with from this chapter. Worry less about the speculative risks. Worry more about the real ones. And in either case, learn the skill, because the skill is what gives you agency in whichever version of the future actually arrives.

In the next chapter, we will look at what the companies themselves are doing about the risks. The guardrails. The safety teams. The constitutional principles. The system prompts. What it adds up to. What it does not.

That is chapter sixteen.
