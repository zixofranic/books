THE LOCKED PALETTE

   #F5EFE3   Cream background
   #2C2A26   Ink, primary marks and text
   #D85A30   Terracotta, accent for input or central concept
   #9C3818   Deep clay, machine state and secondary emphasis
   #4A4742   Charcoal, descriptive labels in italic

Use three colors plus the background, never more, in any single 
diagram. Most diagrams use two plus background.


THE LOCKED CONVENTIONS

TOKENS
   Thin-ruled rectangles containing the token text.
   Border: ink, 0.75pt stroke, very subtle 2pt corner radius.
   Text inside: serif, ink color, centered.
   Every place a token appears in any diagram in this book, it 
   uses this convention. No exceptions.

WHAT THE HUMAN WRITES
   When showing the original human input, the box gets a 
   terracotta-colored text fill.
   This signals "this is the starting point" and draws the 
   reader's eye to it first.

WHAT THE MACHINE SEES
   When showing the machine's internal representation, the 
   element uses ink color for primary marks and deep clay for 
   numbers or machine-specific data.

NUMBERS AND MACHINE-FORMATTED CONTENT
   Always monospace. Always deep clay color. 
   This visually separates "what the machine computes" from 
   "what the human reads."

TRANSFORMATIONS
   Dashed lines (3pt dash, 3pt gap). 
   Used when one thing becomes another thing.
   Solid lines are reserved for actual connections, references, 
   or attention flows.

MARGIN LABELS
   Italic serif, charcoal, small (13pt).
   Sit in the left margin, providing meta-context for what is 
   shown in the diagram body.
   Examples: "What the human writes" / "What the machine sees" /
   "After embedding" / "Inside the attention layer"

CAPTIONS
   Italic serif, charcoal, small (13pt).
   Sit beneath a thin horizontal rule near the bottom of the 
   diagram.
   Optional. Used only when the diagram benefits from a 
   one-sentence interpretation.

STROKE WEIGHTS
   Box borders: 0.75pt
   Connection lines: 0.75pt
   Transformation lines: 0.5pt dashed
   Section rules: 0.5pt at 30% opacity

TYPOGRAPHY IN DIAGRAMS
   Body text and token text: EB Garamond (or Georgia substitute)
   Numbers and machine output: JetBrains Mono (or monospace 
   substitute)
   Labels and captions: EB Garamond italic, smaller size


THE TEACHING DISCIPLINE

Every diagram must answer the question: what does the reader 
learn from this?

If the diagram cannot complete this sentence with something 
specific, it should not be in the book.

Every diagram must pass the Tufte test, the Alammar test, the 
Urban test, and the prose-pairing test. (See illustration 
strategy document for details.)
