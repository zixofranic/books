================================================================
ILLUSTRATION STRATEGY FOR JUST PREDICTING WORDS
Research-based principles for visualizing complex AI systems
================================================================

This document supersedes parts of the original artistic_direction.txt.
The artistic system (palette, typography, page size) still holds.
What changes is the PURPOSE of the visuals. They must teach. They
must not just decorate.


================================================================
THE FUNDAMENTAL SHIFT
================================================================

The original artistic direction over-indexed on decorative visuals.
Part dividers as geometric compositions. Chapter openers as visual
"haiku." That was wrong for a book that exists to teach how AI
actually works.

The right model is what Jay Alammar did in "The Illustrated 
Transformer" - the post that became the standard reference for 
how this stuff gets explained, was translated into 12 languages, 
and is used in courses at Stanford, MIT, Harvard, Princeton, 
CMU. Combined with Tufte's principles of information design.

Every visual must answer: WHAT DOES THE READER LEARN FROM THIS?
If the answer is "the chapter looks pretty," cut the visual.
If the answer is "the reader sees the concept become clear,"
keep it.


================================================================
TUFTE'S PRINCIPLES (the foundation)
================================================================

Edward Tufte, the most influential thinker on information design,
established principles we will follow:

1. MAXIMIZE DATA-INK RATIO
   Every mark on the page should carry information. Decorative
   ink is suspect. The ratio of "ink that teaches" to "ink that
   decorates" should be as high as possible.

2. NO CHARTJUNK
   No boxes around things just because they need a box. No 3D
   effects. No gradients. No drop shadows. No decorative
   borders. Every visual element must justify its presence.

3. GRAPHICAL INTEGRITY
   Visual representations must match the underlying reality.
   If something is bigger in the diagram, it should be bigger
   in the concept. If two things are connected, they really
   are connected.

4. MICRO/MACRO READING
   Good information design works at two scales simultaneously.
   You see the overall pattern at a glance. You can zoom into
   any detail and it still makes sense. Most popular AI
   diagrams fail this test. They give you the macro or the
   micro, never both.

5. LAYERING AND SEPARATION
   When showing multiple things at once, layer them visually.
   The most important layer is most prominent. Supporting
   information is subordinated. This is how complexity becomes
   navigable.

6. SMALL MULTIPLES
   When showing variations, show them as a series of small
   comparable diagrams rather than one large diagram with
   variations marked inside it. This is one of Tufte's most
   powerful insights.

7. NARRATIVE
   Diagrams that tell a story work better than static
   snapshots. Show before and after. Show the sequence of
   transformation. Move the eye through a path of meaning.


================================================================
THE ILLUSTRATED TRANSFORMER MODEL (Alammar's approach)
================================================================

Jay Alammar's "The Illustrated Transformer" is the most successful
visual explanation of how AI models work that has ever been
produced for a general audience. What he does that works:

1. PROGRESSIVE DISCLOSURE
   He never shows everything at once. He starts with the simplest
   possible picture, then adds one element at a time. By the end,
   you understand the full complexity. But you got there by
   understanding each layer first.

2. CONCRETE EXAMPLES BEFORE ABSTRACT NOTATION
   Before he shows a formula, he shows what the formula does on
   a specific example. The example becomes the mental model. The
   formula is just the notation for that mental model.

3. THE SAME EXAMPLE THROUGHOUT
   He uses one sentence throughout the entire essay. The reader
   does not need to re-orient with each new diagram. The
   continuity carries them through.

4. CONSISTENT VISUAL LANGUAGE
   Every "token" is a small box. Every "vector" is a row of cells.
   Every "attention weight" is a thin line. The reader learns the
   visual vocabulary in the first diagram and reuses it through
   all subsequent diagrams.

5. SPATIAL METAPHORS THAT MATCH THE CONCEPT
   When information "flows" through the network, his diagrams
   literally show arrows of flow. When two things are being
   compared, they are placed side by side. When something is
   stacked, the diagram is stacked. The visual structure mirrors
   the conceptual structure.

6. NO UNNECESSARY ABSTRACTION
   He never uses an abstraction where a concrete picture would
   work. When he could draw a matrix, he draws labeled cells
   instead. When he could draw a function, he draws a process
   diagram. Abstractions only enter when the concrete pictures
   become too cluttered.


================================================================
THE THREE TYPES OF VISUALS THIS BOOK NEEDS
================================================================

Based on the research, this book actually needs THREE distinct
visual categories, each with its own discipline.


----------------------------------------------------------------
TYPE 1: CONCEPT-TEACHING DIAGRAMS
----------------------------------------------------------------

These are the workhorses. They appear inside chapters at moments
when the prose has set up an idea and the reader is ready to see
it visualized. They follow the Alammar progressive-disclosure
model.

Examples we need in this book:

  TOKENIZATION (Chapter 3)
  Show one sentence being broken into tokens. Each token in a
  small box. Each character that joined into a token highlighted.
  No more. The reader sees: "Oh, this is how words become
  pieces."

  VECTOR SPACE / WORD EMBEDDING (Chapter 3)
  Show a 2D projection of word positions. "King" near "queen."
  "Doctor" near "hospital." The arithmetic of king - man + woman
  = queen drawn as actual arrows on the map. The reader sees:
  "Oh, this is what 'meaning as geography' actually looks like."

  ATTENTION (Chapter 1 and Chapter 3)
  The cocktail party metaphor as a literal diagram. One figure
  at the center. Six conversations around them. Lines of varying
  thickness connecting the figure to each conversation,
  representing attention weight. The reader sees: "Oh, this is
  what 'attention' means as a mechanism."

  THE TWO TRAININGS (Chapter 4)
  A simple before-and-after. Left side: a chaotic cloud of text
  becoming a "raw word predictor." Right side: human feedback
  ratings shaping that predictor into a tuned assistant. The
  reader sees: "Oh, the model learns twice, in two different
  ways."

  LAYERS OF SAFETY (Chapter 16)
  Concentric circles or stacked filters, each labeled with one
  of the seven safety layers. Some have visible gaps. The reader
  sees: "Oh, safety is not a wall, it is overlapping filters."

  THE REGULATORY APPROACHES (Chapter 17, optional)
  Three small panels side by side. EU as a structured pyramid.
  US as a patchwork of squares. China as a centralized hub.
  Small multiples in the Tufte sense. The reader sees: "Oh,
  countries are taking different approaches."

  THREE TRAJECTORIES (Chapter 21)
  A simple branching diagram. Single path becomes three at a
  fork. Each labeled. The reader sees: "Oh, the future is
  uncertain in this specific way."

Style discipline for Type 1:
- Black ink lines on cream background. Dusty blue for technical
  layers. Terracotta only for emphasis.
- 0.75pt to 1pt strokes. Consistent throughout.
- Labels in EB Garamond small caps for parts. Inter for measurements.
- White space inside and around. Generous margins.
- Use the same example sentence wherever possible across diagrams.
- Show transformation. Show flow. Show comparison. Avoid static
  snapshots.

Roughly 8-10 of these in the whole book. Each one earned by the
prose around it. Each one teaching something the prose cannot
teach as efficiently in words alone.


----------------------------------------------------------------
TYPE 2: METAPHOR ILLUSTRATIONS (chapter openers)
----------------------------------------------------------------

These are different. They are not teaching the mechanism of AI.
They are illustrating the metaphor that the chapter uses to
frame its argument. They give the reader a visual anchor for
what the chapter is about, before they start reading it.

This is what Direction 4 (illustrative-metaphorical) was always
meant to be. But now we are clearer about its purpose. The
chapter opener is the visual equivalent of a chapter title. It
sets up the metaphor.

Examples reconsidered:

  CHAPTER 1 (Just Predicting Words)
  The cocktail party scene, but treated as a metaphor opener,
  not a technical diagram. One figure leaning slightly toward
  one conversation. Other conversations visible as text bubbles.
  The visual itself does no teaching. It just plants the
  metaphor.

  CHAPTER 12 (How They Fail)
  A compass pointing confidently in the wrong direction. Single
  image. No labels. The visual says: "confident but wrong" before
  the chapter does.

  CHAPTER 17 (Regulation)
  A patchwork quilt, with stitching visible between very
  different fabric squares. One image. The reader picks up:
  "different approaches being sewn together imperfectly."

Style discipline for Type 2:
- Terracotta and ink on cream. Limited palette.
- Hand-drawn feel preferred. Slight wobble in lines. Not
  machine-perfect.
- Asymmetric composition. Single focal point.
- No labels. The image speaks alone, supported by the chapter
  title beside or below it.
- Generous white space. The image breathes.

These are quieter than Type 1. They do not teach a mechanism.
They establish a frame.

21 of these total, one per chapter.


----------------------------------------------------------------
TYPE 3: PART DIVIDERS (the lowest priority)
----------------------------------------------------------------

These were the visual category I started with, which was a
mistake. Part dividers are the LEAST important visual category
in this book. They appear five times. They exist mostly to give
the reader a pause between sections.

In hindsight, they should be the simplest possible visual move.
Just typography. A part number. A part title. A short epigraph
maybe. No illustration at all.

This frees up budget and attention for Types 1 and 2 where it
actually matters.

Style for Type 3:
- Pure typography on cream background.
- Part number in Fraunces, very large.
- Part title in Fraunces, large.
- Optional one-line epigraph in italic.
- That is the entire visual.

5 of these total.

================================================================
THE EXAMPLE-DRIVEN APPROACH
================================================================

One critical Alammar insight that should govern all Type 1
diagrams: use the same example throughout the book.

Right now, different chapters reach for different examples. We
should pick ONE example sentence and reuse it in every concept
diagram. This builds reader familiarity and reduces cognitive
load.

Candidates:

  "The cat sat on the mat."
  Too simple. Does not have enough relationships to demonstrate
  attention well.

  "I want to text my ex."
  Already in Chapter 4 as the example for system-prompt
  handling. Slightly informal but distinctive. Has emotional
  loading that makes it memorable.

  "Build me a website that sells handmade ceramics."
  More realistic of how people use AI. Has multiple semantic
  parts that demonstrate attention. Might be too long.

  "The doctor put on her coat and walked into the hospital."
  Used in Chapter 3 already for vector-space demonstration
  (doctor/hospital cluster). Has a pronoun reference that
  attention can resolve. Has enough words to demonstrate
  tokenization meaningfully.

RECOMMENDATION: Use "The doctor put on her coat and walked into
the hospital" as the through-line example for all Type 1
diagrams. It is already in the manuscript. It demonstrates
multiple AI concepts. It has enough complexity to be useful and
not so much that it is overwhelming.


================================================================
PROGRESSIVE DISCLOSURE FOR THE TECHNICAL DIAGRAMS
================================================================

Following Alammar, the technical diagrams in Chapter 3 should
build progressively. Each diagram shows ONE new thing on top of
what the reader has already understood.

DIAGRAM 1: Tokenization
  Show: "The doctor put on her coat and walked into the
  hospital" split into 10 tokens. Each token in its own small
  box, in a row.
  Teaches: words become tokens.

DIAGRAM 2: Vector embedding
  Show: the same 10 tokens, but now each token has a small array
  of numbers next to it. The arrays are simplified to 4 cells
  each (the actual vectors are much longer but 4 communicates
  the idea).
  Teaches: tokens become vectors. The numbers ARE the meaning.

DIAGRAM 3: Vectors as geography
  Show: a 2D map. The 10 tokens placed as dots on the map. "The"
  and "and" near each other in one corner. "Doctor" and
  "hospital" near each other in another. Distance shows
  relationship.
  Teaches: vectors are positions in meaning-space.

DIAGRAM 4: Attention
  Show: the same sentence. One token highlighted ("her"). Lines
  of varying thickness from "her" to every other token. Thickest
  line goes to "doctor" because that is what "her" refers to.
  Teaches: attention is the model choosing which other words to
  listen to.

The same sentence carries through all four diagrams. The reader
never has to re-orient. They just see the layers being added.


================================================================
WHAT THIS MEANS FOR THE FIRST ARTWORK TO PRODUCE
================================================================

The Part One divider I produced earlier should be scrapped.

Instead, the right first artwork to produce is a TYPE 1 DIAGRAM
that tests the visual language we will use throughout the book.

I recommend: TOKENIZATION DIAGRAM for Chapter 3.

Why this is the right first test:
1. It is the simplest of the Type 1 diagrams.
2. It establishes the visual vocabulary (tokens as small boxes)
   that will be reused throughout.
3. It introduces the through-line example sentence.
4. If we get this right, the rest of the chapter 3 sequence
   follows naturally.
5. If we get it wrong, we learn the corrections cheaply.

After tokenization works, we build vector embedding on top of it
using the same tokens. Then we add the map. Then attention. By
the end, we have all four chapter 3 diagrams forming a coherent
sequence.

THEN we revisit the chapter-opener metaphors with what we have
learned about the visual language. THEN we do the part dividers
last, as pure typography, because they are the simplest.


================================================================
PRINCIPLES TO TEST EVERY VISUAL AGAINST
================================================================

Before any visual goes into the book, it must pass these tests:

THE TUFTE TEST
   Is every mark on the page carrying information?
   Could I remove anything without losing meaning?
   Does the visual hierarchy match the conceptual hierarchy?

THE ALAMMAR TEST
   Does this visual teach one thing the reader did not know
   before?
   Could a reader explain the concept by looking at this image?
   Does it use the same visual vocabulary as adjacent diagrams?

THE URBAN TEST (Wait But Why)
   Could I draw this on a napkin for a friend?
   Is it warmer than a textbook but smarter than a meme?

THE CHAPTER-PROSE TEST
   Does this visual come at the exact moment the reader needs
   it, after the prose has set it up?
   Would the reader pause, then turn the page, and feel
   confirmed?

If a visual fails any test, redesign it or cut it.

WHAT TO READ AND STUDY BEFORE PRODUCING THE FIRST VISUAL
================================================================

Three references that should shape what we produce:

1. Jay Alammar, "The Illustrated Transformer"
   https://jalammar.github.io/illustrated-transformer/
   The gold standard for visualizing this exact subject matter
   for a general audience.

2. Edward Tufte, "Envisioning Information"
   The principles, in their pure form. Chapter on small
   multiples is particularly relevant.

3. Bret Victor, "Up and Down the Ladder of Abstraction"
   http://worrydream.com/LadderOfAbstraction/
   On how to make abstract concepts navigable through visual
   progressive disclosure.
