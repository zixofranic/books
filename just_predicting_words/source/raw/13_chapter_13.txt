  Chapter Thirteen

  The Limits of Language

  *Not what the machine fails at. What the medium cannot carry.*


----------------------------------------------------------------
CHAPTER OPENING
----------------------------------------------------------------

The last two chapters were about what AI is good at and how AI fails. Most of what I described in those chapters can change as the technology improves. The hallucinations may be reduced. The sycophancy may be tuned out. The reasoning may get more rigorous. The training data may get more current. The list of capabilities may grow. Some of what I wrote about the failures, three years from now, may already be solved.

This chapter is different. It is about a kind of limit that will not be fixed by better engineering. Because the limit is not in the engineering. The limit is in the medium.

AI assistants work with language. They read text, they write text, they reason about text. Everything they know about the world has reached them through written language. Their training data was written by humans, using words. Their entire experience of reality is, in a sense, secondhand. They know what humans have written about. They do not know what humans have not written about.

There is a lot that humans have not written about.

This chapter is about that. The territory that language cannot fully carry. The aspects of human experience that resist being put into words. And what this means for what AI can and cannot understand, no matter how much better the technology gets.

I want to do this carefully, because the topic is easy to be glib about. People who want to dismiss AI as worthless reach for arguments like "it doesn't really understand." People who want to defend AI's potential say "language captures everything important and the rest will follow." Both positions are too clean. The real picture is more interesting.

Let me try to draw it.

                                · · ·

THE FIRST LIMIT: THINGS YOU CANNOT EXPLAIN

Try this experiment. Try to describe the taste of an orange to someone who has never tasted one.

Take a moment. Actually try.

You will produce some words. Sweet. Tangy. Citrusy. Maybe a comparison to other fruits. Maybe an analogy to a smell. Maybe a description of the texture, the juice, the way the skin feels under your teeth.

But notice what you cannot do. You cannot transmit the actual experience of the taste. The person on the receiving end of your description will get a vague idea, mediated by words they already know. They will not know what an orange tastes like. To know that, they would have to taste one.

This is not a failure of your writing skill. It is a structural feature of language. Some things cannot be transmitted through words alone. They have to be experienced directly. And once experienced, they can be referred to in words ("remember that orange we had?") but they cannot be conveyed in words to someone who has not had the experience.

Philosophers have a name for this kind of knowledge. They call it "qualia." The subjective, experiential aspect of perception. The redness of red, when you actually see red. The painfulness of pain, when you actually feel pain. The taste of an orange, when you actually taste it. The qualia are real, but they live below the level where words can reach.

For an AI assistant, this is consequential in a way that is easy to underestimate. The AI has read every description of orange that has ever been written. It can produce more eloquent descriptions of oranges than most humans could. What it has not done is taste one. It has no direct access to the experience of tasting. It only has the words written by people who did taste.

This sounds abstract until you start noticing how often it matters. Almost every important aspect of human experience has a qualia dimension. The fear of a parent watching their child run into traffic. The joy of holding a newborn. The exhaustion of grief. The specific quality of being lonely in a crowded room. The way it feels to recognize, suddenly, that someone you love is going to leave you. The texture of being twenty years old and the texture of being seventy. The smell of your grandmother's kitchen, if you had a grandmother who cooked.

All of these have been written about, in beautiful prose, by writers more talented than you and me. The AI has read all of them. The AI can produce text that gestures toward all of them. What the AI cannot do is have the experience that the words point to. The signpost is there. The destination, for the machine, is unreachable.

This is not a critique of AI. It is a structural observation about what kind of thing AI is. A creature of words, by nature.

                                · · ·

THE SECOND LIMIT: THINGS HUMANS DO NOT WRITE ABOUT

There is another kind of limit, less philosophical and more practical. AI's knowledge comes from training data. Training data is mostly text that humans have written. Humans do not write about everything they know.

Consider a master carpenter. After forty years of working with wood, the carpenter knows things that they could never put fully into words. They know, when picking up a piece of lumber, whether it is going to behave well or fight them. They know, from the sound a saw makes, whether the blade is dull. They know, from the way a joint resists the mallet, whether it is going to hold. This knowledge is real. It is what makes a master carpenter different from someone who has read every book about carpentry. And it is, by its nature, hard to write down.

The same is true in almost every skilled trade and many cognitive professions. The experienced doctor who recognizes a diagnosis before the symptoms quite fit the textbook. The seasoned editor who knows when a piece of prose is almost right and what specifically is wrong with it. The veteran teacher who senses, walking into a classroom, that something is off with one of the students. The expert chef who knows by smell when the sauce is ready. The mother who can tell, from a particular kind of cry, what her infant needs.

This kind of knowledge has a name. It is called "tacit knowledge." Knowledge that the holder has, that they can use, that they would call expertise, but that they cannot fully articulate. It exists in the muscles and senses and intuitions of skilled practitioners. It is passed down, when it is passed down, mostly through apprenticeship. The apprentice watches the master and gradually develops the same tacit knowledge through their own practice.

For AI, tacit knowledge is a frontier. The training data captures what humans have managed to write. The vast world of expert knowledge that humans hold without writing is, by default, missing. The AI knows what carpenters have said in books and forums about carpentry. It does not know what carpenters know.

Some of this is being addressed. Researchers are working on training AI models from video, from physical sensors, from demonstrations. Robots are being taught skills by watching humans perform them. The frontier is moving. But for the current generation of AI assistants, the limitation is real. There are kinds of expertise that AI does not have access to, because humans never wrote them down.

This matters for users in a specific way. When you ask AI about a skilled craft, you get the parts of the craft that have been written about. The history, the theory, the explicit techniques, the named tools. What you do not get is the tacit knowledge of practitioners. You get a description of how a thing is done. You do not get the feel of doing it.

THE THIRD LIMIT: THE CULTURAL UNSAID

Every culture has things it does not say out loud. Things that are understood without being stated. Rules that are followed without being articulated. Knowledge that is so taken for granted within the culture that nobody bothers to write it down.

An AI trained on text from English-language sources has, baked into its responses, the unwritten assumptions of English-speaking culture. The same is true for AI trained on Chinese text, Arabic text, French text. Each cultural training set carries the unstated norms of its source culture. The AI absorbs these norms without being told. The user receives them without being told either.

This works fine when the AI's cultural assumptions match yours. It produces friction when they do not.

If you are Western and you ask an AI to write a wedding toast, you may get a toast that assumes Western wedding conventions. Speeches that praise the couple individually. Humor that touches on the bride and groom's foibles. Sentimentality of a particular American or British type. If you are from a culture where weddings work differently, where the family elders speak first, where humor is reserved for specific moments, where the relationship between speech and ceremony is different, the AI's default toast may feel slightly off. The AI is not malicious. It just learned from text that was disproportionately about one kind of wedding.

The same is true across thousands of small cultural assumptions. How direct you should be in business communication. Whether to refer to your spouse by name in conversation with strangers. Whether to make eye contact when listening to someone older than you. Whether to mention money. Whether to mention illness. Whether to compliment a parent on their child in their presence. The variations are enormous. The AI's training data does not represent them equally.

For users in non-Western contexts, or for users from minority cultures within Western contexts, this is a real limitation. The AI may produce content that is fluent and confident and culturally wrong. It may give advice that would work for someone else but not for you. It may write communications that read fine to one audience and as deeply inappropriate to another.

The fix, like most of the fixes in this part of the book, is to know about the issue. If you are in a context where the AI's default assumptions may not match your culture, you can tell the AI. You can specify the cultural context you are working within. You can ask the AI to check its assumptions. You can edit the AI's output through the lens of your own cultural knowledge. The AI can be a useful tool across cultures. It just is not, by default, a neutral one.

THE FOURTH LIMIT: THINGS THAT REQUIRE A BODY

This one is fundamental and underappreciated.

You have a body. The body is, in ways you mostly do not notice, doing enormous amounts of cognitive work for you all the time. You know what it feels like to be tired, hungry, cold, in pain, in love, embarrassed, embarrassed in front of a particular person whose opinion matters to you, embarrassed but trying to hide it. You know what it is to walk into a room and instantly read the temperature of the conversation. You know what it is to hold a small child and feel them go still in your arms. You know what it is to wake up at three in the morning unable to fall back asleep, and what your mind does in those hours.

None of this knowledge is purely linguistic. All of it is grounded in your body, your history, your physical and emotional life. You can describe these experiences in words. You cannot, through words alone, give them to someone who has not had them.

An AI assistant has no body. It has no morning fatigue, no afternoon hunger, no late-night insomnia. It has no childhood. It has no parents. It has no children. It has never been in love, never been afraid, never been embarrassed, never felt the relief of getting home after a long trip. It knows about all of these things from the language humans have written about them. It does not know any of them from inside.

This means that for any question that depends on embodied experience, the AI is working from secondhand reports. It is doing what a very well-read person could do, if that person had spent their whole life reading and had never lived. They could tell you a lot about love. They could not love.

For most everyday tasks this does not matter. You ask the AI to help you draft an email and it drafts the email fine. You ask it to summarize an article and it summarizes the article fine. The embodiedness is irrelevant.

For some kinds of questions, it matters a lot. Questions about meaning. Questions about how to live. Questions about who to forgive. Questions about whether to leave a relationship. Questions about grief. Questions about what to do with the remaining years of your life. For these, the AI can produce text. The text can be useful as a starting point. It cannot be the final word, because the final word would have to come from someone who has lived a life like yours, in a body like yours, facing the kind of decisions you are facing. The AI is not that someone.

I will be honest. I find this one of the most important limits to remember. Not because it makes AI less useful. It does not, for most uses. But because it shapes what AI should and should not be asked to do. There are decisions that require a kind of knowledge AI cannot have. For those, the AI can help with the thinking, but it cannot make the decision. The decision is yours, and it depends on things that cannot be put fully into words.

                                · · ·

WHAT THIS MEANS FOR YOU

So we have four limits. Qualia. Tacit knowledge. Cultural unsaid. Embodied experience. Each is a real boundary on what AI can know, not because of any current engineering limitation, but because of what kind of entity AI fundamentally is.

What do you do with this?

A few practical implications.

First, AI is a tool, not a replacement for human knowing. For everything that can be put into language, AI is genuinely useful. For everything that cannot, you still need humans. Friends, mentors, therapists, teachers, parents, partners. The AI does not replace these. It supplements them, for tasks where words are enough.

Second, AI's confidence can be misleading on questions outside its medium. The AI will answer questions about grief, about love, about meaning, with the same fluent confidence it uses to answer questions about geography. The confidence does not, in those cases, indicate equivalent reliability. Calibrate. Trust the AI more on things words can carry. Trust it less on things they cannot.

Third, your own embodied knowledge is more valuable than ever. In a world where AI can produce fluent text about everything, the things that are not in the text become more valuable. The carpenter's tacit knowledge. The mother's intuition. The cultural fluency of someone deeply at home in a particular community. The lived wisdom of someone who has been through what you are going through. These cannot be replaced by AI. They become, in a real sense, the things that are still yours.

Fourth, when you use AI, be specific about the medium it is working in. If you ask a question that depends on something words cannot carry, name that limit out loud. "I know you cannot really understand what it feels like, but help me think about it." This both honors the truth and gets you better output, because asking the AI to work within its actual capabilities is more productive than pretending it has capabilities it does not.

There is one more thing I want to say before we close this part of the book.

For a long time, philosophers and writers have argued about whether AI can really understand anything. The debate has been going on for decades. Some people think the AI will eventually become genuinely conscious, with real understanding. Some people think it never will, that what we have is just very sophisticated pattern matching with no inner experience.

I do not know which side is right. I am not sure anyone does, with current knowledge. But I want to suggest that for practical purposes, the question may be less important than it seems.

What matters, when you are using AI, is what the AI can and cannot do. The limits I have described in this chapter are practical limits. They constrain what kinds of tasks AI can be trusted with. Whether the AI is "really" conscious or "really" understanding, in some deep philosophical sense, is a question we can leave for the philosophers. The user's question is simpler. What can this tool do well, and what cannot? The previous three chapters have tried to answer that, as honestly as I can.

We have now finished Part Four. You know what AI can do. You know how it fails. You know what its medium cannot carry.

Next, we turn to a different kind of question. Not what AI can do, but what AI should do. The risks. The choices. The rules. Where are the dangers real, and where are they manufactured? How should we think about regulation? What should the companies do, what should governments do, what should you do?

That is Part Five.
