CHAPTER OPENING
----------------------------------------------------------------

If you've gotten this far in the book, you already know the punchline. The machine predicts the next word.

This chapter is about what's actually happening when it does that.

I want to say something up front, because the next few thousand words are the most technical part of this whole book, and I don't want you to feel intimidated. There is no math in this chapter. There are no equations. You do not need to know anything about computer programming. What I'm going to do is take the three central ideas that make modern AI work, and explain each one of them with something from your everyday life. By the end of this chapter, you'll have a real mental picture of what's happening inside ChatGPT when you press send. You'll know the words tokens, vectors, and attention, and you'll know what they mean.

If you ever need to skip a section, the bottom of this chapter has a summary that pulls everything together. But I think you'll find this is less hard than you expect. The math is unfamiliar. The intuitions, you already have.

Let's go.

THE FIRST IDEA: TOKENS

Before the machine can predict the next word, it has to deal with a problem you and I never have to think about. The problem is this: a machine cannot read words.

I know that sounds strange. ChatGPT obviously reads words. You type "What is the capital of France?" and it understands you. So how can I say it doesn't read words?

The honest answer is that it doesn't read them the way you do. You see the letters c, a, t, and your brain assembles them into the concept of a small furry animal that meows. The machine doesn't do that. The machine works with numbers. Everything inside a computer, ultimately, is numbers. So before the machine can do anything with your sentence, your sentence has to be converted into numbers.

The conversion happens in two steps. The first step is called tokenization, and it's where words get broken into pieces.

Try this. I want you to count, in your head, how many pieces the word "unbelievable" should be broken into.

Pause for a second and actually do it.

You probably said one, because to you it's one word. Or maybe you said three, because you saw "un," "believ," and "able" as natural parts. Or five, because you counted syllables.

For the machine, the answer is usually three. It breaks "unbelievable" into "un," "believ," and "able." Those three chunks are what the machine sees. Each one is called a token.

Why does the machine do this? Why not just keep "unbelievable" as one piece?

Because there are too many words. The English language has somewhere around a million distinct words, depending on how you count. Most other languages have similar numbers. If the machine treated every word as a separate entity, it would have to learn a million separate things, and most of those things would only appear a handful of times during its training. Rare words would be poorly understood. New words would be impossible to handle. Names of people the machine had never seen would just confuse it.

But if the machine breaks words into smaller pieces, magic happens. The word "running" becomes "run" plus "ning." The word "runner" becomes "run" plus "ner." The word "runs" becomes "run" plus "s." Now the machine has learned one core piece, "run," that shows up in three different words. It knows much more about "run" than it would have if each of those three words were treated separately. And when it encounters "rerunning" for the first time, it can break that into "re" plus "run" plus "ning" and figure out, from the pieces it already knows, roughly what it means.

This is the same trick humans use. You've never seen the word "unbookishness," but if you saw it in a sentence, you'd figure out what it meant. Not bookish. The state of being not bookish. You did this by breaking the word into pieces you already knew. Un. Book. Ish. Ness. Same trick. Different scale.

Once a word has been broken into tokens, each token gets a number. There's a giant lookup table inside every language model that says "the token 'the' is number 1023, the token 'cat' is number 4519, the token 'on' is number 327," and so on. Tens of thousands of these.

So when you type "The cat sat on the mat" into ChatGPT, what the machine actually sees, internally, is something like 1023, 4519, 6201, 327, 1023, 8842. A sequence of numbers. From that point on, everything the machine does is math on numbers, not English on words.

This is going to matter later. There are things the machine does strangely because of this. If you ever ask a language model to count the letters in the word "strawberry," it often gets the wrong answer. Why? Because it didn't read "strawberry" as ten individual letters. It read it as two tokens, "straw" and "berry." Asking it to count letters is like asking a chef to tell you how many flour molecules are in a cake. It just doesn't work at that level.

That's tokenization. Words come in. Pieces of words come out. Each piece gets a number. Now the machine has something it can do math on.

THE SECOND IDEA: VECTORS

Now we have a number for each token. But a number on its own doesn't mean anything. The number 4519 doesn't tell you "cat." It's just an index, like a parking spot number. To do anything useful, the machine needs to know what that token *means.*

Here's where things get interesting.

I'm going to ask you to do another thing. Imagine, in your head, a giant map of a city. Not a real city, an imaginary one. Anywhere in this city you put a building, the building's location says something about what it is. If you put a hospital somewhere, then doctors' offices, pharmacies, and ambulance stations would naturally cluster near it. If you put a bakery somewhere else, then cafes, coffee shops, and patisseries would cluster near *it*. The city's geography would encode meaning. Buildings near each other on the map would be similar in what they did.

Now imagine this same map, but for words.

Some neighborhood of the map contains "doctor," "nurse," "hospital," "patient," "medicine," and "ambulance." Those words live close to each other because they belong to the same world. A different neighborhood contains "kitchen," "stove," "recipe," "chef," and "salt." That's the cooking neighborhood. Somewhere else is the music neighborhood, with "guitar," "piano," "concert," and "melody." And so on, for every word in the language.

This is, roughly, what a language model has inside it. Every token gets converted, not just to a number, but to a *position on a map.* The position is what tells the machine what the token means.

In practice, the map isn't a flat city. It's a space with hundreds or thousands of dimensions, which I know sounds insane, but it's the same idea scaled up. A position in this space is described by a long list of numbers, not just two coordinates. Each number captures some aspect of the word's meaning. The list is called a vector. The whole space is called an embedding space.

You don't need to picture a thousand dimensions. Almost nobody can. Just hold onto the city map idea. Words live near other words that are similar to them. That's the whole concept.

What's remarkable about this is that the machine wasn't told where to put each word. It figured out the geography on its own, during training, just by looking at billions of sentences and noticing which words tended to appear near which other words. The word "doctor" appeared near "hospital" so often that the system put them on the same block of the map. The word "guitar" appeared near "concert" enough that they ended up in the same neighborhood.

This has some genuinely beautiful consequences. We saw one of them in the last chapter, the word2vec discovery that king minus man plus woman equals queen. Now you can see why that works. On the map, "king" and "queen" sit in roughly parallel positions, the way "man" and "woman" do. The relationship between man and woman is a particular direction on the map. If you move in that same direction from "king," you arrive at "queen." It's not magic. It's geography.

The same thing happens with countries and capitals. Paris and France sit in one relationship. Berlin and Germany sit in the same relationship. So if you take Paris, subtract France, and add Germany, you arrive at Berlin. The system has encoded "capital of" as a direction on its map, without anyone telling it to.

This idea is, in some ways, the deepest one in this whole book. It's the bridge between what the machine does mechanically (math on numbers) and what we feel it does (work with meaning). The numbers in the vectors are not arbitrary. They encode a geography of meaning, learned from us, from our writing, from billions of sentences that captured, between the lines, how we humans actually think about words.

When I said in chapter one that the machine is a kind of mirror held up to humanity's writing, the vectors are part of what I meant. Those vectors are us. They're the shape of how we use language, captured as positions on an unimaginably large map.

THE THIRD IDEA: ATTENTION

So far we have tokens (pieces of words with numbers) and vectors (positions on a meaning map). Now we need the third idea, which is the one that made modern AI possible.

I want you to think about a sentence you've already seen in this book. It was the one I used in chapter two:

   *The trophy did not fit in the suitcase because it was too big.*

What does "it" refer to in this sentence?

You said "the trophy." Of course you did. Now let me ask you a harder question. *How did you know?*

Take a moment with this. Try to articulate, in words, what your brain just did.

Here's what you probably came up with. You looked at the word "it." You noticed that "it" needed to refer to something earlier in the sentence. You considered the two candidates, the trophy and the suitcase. Then you noticed the word "big" at the end of the sentence. You ran a tiny piece of logic: trophies that don't fit in suitcases are too big. Suitcases that can't hold trophies are too small. So "big" implies "trophy." Therefore "it" is the trophy.

That whole process happened in something like a quarter of a second. But notice what it required. You had to *look back* at the sentence. You had to figure out which words in the sentence mattered for understanding "it." You had to pay more attention to "trophy" and "big" than to "in" or "the" or "fit." You weighted some words more than others, based on what they could tell you about the puzzle in front of you.

That is attention.

This is the core trick of the Transformer. When the machine is trying to understand any token in a sentence, it doesn't just look at that token in isolation. It looks at every other token in the sentence and decides which ones matter. It pays *more attention* to relevant tokens and *less attention* to irrelevant ones. Then it builds up its understanding of the original token based on that weighted view.

Let's run this for "it" in the trophy sentence.

The machine sees the token "it." It needs to figure out what "it" represents. So it looks at every other token in the sentence and asks, in effect: how much does each of you help me understand "it"?

The token "trophy" raises its hand high. So does "suitcase." Both could be candidates for "it." The token "fit" raises its hand because the question is about fitting. The token "big" raises its hand because that's the clue. The token "too" raises a small hand because it modifies "big." Tokens like "the" and "in" don't raise their hands at all, because they're filler. The machine weights all of these contributions and produces an updated understanding of "it" that is now, in the machine's internal map, very close to "trophy."

This process happens for every token in the sentence, all at once, in parallel. Every token looks at every other token and decides who to listen to. The result is that the machine ends up with a rich, context-aware understanding of what every word in the sentence is doing, given the company it's keeping.

Now do you remember the cocktail party from chapter one?

You're at a party. Six conversations are happening at once. You focus on the person in front of you and tune out everything else, but when someone three tables away says your name, you hear it instantly. Your brain is paying weighted attention to all the inputs around you, listening *more* to some things, *less* to others, based on relevance.

This is exactly what the Transformer does inside a sentence. Every word is "at the party," surrounded by every other word, and each word decides which of its neighbors to actually listen to. The machine learned, during training, that words like "big" carry signal about what pronouns refer to. It learned that articles like "the" mostly don't. It learned thousands of subtle rules of this kind, not from anyone teaching them, but from reading billions of sentences and noticing which words tended to inform which other words.

When you read the 2017 paper title from the last chapter, "Attention Is All You Need," now you can hear it differently. The authors weren't being cute. They were making a real claim. They had built a system that, by paying attention well enough, no longer needed any of the other complicated machinery that earlier systems had used. Attention alone, applied at scale, did the job.

It still does the job. Every assistant you've ever used does this trick, billions of times a day.

PUTTING IT TOGETHER

So now let's run a full example, with everything in place.

You type into ChatGPT: "What is the capital of France?"

Step one. The sentence gets tokenized. It becomes a sequence of tokens: what, is, the, capital, of, France, question mark. Each token gets a number from the lookup table.

Step two. Each token gets converted to a vector, a position on the meaning map. "France" lands somewhere near "country," "Europe," "Paris," and "wine." "Capital" lands somewhere near "country," "city," "government," and "currency." Already, before any other processing, the machine has a rough sense of the neighborhoods involved.

Step three. The attention mechanism kicks in. Every token looks at every other token in the sentence and figures out which ones matter. The machine notices that "capital" and "France" are the load-bearing words. It notices that they're linked by "of," which signals a relationship. It builds up a context-aware understanding of the whole sentence, weighted toward the parts that carry meaning.

Step four. This process happens not once but in many layers. Each layer takes the understanding from the previous layer and refines it. By the time the input has passed through all the layers (often dozens of them, sometimes more than a hundred), the machine has built up a rich representation of what you're asking.

Step five. The machine produces a probability distribution over all possible next tokens. Given everything it now understands about your question, what is the most likely next token in a response? "Paris" gets a very high probability. "The" gets a high probability. "Berlin" gets a low probability. "Banana" gets essentially zero.

Step six. The machine picks one of the high-probability tokens (usually with some randomness, which is why responses vary). It adds that token to the input and runs the whole process again to pick the next token. And the next. And the next. Until the response is complete.

That's the whole thing. Tokens, vectors, attention, repeated through many layers, applied at enormous scale.

It is, as I keep saying, almost embarrassingly simple as an idea. The reason it produces such complex behavior is that the simple idea, run on billions of parameters and trained on most of the internet, accumulates a kind of compressed understanding of human language that no individual human could carry in their head.

The mirror, again.

WHAT YOU NOW KNOW

If you've followed me this far, you now know how a language model actually works. Not the math. The intuitions. Let me summarize, so you can carry the picture forward.

Tokenization breaks your words into smaller pieces, because there are too many possible words for the machine to handle as wholes. The pieces become numbers, because computers can only do math on numbers.

Vectors give each token a position on a high-dimensional map of meaning. Words that are similar in meaning live near each other on the map. The map was learned, not designed, from billions of sentences.

Attention is the trick that lets the machine understand each token in context, by paying weighted attention to the other tokens around it. This is what makes the difference between "I see" meaning "I observe" and "I see" meaning "I understand," based on which words follow.

These three ideas, stacked up in many layers and trained at scale, are most of what modern AI is.

You can now read almost any popular article about large language models and follow what it's saying. You can hold your own in any conversation about how ChatGPT works. You won't be able to write the code, but you'll understand what the code is doing, and you'll be able to ask sharper questions when something the machine does surprises you.

That's a real thing to know.
