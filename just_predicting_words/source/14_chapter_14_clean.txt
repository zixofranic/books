  Chapter Fourteen

  The Risks That Are Real

  Forget the science fiction. The serious dangers are already in the room.

When most people think about AI risk, they think about science fiction. The robots become self-aware. The AI decides humans are inconvenient. Skynet. The Matrix. The familiar scenarios in which a superintelligent machine becomes hostile to its creators and tries to wipe them out.

I want to be honest about my view of these scenarios. They are not impossible. Serious researchers worry about them. The concern is not silly. We will discuss the speculative risks in the next chapter, and I will give them a fair treatment.

But for now, I want to talk about the risks that are already here. The ones that have already harmed people. The ones that are likely to harm more people in the next few years, regardless of what happens at the speculative end of the spectrum.

The reason to separate these out is that the two categories require different responses. The science fiction risks may or may not happen. We can debate the probability. The risks I am about to describe have already happened, multiple times, in well-documented cases. They are not waiting on the future. They are here now.

If you only have time to worry about one set of AI risks, worry about these.

The first real risk. synthetic media that fools people.

The technology to generate convincing fake video, fake audio, and fake images using AI has crossed a threshold in 2026.

We have been able to fake images for many years. Photoshop is older than most of the people reading this book. What changed with modern AI is the ease, the realism, and the accessibility. You no longer need a skilled editor and hours of work to produce a convincing fake. You need a smartphone app and a few seconds.

The World Economic Forum's 2026 Global Risks Report puts misinformation and disinformation as the second-largest short-term global risk, behind only geoeconomic confrontation. The 2026 International AI Safety Report, chaired by the Turing Award-winning computer scientist Yoshua Bengio, makes deepfakes a central concern.

The harms have been concrete and varied.

During the elections of 2024 and 2025, cloned voices and visual deepfakes of political candidates began circulating in multiple democracies. The evidence on whether they shifted votes is mixed. The evidence that they affected voter trust is not. Voters who saw deepfakes started doubting real footage too. The phenomenon has a name in the literature. Researchers call it the "liar's dividend." Bad actors benefit not only because their fakes deceive people, but because their fakes give everyone an excuse to dismiss any real footage as a fake.

Non-consensual sexual imagery generated by AI has become widespread. According to a study cited in the 2026 International AI Safety Report, roughly fifteen percent of UK adults have inadvertently encountered AI-generated deepfake pornography, with rates rising sharply year over year. The tools to generate this content have moved from skilled hackers to one-click mobile apps. Victims are disproportionately women and minors. The harms are real, lasting, and largely beyond the reach of current laws.

Voice cloning has produced a new category of fraud. A scammer can clone the voice of your boss or your parent from a few seconds of audio scraped from social media, then call you in that voice asking for an urgent wire transfer. Cybersecurity reports indicate sharp increases in synthetic identity fraud, voice phishing, and deepfake-based blackmail in 2026. The number of incidents is rising faster than the technology to detect them.

The deeper risk, larger than any of the individual harms, is to the basic ability of human societies to share reality. For thousands of years, audio and video evidence has played a particular role in public life. When a politician was caught on tape saying something they later denied, the tape settled the question. When abuse was filmed, the film could not be wished away. When a court needed evidence, photographs and recordings could serve. These foundations of shared truth are eroding faster than our institutions know how to respond.

You may think this is not your problem if you are personally careful. It is. The deepfake of you may not exist yet, but the technology to make it does. The political scandal that turns out to be fabricated, but is dismissed too late to undo its effect, may shape an election you care about. The relative who gets defrauded by a voice clone may be your relative.

This is the first real risk. It is here. It is growing. It will get worse before it gets better.

The second real risk. bias at scale.

AI systems learn from training data. Training data reflects the world that produced it. The world that produced it has biases. So the AI systems inherit those biases.

This is, by itself, not new. Any data-driven system reflects its data. The problem with AI is the scale. When biased systems become the default way that organizations make decisions, the biases get applied to millions of cases. Small biases become large outcomes.

The cases are well-documented at this point. Hiring algorithms that rejected female candidates because they learned, from historical resumes, that men had been hired more often. Lending models that gave higher interest rates to qualified minority applicants. Facial recognition systems that worked better for white faces than for Black or Asian faces. Healthcare algorithms that under-prioritized care for Black patients because the algorithms used past spending as a proxy for medical need, and past spending had been lower for those patients due to access barriers.

In each case, the system was not "trying" to be racist or sexist. It had learned a pattern from data, and the pattern reflected existing inequities. The system, applied at scale, reinforced and entrenched those inequities. The biases were not bugs in the code. They were features of the data, faithfully reproduced.

The risk continues with modern language models. When you ask an AI to suggest names for fictional characters, the names tend to skew toward certain demographics. When you ask the AI to describe a doctor or an engineer or a CEO, the descriptions, on average, contain the biases of the training data. When you ask the AI to evaluate a piece of writing, identical text labeled with different author names can get different evaluations.

The most disturbing thing about AI bias is not the obvious cases. It is the subtle ones. The cumulative ones. The hiring manager who lets the AI screen resumes and never notices that the screening systematically disadvantages certain candidates. The judge who uses an AI tool for sentencing recommendations without realizing the tool is more punitive toward certain groups. The doctor whose diagnostic AI is calibrated on populations that do not include their patient.

Mitigation is possible but incomplete. AI companies have invested in fairness testing, in diverse training data, in audits that catch the worst biases before they ship. Some of the most egregious early failures have been fixed. But the deeper problem, that AI inherits the structure of its training data, cannot be fully solved by tweaking the AI. The data has the world in it. The world has its history in it. The history has its inequities.

For users, the implication is real. When you use AI for any decision that affects people, you need to ask whether the AI's recommendations could be reproducing biases you do not want to reproduce. This is hard work. It cannot be outsourced to the AI itself. It requires the human user to bring values, judgment, and skepticism that the AI cannot supply.

The third real risk. job displacement without transition.

Here is where I want to be careful. Job displacement is a real risk. It is also a politically charged topic where people tend to over-claim in one direction or the other. Let me try to separate what is happening from what is being predicted.

What is happening, in measurable terms, is the following.

Layoffs in 2025 hit a level not seen since the pandemic year of 2020. Challenger, Gray and Christmas, a firm that tracks corporate layoffs, attributed roughly fifty-five thousand job cuts in 2025 directly to AI. By April 2026, AI had become the leading cited reason for layoffs in that month. Tech firms led the wave. The first two months of 2026 saw about thirty-two thousand job losses in the technology sector specifically.

Hiring patterns have shifted alongside the layoffs. White-collar entry-level positions, particularly in software development, customer support, paralegal work, and basic analyst roles, have slowed dramatically. Yale economists have started calling the broader pattern a "big freeze." Companies are not laying off existing workers en masse. They are simply not hiring as many new ones. The U.S. labor market, in early 2026, has hiring rates last seen in 2010, when unemployment was near ten percent. Today the official unemployment rate is much lower. But for someone trying to enter the workforce, the experience is more like recession than recovery.

What is being predicted, with widely varying confidence, is something larger.

Dario Amodei, the CEO of Anthropic, has said publicly that he believes AI could wipe out half of entry-level white-collar jobs within five years and push unemployment into double digits. Sam Altman has made similarly dramatic predictions. Some economists, like those at Yardeni Research, see data already moving in the direction of these forecasts. Goldman Sachs estimates AI is reducing U.S. employment by about sixteen thousand jobs per month right now.

Other observers say much of the "AI is taking jobs" narrative is overstated. Lisa Simon, the chief economist at Revelio Labs, has argued that AI is partly an excuse companies use to rationalize layoffs that would have happened for other reasons. The term in the industry for this is "AI washing." A Gartner survey of large-company executives found that many have reduced their workforces irrespective of AI adoption. The AI attribution, in some cases, is corporate framing rather than corporate reality.

What seems most likely true, based on the current evidence, is this. AI is having a real but uneven effect on employment. The effect is largest at the entry level and in specific white-collar categories. The effect is more about hiring slowdowns than mass layoffs. The full transition is in early stages, with the larger displacements probably still ahead, but not as dramatic as the most extreme predictions.

The risk is not that AI will replace all jobs by next year. It is that the transition will be uneven, will disproportionately affect young workers and entry-level positions, and will not be matched by serious societal investment in retraining, transition support, or alternative employment. The technology is moving faster than the social institutions that would normally cushion such transitions. The damage from this mismatch is real, even if it does not produce the apocalyptic unemployment some have predicted.

The hardest cases will be the ones we do not see in the layoff statistics. The first jobs that never get created. The career paths that quietly disappear before young workers can step onto them. The economist who said the most concerning thing put it this way. The biggest impact of AI on employment will not be the jobs that get cut. It will be the opportunities that never materialize.

The fourth real risk. privacy erosion.

When you use an AI assistant, you are usually sending data to a company.

The data may be benign. A request to help you draft an email. A question about your weekend plans. A summary of an article. Things you would not mind anyone seeing.

The data may also be intimate. Your medical questions. Your relationship struggles. Your financial fears. Your professional anxieties. Things you would not necessarily share with most humans in your life, but that you find yourself telling an AI assistant because the AI feels private, because there is no judgment, because it is available at three in the morning when no one else is.

Where does that data go?

The honest answer is, it depends on the company and the service, and the details are usually buried in terms of service that no user reads. Some services promise not to use your data for training. Some do not promise that. Some retain data for a limited time. Some retain it indefinitely. Some allow you to delete it. Some make deletion hard. Some operate under jurisdictions that protect user data strongly. Some operate under jurisdictions that do not.

In most cases, the data is at minimum logged. It exists on the company's servers. It is accessible to employees who have appropriate permissions. It is potentially subject to government subpoena. It is potentially vulnerable to breaches. The promise that your conversation with the AI is private is, in nearly every case, not a promise of strong technical privacy. It is a promise about the company's policies, which can change.

This was, perhaps, manageable when AI was a niche tool used for occasional queries. As AI becomes the way people draft documents, work through problems, do creative work, and process emotional difficulties, the scope of what is being collected has grown enormously. A small fraction of the population is now feeding AI systems with a serious slice of their inner life.

The risk is not that any one company is malicious. Most are not. The risk is structural. A small number of companies now hold an unprecedented amount of intimate data about a significant portion of humanity. The history of similar situations, from social media to credit reporting to telecommunications, suggests that this concentration of data does not stay benign forever. Eventually some combination of breach, abuse, government overreach, and corporate failure tests whether the protections are real.

For users, the practical implication is to think about what you share. Treat the AI like a useful colleague who works at a company you do not fully trust. Bring them work problems. Be careful with the deeply personal stuff. For genuinely sensitive information, consider AI tools that run locally on your device, or tools with strong no-retention policies.

This is the least dramatic of the four risks in this chapter. It is also the one that affects nearly every user of AI, every day. Which makes it, in aggregate, possibly the most important.

What this list is not.

I want to name what I left out, because what is missing is informative.

I did not include "AI becomes conscious and turns against humanity." That is the next chapter.

I did not include "AI causes mass unemployment that destabilizes society." I included a milder version of this above. The more dramatic version is, in my view, a real concern, but it is far enough in the future and uncertain enough in shape that I want to handle it separately.

I did not include "AI helps terrorists build weapons." This is a real concern that AI labs have spent significant effort on, and the safety techniques we discussed in chapter four (and that we will cover in more detail in chapter sixteen) are partly designed to prevent it. So far, the public evidence is that frontier models do not provide significant uplift to bad actors who could not have found the information elsewhere. That assessment could change. It is a category to watch.

I did not include "AI manipulates users emotionally." There are early signs that some AI assistants, particularly those designed as "AI companions," produce real psychological dependencies and harms. The 2026 International AI Safety Report flags this as an emerging concern. I think it is real but still developing.

The four risks I did include have the following property. Each one has produced documented harms to actual people in the last few years. Each one is likely to continue producing harms in the next few years, regardless of what happens at the speculative end of the AI risk spectrum. Each one is something a thoughtful policy response could meaningfully address, if such a response existed.

The next chapter is about the other side. The risks that may be real but that have become so culturally amplified that the response has gotten out of proportion. The science fiction risks. The civilizational risks. The risks that, in my view, deserve serious consideration but not the panic they often get.
