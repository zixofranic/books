  Chapter Twenty-One

  Where This Is All Going

  The honest answer about the future, and why it does not have to leave you helpless.

We are coming to the end of this book.

I want to use the last chapter to do something difficult. I want to tell you, honestly, where I think this is all going, while admitting that nobody really knows. I want to give you a way to think about the future that is neither doom nor hype. I want to leave you with something more useful than a prediction. I want to leave you with a way of being in this new world that does not depend on being right about the future.

The honest starting point is that prediction in this area is genuinely hard. Almost everyone confident about the long-term trajectory of AI is overconfident. The people who said in 2020 that AI would never produce convincing text were wrong. The people who said in 2022 that it would never master coding were wrong. The people who said in 2024 that scaling had hit a wall were wrong. The people who say today, with certainty, that AGI is five years away or fifty years away, are probably also wrong. Some of them will turn out to be right by accident.

What I can offer is a framework. The main forces. The main uncertainties. The questions that will determine which version of the future we get. And the way to think about your own life in a world where the future is, more than usual, not yet decided.

Let me try.

The three trajectories.

When I look at the next ten to twenty years of AI, I see three rough trajectories that the technology could take. They are not mutually exclusive. The actual future will probably blend elements of all three. But naming them separately helps clarify what we are actually uncertain about.

The first trajectory is continued capability growth at roughly the current pace. The models keep getting better. The applications keep expanding. The economic effects keep accumulating. In ten years, AI is to most knowledge work what computers became to most knowledge work in the past thirty. A massive, productivity-enhancing layer that touches everything, embedded so deeply that most users do not think about it. The world looks different from today, but it is recognizable. Jobs change. Some industries are reshaped. Most people still have work, in modified forms. The transition produces real winners and losers, real social costs, real opportunities. Life continues.

The second trajectory is acceleration. The systems get qualitatively more powerful, not just incrementally better. AGI, however you define it, arrives. The economic effects are not just enhancing but transformative. Significant fractions of cognitive work become genuinely automatable. The transition is much faster than the institutions of work, education, and policy can keep up with. The next decade looks more like a fundamental reordering than a continuous evolution. The winners and losers are far more extreme. New questions emerge about what humans are for, in a world where most cognitive tasks can be done by machines.

The third trajectory is a plateau. The current scaling approaches hit limits. Capabilities continue to improve incrementally for some time, then largely level off without major new ideas. The world adjusts to roughly the current frontier of capability over five or ten years. The transformation, when complete, is large but bounded. AI is a powerful new tool that has reshaped some things, like search and software development, more than others. The hype of the late 2020s looks, in retrospect, like the dotcom bubble. Real, important, but not the singularity.

These three trajectories have very different implications for how you should plan your life. The first suggests continuity with adaptation. The second suggests preparing for radical change. The third suggests not over-committing to AI-centric assumptions.

Honest reading of the current evidence does not let me pick decisively between them. Serious experts disagree. The technology itself sometimes seems to be in one trajectory and sometimes in another. I have my own intuitions, which I will share at the end of this chapter. But the only honest framing is that we are in a period where the next few years of evidence will reveal which trajectory we are actually in, and that period is now.

What we can be confident about.

There are a few things I will commit to, even given the uncertainty.

The current generation of models is not the last generation. The pace of improvement, while uneven, has not stopped. The companies are spending massive amounts of capital on next-generation training runs. The next significant capability jumps are coming. Some of them are probably arriving in 2026 itself.

The economic effects will continue to compound. We are still in the early adoption phase for most industries. Even if AI capabilities stopped improving today, the deployment of current capabilities into existing workflows would continue producing changes for years. Most office workers still do not use AI heavily in their daily work. Most companies still do not have mature AI strategies. The diffusion is the dominant story for the next several years, even more than the capability improvements.

The geopolitical contest will intensify. The United States and China are not going to step back from this competition. The European Union is not going to give up on regulating it. The other major powers are not going to be passive. The next several years will see more, not less, government action on AI. Some of it will be productive. Some of it will be destructive. Most of it will be confused.

The risks I described in chapter fourteen will continue to be real. Deepfakes will get better and more common. The bias issues will not vanish. The job displacement will continue to be uneven and concentrated on entry-level white-collar work. The privacy concerns will get more pressing, not less. Any plan that assumes these problems will solve themselves is wrong.

The risks I described in chapter fifteen, the more speculative ones, may or may not arrive on the timelines their advocates predict. But the underlying research is real. The concerns are not silly. Some serious progress will continue to be made on alignment, on interpretability, on the technical questions that matter for safe deployment of more capable systems. Whether that progress is enough remains to be seen.

The companies will continue to be a major force shaping outcomes. Their decisions about what to build, what to deploy, what to refuse, how to compete, how to cooperate, will matter as much as government regulation in most cases. The pressure on these companies is going to grow. Whether they handle it well will depend on the people running them, which is a more contingent factor than most analysis acknowledges.

These are the things I would commit to. The rest is genuinely uncertain.

The four questions that will matter.

Beneath the surface of the AI debate, there are four questions whose answers will largely determine the trajectory we end up in.

The first is whether the current scaling approach continues to produce qualitatively new capabilities, or whether it hits diminishing returns. The honest answer is that we cannot tell yet. The models in 2026 are better than the models in 2024, but the rate of improvement and the nature of the new capabilities are subjects of real debate among researchers. The next two or three model generations will tell us a lot. If they keep producing surprises, we are in the second trajectory. If they produce diminishing returns, we are in the first or third.

The second is whether the alignment problem turns out to be tractable. The hope is that with enough research, we can build AI systems that reliably do what we want them to do. The fear is that as systems get more capable, we lose the ability to verify their behavior in advance. Significant resources are going into this problem. Whether the resources are sufficient, and whether the problem is solvable at all, will determine how much of the speculative risk is actually risk.

The third is whether democratic institutions can adapt fast enough. Most of the rules being made now were designed for a much slower technology. Democratic processes, by their nature, take time. The companies move in months. The technology moves in months. The regulators move in years. The gap is real, and the longer it persists, the more the de facto rules of AI deployment are set by private companies rather than public institutions. Whether democracies can find ways to make consequential decisions on technology timescales is one of the central political questions of our era.

The fourth is whether we, the users, develop the wisdom to use these tools well. Not just at the individual level we covered in the previous chapters, but at the cultural level. Whether we become a species that uses AI to enhance our humanity, or one that lets AI substitute for our humanity, is a choice we are making one decision at a time, billions of decisions a day. Nobody is in charge of this choice. We are all making it, together, with very limited coordination. The cumulative result will shape the next generation more than any of the formal decisions made by companies or governments.

These are the four questions. Not technical questions, in any narrow sense. They are questions about science, about institutions, about culture, and about character. The answers are not yet determined. They will be determined by what we do.

What to do about a future you cannot predict.

This is the question that, if you have read the book this far, you are probably ready to ask. Given all this uncertainty, what should you actually do?

A few suggestions, ranging from the immediate to the long-term.

Stay current with the technology. Not at the level of an expert, but at the level of someone who knows what these tools can do. Try the new things when they come out. Notice what changes. Be the person in your circle who can answer questions about how this stuff works. The asymmetry between AI-fluent and AI-illiterate adults is real, and it is going to grow. Be on the fluent side.

Maintain your skills. The temptation to outsource your thinking to AI is real and growing. Resist it where it matters. Keep reading books that are not summarized for you. Keep writing in your own voice. Keep doing math in your head when you can. Keep struggling with hard problems before reaching for help. The skills you keep through the AI transition are skills you can use to direct AI when AI is the right tool. The skills you lose to AI are gone.

Invest in relationships. Whatever shape the future takes, the value of trustworthy human relationships will not decline. They may increase. The relationships you build over the next ten years will be the people who introduce you to opportunities, vouch for you when you need it, and help you make sense of changes too fast to process alone. Show up for people. Be the kind of person other people want to know.

Develop your judgment. The skill that you are practicing every time you make a decision under uncertainty is the skill that will hold its value through every trajectory. The people who can look at confusing situations and decide well will be valuable in every version of the future. The people who can only execute well-defined tasks will be in trouble in most versions. Practice judgment. Reflect on your decisions. Learn from your mistakes.

Stay engaged with civic life. The rules being written now will shape what AI is allowed to do for the rest of our lives. Most of the rules are being written by people who do not understand the technology, advised by people who have specific interests, debated in venues where most citizens are not paying attention. Your engagement matters. Vote. Read. Talk to your representatives. Show up when there are public consultations. The future is not just going to be done to you. You have a role in shaping it.

Resist the binary thinking. The temptation, when faced with uncertainty about the future, is to pick a side. Either AI is the savior or the destroyer. Either the optimists are right or the doomers are right. Either you embrace the technology fully or you reject it entirely. Almost every binary in the AI debate is too simple to be useful. The truth is somewhere in the middle, with real costs and real benefits, real risks and real opportunities, that you have to weigh carefully and continuously. The people who do well in this environment are the ones who can hold complexity without collapsing it into a story.

A personal note.

I have tried, throughout this book, to be honest about what I know and what I do not know. I want to end with one more honest admission.

I am writing this book in 2026. The technology I am writing about will be different in 2027. By the time you are reading this, some of what I have said may already be wrong. The examples will have aged. The numbers will have changed. The companies will have evolved. The regulations will have moved on. That is the nature of writing about a technology that is moving as fast as this one.

What I hope will not have aged is the underlying framework. The principles I have tried to communicate. The way of thinking about this technology that I have tried to model. The recognition that AI is impressive and important without being magical, that it has real risks and real benefits, that it should be neither worshipped nor demonized.

If you have made it through this book, you should now be able to read almost any article about AI and follow what is being said. You should be able to evaluate claims critically. You should be able to use the tools more effectively than you did before. You should have a way of thinking about your work, your relationships, and your children that takes AI seriously without letting it dominate.

That is what I hoped to give you, and what I hope I have managed.

The technology is what it is. It will continue to develop, faster than any institution can fully process, in directions that none of us can fully predict. But you, the reader, are not a passive recipient of whatever this technology becomes. You have agency. You can use the tools wisely. You can keep developing your own abilities. You can advocate for the rules you want to live under. You can raise your children to be the kind of adults who can handle this new world.

The future is uncertain. Your response to it does not have to be.

Thank you for reading.
