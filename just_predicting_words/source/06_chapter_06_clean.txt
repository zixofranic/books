  Chapter Six

  Anthropic, the Company That Broke Off Over Safety

  Founded by people who thought the leader in their field was being reckless. Now worth nearly as much as that leader.

A note before we begin.

I have been using Anthropic's assistant, Claude, extensively while writing this book. It has been a useful tool for me. That makes me, in some honest sense, a customer of the company we're about to discuss. I am not employed by Anthropic, paid by them, or in any way connected to them other than as a user. But I want you to know about my use of their product before I tell you about them, because it is the kind of thing that could shape how I write.

I am going to try to give Anthropic the same treatment I gave OpenAI in the last chapter. Real story. Real complications. Honest assessment. Where I think the company is admirable, I will say so. Where I think it has problems, I will say so. You can judge how well I succeed.

With that out of the way, let's begin.

THE FOUNDING

In late 2020, a group of senior researchers at OpenAI were unhappy.

The exact details of their unhappiness have never been made entirely public. What is known is that they were concerned about the direction OpenAI was taking. The pace, the priorities, the structure that the for-profit conversion was creating. They believed that the careful safety-focused research mission they had signed up for was being subordinated to commercial pressure.

In early 2021, they left.

The group included Dario Amodei, who had been OpenAI's Vice President of Research. It also included his sister Daniela Amodei, who had been OpenAI's Vice President of Operations. Tom Brown, the lead author of the GPT-3 paper. Jared Kaplan, a physicist who had done foundational work on the mathematics of scaling. And several other senior researchers and engineers.

They founded a new company. They called it Anthropic.

The name comes from "anthropos," the Greek word for "human." The founding premise was that AI development should be done with human safety and human values at the center, and that this required a different kind of organization than the one they had left.

Like OpenAI before it, Anthropic was structured unusually. It was set up as a Public Benefit Corporation, a legal structure that requires the company's board to balance shareholder interests against the company's stated public mission. The mission, written into the corporate documents, was the development of safe and beneficial AI.

There was something almost defiant about this. The founders had just left a company that was struggling with the tension between commercial pressure and safety mission. They were now starting a new company with a similar tension built into its DNA. The bet was that they could do it better. That they could build a frontier AI lab whose commercial success and safety mission would reinforce each other rather than fight each other.

Whether they have succeeded at this bet is, in 2026, a genuinely open question. The evidence so far is mixed. We'll get to that.

The early years.

Anthropic's first few years were quieter than OpenAI's, by a lot. They published research papers. They hired carefully. They built models, but did not release a consumer product until 2023.

In that period, they did something distinctive. They invested heavily in what AI researchers call interpretability. The goal of interpretability is to understand, mechanically, what is going on inside an AI model. Why did the model produce this answer rather than that one? Which internal patterns are responsible for which behaviors? Where, inside the billions of parameters, are the concepts the model has learned?

This is hard work. The interior of a large language model is famously opaque. The "weights" that make the model do what it does are smeared across hundreds of billions of parameters in ways that defy human inspection. Interpretability research tries to crack open this black box. Make the smear legible. Anthropic was one of the labs that took this work most seriously, and they continue to lead in it.

The reason this matters, to them and to the field, is that you cannot reliably make a system safe if you do not understand what it is doing. The current generation of AI safety techniques (the RLHF and constitutional AI methods we discussed in chapter four) are essentially behavioral. You train the model to behave a certain way and you hope it generalizes. But you cannot, with current techniques, look inside the model and verify that it has actually learned the values you intended. You can only observe its outputs and hope.

Interpretability research is the long-term attempt to fix this. It is slow. It is expensive. It does not produce flashy consumer demos. But Anthropic, and a few other labs, have continued to invest in it because they believe it is necessary for any version of AI safety that is more than wishful thinking.

This focus on interpretability is, in some ways, the clearest signal of what Anthropic is. A company that thinks the most important research in the field is the kind that does not pay off commercially for years.

THE PRODUCTS

Anthropic released its first version of Claude, the AI assistant, in March 2023. Initially it was available only through an API for developers. Later that year it became available as a consumer chatbot at claude.ai.

The early versions of Claude were not the best on every benchmark. ChatGPT was usually faster. Various tests showed Claude as roughly competitive but rarely dominant. What Claude did have, from very early on, was a particular character. It was, by general consensus, more careful than ChatGPT. More willing to push back on the user when something seemed wrong. More honest about its limitations. Less prone to certain kinds of confident error.

It was also, in some users' view, too cautious. The early Claude refused things people wanted it to do, often for reasons that seemed unjustified. The model would decline to write a piece of fiction because it touched a sensitive topic. Or refuse a coding task because the code could theoretically be misused. The over-refusal problem was a real complaint in the early days, and Anthropic spent significant effort addressing it.

By 2024, Anthropic had introduced what it called Constitutional AI publicly. We covered this in chapter four. The idea was to make the model's values explicit. Not smeared opaquely across human ratings, but written down in a document. The constitution, in early versions, drew on sources like the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, the principles of various ethical frameworks, and Anthropic's own analysis of what made for responsible AI behavior.

Whether the constitution actually constrains the model is a contested question. Critics point out that the constitution is interpreted by the model itself, using the same neural network that is being trained, so the principles in the constitution can be only as reliable as the model's ability to apply them. Defenders point out that even imperfect explicit principles are better than no principles at all, and that the constitution at least gives outside observers something to point at and argue about.

In 2024 and 2025, Anthropic released a series of increasingly capable models. Claude 3. Claude 3.5. Claude 3.7. Then the Claude 4 family in 2025, the Claude 4.5 family in late 2025, and Claude Opus 4.6 and 4.7 by spring 2026. Each generation closed the gap with the leading competitors, and on some measures, opened a new one.

Then in 2025, they released Claude Code. And the curve broke.

The claude code moment.

Claude Code was, in some ways, a small thing. A command-line tool that let developers use Claude as a coding assistant from the terminal. It was launched in May 2025. It was aimed at a niche audience of software developers.

Within six months, it was generating one billion dollars in annualized revenue.

Within a year, that number was two and a half billion.

Within fifteen months, Anthropic's overall annualized revenue had jumped from a few hundred million to thirty billion dollars. Eighty times the company's own internal forecasts.

What had happened? Claude Code had hit something the rest of the industry had been chasing without quite getting right. It was an AI agent that could actually do useful work, end to end, in the place where developers actually worked. Not a chatbot that you copied and pasted answers from. A real assistant that read your files, ran your tests, fixed your bugs, and shipped your code, with you watching.

The implication, beyond Anthropic's revenue numbers, was that the long-promised "agentic" AI was finally arriving in production for a particular kind of work. Developers, who had previously been skeptical of AI assistants, now used them constantly. Engineering teams reorganized around them. Companies that did not adopt them found themselves outpaced by companies that did.

The economic shift this represented, even just within software engineering, was significant. The full implications of agentic AI for other kinds of work, knowledge work, professional services, creative work, are still rolling out.

I want to flag here that Anthropic's success with Claude Code, however impressive, is one data point in a much larger competitive picture. OpenAI has its own agentic products. Google has shipped agentic capabilities throughout its workspace. Chinese labs have released open-weight models that can be used to build agentic systems for a fraction of the cost. The frontier in this area is moving every few weeks. Whatever I write about it will be partly out of date by the time you read it.

What is clear is that Anthropic, the company that started smaller and slower than its main rival, has caught up. By some measures, surpassed.

The valuation race.

As I write this in May 2026, Anthropic is in talks with investors about a funding round that would value the company at approximately nine hundred billion dollars. If the round closes near that figure, Anthropic will be worth slightly more than OpenAI, the company its founders left five years earlier.

This is, on its face, a remarkable thing.

The reasons for the rise are reasonably clear. Anthropic's revenue, driven by Claude Code and enterprise adoption, is reportedly growing faster than OpenAI's. They have over a thousand large customers each spending more than a million dollars a year. They have invested heavily in serving developers, in safety research, and in the kind of careful enterprise relationships that take longer to build but tend to be more durable than consumer subscriptions.

The funders involved are substantial. Google has committed up to forty billion dollars in investments. Amazon has committed up to twenty-five billion. The company has signed compute contracts with Microsoft, Amazon, Google, and Broadcom, and even, in a surprising arrangement, with Elon Musk's SpaceX for additional capacity.

Anthropic is now in a similar position to OpenAI a few years ago. A small company that has become a giant, with the same set of pressures that giants face. Hyperscaler relationships. Government scrutiny. Workforce challenges. Decisions about whether and when to go public.

In March 2026, the United States Pentagon designated Anthropic a supply chain risk and blacklisted the company from working with the military. The reasoning, which has not been fully public, appears to involve concerns about the company's funding sources and corporate connections. Over a hundred enterprise customers reportedly raised concerns. Anthropic has been navigating this issue while continuing its broader growth.

The full story of how a safety-focused lab founded by twelve researchers became a nine-hundred-billion-dollar company in five years has not yet been told. It will be told eventually. As an outside observer, I can say only that the company has been more successful, faster, than almost anyone outside its founders predicted, and that this success has brought its own complications.

What ANTHROPIC actually believes.

Like the OpenAI chapter, let me try to lay out the stated beliefs of the people running Anthropic. These are not the only views inside the company, but they are the views that get expressed publicly most often, and they shape the products you use.

The first belief is that powerful AI is coming. This is the same belief OpenAI holds. The Amodeis and other Anthropic leaders have said publicly that they think transformative AI may arrive within this decade. Their strategic decisions reflect this belief.

The second belief is that the safety problem is real, technically hard, and not solved. Anthropic's leadership has consistently said that current safety techniques are not sufficient to confidently align very powerful AI systems, and that significant research is still required. They have argued that the pace of capability development should be matched by a serious pace of safety research, and they have publicly said this on many occasions.

The third belief is that the responsible position is to be at the frontier. The argument runs like this. If transformative AI is coming, someone will build it. Better that someone be a lab that takes safety seriously than a lab that doesn't. Therefore, even though deploying AI capabilities adds to the overall pace of the field, it is worth doing if the alternative is ceding the frontier to less careful builders.

This argument is one of the most contested ideas in modern AI policy. Critics point out that every frontier lab uses some version of this reasoning to justify what would otherwise be reckless behavior. Defenders point out that the alternative, a world in which the safest researchers withdraw from the frontier, leaves the field to whoever cares least about safety. Both arguments have merit. Honest people disagree.

The fourth belief is that the company should publish what it learns about safety, even when doing so might help competitors. Anthropic has published a great deal of research, including research that explicitly aims to make AI training safer for everyone. This is genuinely unusual in a competitive industry, and it is one of the clearer pieces of evidence that the company's mission is not pure rhetoric.

Whether you find these beliefs convincing depends on your views about how the AI field should develop. Reasonable people land in different places. What is undeniable is that the company has been remarkably consistent in stating these beliefs, and the products you use from Anthropic are products that beliefs like these produce.

What this means for you.

If you use Claude, you are using a product made by a company that, at least in its stated values, takes the safety of AI more seriously than most. Whether that translates to a better product for you depends on what you want from an AI assistant. Some users find Claude more pleasant and more honest than alternatives. Some find it too cautious. Many switch between assistants depending on the task.

The thing to understand, regardless of your personal preference, is that Claude reflects a particular set of choices about what an AI assistant should be. The choices are made by a particular company, with particular values, in a particular competitive environment. They are different choices than the ones being made at OpenAI, or Google, or any of the other labs. The differences are usually subtle, but they accumulate.

The bigger picture, beyond any one product, is that Anthropic represents a real alternative model for how AI development can work. Not the only model. Not necessarily the best model. But a model in which a company can take safety seriously, invest heavily in long-term research, refuse to chase every short-term metric, and still build a viable business. The fact that this is possible at all is one of the more hopeful developments of recent years. Whether it remains possible as the pressures intensify is one of the questions the next few years will answer.
