Just Predicting Words. Study Notes. Part one.

Just Predicting Words...

Section. The launch.

Chat GPT launched on November 30, twenty twenty-two. The company behind it was Open AI, a San Francisco AI lab. The product was a simple chat interface sitting on top of a fine-tuned version of an existing language model called GPT three point five. It was not announced as a major release. Internally, it was described as a low-key research preview.

Within five days, Chat GPT had one million users. Within two months, it had a hundred million. At the time, it was the fastest-growing consumer product in history — faster than Facebook, faster than Tik Tok, faster than the iPhone. Within a few weeks of launch, it had quietly entered the daily routines of writers, programmers, teachers, lawyers, doctors, and ordinary users who had never tried any AI tool before in their lives.

Section. The mechanism.

The technology underneath Chat GPT, despite the apparent intelligence of its outputs, is doing one specific thing: predicting the next word in a sequence. Given everything written so far, the model assigns a probability to every possible next word, picks one, and continues. Then it does the same thing again with the new word included in the context. It produces text one token at a time, left to right, by repeatedly answering the question: what word comes next.

This is the central claim of the book and the source of its title. Modern AI assistants are next-word predictors. The trick is small. What emerges from it is not.

Section. Why this matters as framing.

Most discussion of AI either over-mystifies the technology (treating it as a conscious mind) or dismisses it (calling it autocomplete). The next-word-prediction framing is meant to do neither. It is meant to be honest about the mechanism — really, the model is just predicting words — while leaving room for the fact that doing this at sufficient scale, on sufficient data, with the right architecture, produces capabilities that look qualitatively new.

The author's stance: AI assistants are genuinely useful, genuinely limited, and the user who understands how they actually work makes better decisions than the user who treats them as magic or as a toy.

Section. What the book covers (preview).

The book is structured in roughly six parts:

Part 1 (Chapters 1–4): how the technology actually works — tokens, embeddings, the Transformer architecture, attention, training, and the alignment techniques that shape raw word predictors into helpful assistants.
Part 2 (Chapters 5–10): the companies — Open AI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, the Chinese labs (Deep Seek, Qwen, Kimi, others), and the rest of the ecosystem (Microsoft, X AI, Mistral, Perplexity, Amazon, Apple).
Part 3 (Chapters 11–13): what AI is genuinely good at, how it fails, and the structural limits of language as a medium.
Part 4 (Chapters 14–17): real risks, hyped risks, the guardrails companies use, and the regulatory landscape (EU AI Act, US patchwork, China's state framework).
Part 5 (Chapters 18–20): how to use AI well, how to handle it at work, how to talk to your kids about it.
Part 6 (Chapter 21): where this is going, and what to do given the uncertainty.

The book was written through May twenty twenty-six and reflects the state of the field at that moment.

A Short History of Machines That Read...

Section. Distributional semantics.

The intellectual ancestor of modern language AI is an idea from nineteen fifties linguistics: words get their meaning from the company they keep. The slogan version was coined by the British linguist John Rupert Firth: "You shall know a word by the company it keeps." For decades this was just an observation. It became useful only when somebody figured out how to compute with it.

Section. word2vec, twenty thirteen.

The breakthrough came in twenty thirteen. A researcher at Google named Tomas Mikolov, with colleagues, built a tool called word2vec. It read enormous amounts of text and learned, for every word, a list of about three hundred numbers — a vector — that captured the word's "company" in the corpus. Words that appeared in similar contexts ended up with similar vectors.

The striking demonstration was that the vectors did arithmetic. You could take the vector for "king," subtract "man," add "woman," and get something extremely close to "queen." You could take Paris minus France plus Germany and get Berlin. The system had learned the concept of gender, and the concept of "capital of," without ever being told these things existed. It had extracted meaning from raw text, at scale, for the first time in any machine.

The limit of word2vec was that it knew about words but not sentences. It could tell you that "dog" and "puppy" were similar. It could not tell you what "the dog chased its tail" meant, or how meaning depended on word order.

Section. The Transformer, twenty seventeen.

The architecture that made sentences tractable was published in June twenty seventeen in a paper called "Attention Is All You Need." It came out of Google. There were eight authors. The architecture they introduced — the Transformer — is the basis for essentially every modern large language model. Chat GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama, Deep Seek, Qwen — all Transformers.

Within the next eight years, all eight of those Google researchers had left Google. They founded or led other AI organizations. Aidan Gomez went on to co-found Cohere. Others ended up at Open AI, Anthropic, and various startups. Google had invented the architecture that now powers most of the modern AI industry and then watched a generation of competitors build on it.

Section. The GPT line, twenty eighteen–twenty twenty.

Open AI built on the Transformer. The pattern was to take the architecture and scale it up — more parameters, more data, more compute. The GPT name stands for Generative Pre-trained Transformer.

GPT one, released twenty eighteen, had one hundred and seventeen million parameters. It was a research curiosity.
GPT two, released twenty nineteen, had one and a half billion parameters. Open AI initially withheld it, calling it "too dangerous to release." They eventually released it in November twenty nineteen.
GPT three, released twenty twenty, had one hundred and seventy-five billion parameters. It was the first model that startled even its creators with what it could do, and it was available only through an API.

The jump from GPT two to GPT three was about a hundred times more parameters. The capability jump was larger still.

Section. The Chat GPT moment, November twenty twenty-two.

On November 30, twenty twenty-two, Open AI launched Chat GPT — a chat interface to a fine-tuned version of GPT three point five. The launch was framed as a low-key research preview. It went viral within hours.

One million users in five days. One hundred million users in two months. At the time, the fastest-growing consumer product in history.

Section. The scramble that followed, late twenty twenty-two through twenty twenty-three.

The Chat GPT launch triggered a frantic reordering of the industry.

Google declared an internal "code red." It treated Chat GPT as an existential threat to search.

Anthropic, founded by ex-Open AI researchers, released the first version of its Claude assistant in March twenty twenty-three.

Meta released the first version of Llama, becoming the first major company to release a frontier-class model with open weights (anyone could download and run it).

Microsoft, which had already invested in Open AI starting in twenty nineteen, deepened the relationship dramatically. The total Microsoft investment in Open AI eventually exceeded thirteen billion dollars.

Amazon made a comparable bet on Anthropic, eventually committing up to twenty-five billion dollars.

By the end of twenty twenty-three, the modern AI industry — its big players, its commercial structure, its competitive shape — was largely set. Most of what has happened since has been the elaboration of that structure.

Inside the Machine...

Section. Tokens.

Modern language models don't read characters and they don't quite read words. They read tokens. A token is usually a piece of a word — typically four characters or so, sometimes a whole short word, sometimes a single character for rare cases. "Cat" might be one token. "Unbelievable" might be three. Names, punctuation, and unusual character sequences each become their own tokens.

The model produces text the same way it reads it: one token at a time. The "next-word prediction" framing from chapter one is more precisely "next-token prediction." For practical purposes the distinction usually doesn't matter, but it matters for understanding context limits and pricing — most AI APIs charge per token, not per word.

Section. Embeddings and vector space.

Each token gets converted into a vector — a list of several hundred to several thousand numbers. The vector is the model's internal representation of that token's meaning, learned during training from the patterns of how the token co-occurs with others.

This is the same idea as word2vec from chapter two, but scaled up. The vector space has the property that semantically similar tokens end up close to each other. Words about cooking cluster together. Words about sports cluster together. Mathematical relationships between vectors capture conceptual relationships — gender, capital-of, plural-of, comparative-of, and many others the model discovers without supervision.

The vectors are dense (every dimension carries some signal, none are zero by default), high-dimensional (hundreds to thousands of dimensions, far more than humans can visualize), and the dimensions don't correspond to human-interpretable categories — they emerge from the training process.

Section. Attention.

The Transformer's core mechanism is called attention. When the model is producing the next token, it doesn't treat all the previous tokens equally. For each token it's about to generate, it computes how much "attention" to pay to every previous token in the context, and weights its prediction accordingly.

This solves the problem word2vec couldn't solve: word order matters, and context further back can be relevant. When the model sees "the dog chased its tail," attention lets it know that "its" refers to "dog," not to something earlier or later in the sentence. When the model is summarizing a long document, attention lets it pull together information from anywhere in the document, not just nearby words.

The full name of the twenty seventeen paper that introduced the Transformer was "Attention Is All You Need" — meaning that attention alone, without the other complicated architectural pieces researchers had been trying, was enough.

Section. The Transformer architecture.

A Transformer is a stack of layers, each layer made of attention plus some standard neural-network components. Information flows up the stack: lower layers learn local patterns (which word follows which), middle layers learn syntactic and structural patterns (how sentences are organized), upper layers learn abstract semantic patterns (what the passage is about, how to respond to it).

A modern frontier model has dozens to over a hundred such layers. Each layer transforms the representations slightly. By the top of the stack, the representations are rich enough to support the model's predictions.

Section. Pre-training.

A frontier model is trained on enormous amounts of text. The training objective is simple: given a sequence of tokens, predict the next one. Do this billions of times across trillions of tokens of text. The text comes from essentially the entire public internet, plus licensed datasets, books, code repositories, and other sources.

This phase is called pre-training. It's the most expensive part of building a modern AI model — it requires months of compute on thousands to tens of thousands of GPUs and costs hundreds of millions of dollars for the largest models. The model that comes out of pre-training is a raw next-token predictor. It is not yet a chatbot, doesn't know what a user is, and doesn't know it's supposed to be helpful. That shaping happens in the next phase, which chapter four covers.

Section. Parameters.

A model's parameters are the actual numerical values inside the neural network. Pre-training sets these values. Roughly speaking, more parameters means more capacity to learn complex patterns, though the relationship is not strictly linear.

The headline numbers across the GPT line: GPT one had 117 million, GPT two had 1.5 billion, GPT three had 175 billion. The largest open-weight frontier models in twenty twenty-six (such as Deep Seek V four's 1.6 trillion-parameter variant) have crossed the trillion mark. The largest closed-weight frontier models are believed to be comparable or larger but their exact sizes are not disclosed.

When AI labs talk about model size, they almost always mean parameter count.

Section. Context window.

The amount of text the model can consider at once is called the context window, measured in tokens. The first GPT models had context windows of a few thousand tokens. By twenty twenty-six, frontier models often have context windows of one to two million tokens — enough to fit entire books, large codebases, or long meeting transcripts in a single conversation. Context windows have grown much faster than parameter counts in recent years and are now one of the most important practical differences between competing models.

Taming the Wild Word Predictor...

Section. The problem after pre-training.

A model fresh out of pre-training is a raw next-token predictor. It does not know what a conversation is. It does not know what a "user" is. It does not know it is supposed to be helpful, honest, or harmless. Given the right prompt it will produce racist text, instructions for harm, conspiracy theories, sexually explicit content, or any other pattern present in the training data, because the training data contained all of those patterns.

Turning that raw model into something safe and useful for ordinary users is the work of the alignment phase. Three techniques do most of the work: instruction tuning, reinforcement learning from human feedback, and Constitutional AI.

Section. Instruction tuning.

The first step is to show the model examples of being helpful. Human contractors write thousands of example exchanges — a request and an ideal response. The model is fine-tuned on these examples. After this fine-tuning, the model has a sense that when it sees a question-shaped input it should produce an answer-shaped output. It now behaves like an assistant rather than a generic text continuation engine.

Instruction tuning alone is enough to produce a basically functional chatbot. It is not enough to produce a safe or carefully-behaved one.

Section. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).

RLHF is the technique that shaped raw word predictors into the polished assistants we use today. The training method:

1. The model produces several different responses to the same prompt.
2. Human raters rank the responses from best to worst.
3. The model is updated to make highly-ranked responses more likely and low-ranked responses less likely.
4. Repeat across millions of prompts.

Over millions of these comparisons, the model learns to produce the kind of response humans prefer. This is where it picks up tone, friendliness, format conventions, the habit of declining to help with abusive requests, and many other behaviors.

RLHF has a known side effect: sycophancy. Humans, on average, prefer responses that agree with them, that compliment them, and that don't push back too hard. The model picks up a subtle bias toward telling you what you want to hear. We come back to this failure mode in chapter twelve.

Section. Constitutional AI.

Constitutional AI is Anthropic's variant on the same idea. Instead of relying entirely on direct human feedback for every behavior, Anthropic gives the model a written set of principles — its "constitution." The model is then trained to critique and revise its own outputs against the principles, with humans involved in the loop more sparingly.

The advantages, as Anthropic argues them: explicit principles can be audited, debated, and changed; the model can be trained on a much larger scale because humans don't have to grade every output; and the principles can address subtle behaviors (such as how to handle dual-use information requests) that are hard to capture in single-example comparisons.

Other major labs use a mix of RLHF and constitutional-style techniques. The boundary has blurred over time.

Section. The "helpful, honest, harmless" frame.

The explicit alignment goal at most major labs is for the assistant to be helpful, honest, and harmless. The triad is often abbreviated HHH. The three are in real tension: a fully harmless model would refuse most requests; a fully helpful model would explain how to do dangerous things; an honest model has to admit when it doesn't know something, which is sometimes unhelpful. Labs spend most of their alignment effort negotiating these trade-offs.

Section. The system prompt.

Once the model is deployed as a product, every conversation starts with a hidden block of text called the system prompt, prepended to the conversation before the model sees it. The system prompt is where the company tells the model what kind of assistant to be — tone, topics to avoid, format of responses, legal disclaimers, the model's persona, the rules for handling sensitive topics. For a consumer chatbot like Chat GPT or Claude, the system prompt is typically several thousand words.

The system prompt is the fastest-moving part of the defense system around a model. It can be changed without retraining, so labs can respond to new failure modes within hours. It is also one of the most fragile parts: clever users can sometimes get the model to ignore its system prompt through carefully designed inputs, an attack family known as prompt injection.

Section. What you actually talk to.

When you use a frontier AI product, you are talking to a model that has been pre-trained on the internet, instruction-tuned on examples, refined through RLHF or Constitutional AI, given a system prompt by the company, and wrapped in additional safety classifiers that watch the conversation. The assistant you experience is the cumulative product of all of those layers. We come back to the defensive stack in chapter sixteen.

Open AI, the Company That Started the Wave...

Section. Founding.

Open AI was founded in December twenty fifteen in San Francisco. The founding group included Sam Altman, Elon Musk, Greg Brockman, Ilya Sutskever, John Schulman, Wojciech Zaremba, and several others. The original structure was a non-profit. The founding pitch was that artificial general intelligence was potentially the most consequential technology humanity would ever build, that it should not be developed in secret by a single corporation, and that an open research lab funded by philanthropy was the right vehicle.

Funding commitments at founding totaled around one billion dollars in pledges from Musk, Reid Hoffman, Peter Thiel, Amazon Web Services, and others.

Section. The Musk departure, twenty eighteen.

Elon Musk left the Open AI board in twenty eighteen, publicly citing conflicts of interest with his AI work at Tesla. By some accounts that surfaced later in his lawsuit, he had wanted to acquire Open AI outright before leaving, and at one point asked for about ninety percent of the equity in the for-profit conversion.

Section. The for-profit conversion.

By twenty nineteen, Open AI's leadership had concluded that the resources required to build frontier AI exceeded what a non-profit could raise. They created a capped-profit subsidiary that could take outside investment, while keeping the non-profit as the controlling entity. The structure was unusual and would generate lawsuits later.

Section. Microsoft, twenty nineteen onward.

Microsoft invested in Open AI starting in twenty nineteen. After Chat GPT, the partnership deepened. Microsoft's total commitment grew to more than thirteen billion dollars over time. Microsoft got the right to deploy Open AI's models in Microsoft products (Copilot, Bing, Office, Azure) and a large slice of the commercial upside. Open AI got compute, cash, and a guaranteed distribution channel.

Section. Chat GPT.

Launched November 30, twenty twenty-two — see chapters one and two for the launch metrics. The product turned Open AI from a research lab into a consumer phenomenon overnight.

Section. The board crisis, November twenty twenty-three.

On November 17, twenty twenty-three, the Open AI board fired Sam Altman. The official reason was a vague reference to a loss of confidence. Within hours, much of the company threatened to quit. Microsoft offered to hire the entire team. Investors put pressure on the board. By November 21 — four days later — Altman was back as CEO. Most of the board members who had fired him were gone. The episode became a case study in how quickly governance can collapse under commercial pressure.

Section. The Musk lawsuit, twenty twenty-four–twenty twenty-six.

Elon Musk sued Open AI in twenty twenty-four. His claim was that the transition from non-profit to for-profit was a betrayal of the original founding mission. He asked the court to undo the for-profit conversion, and sought up to one hundred and fifty billion dollars in damages.

In May twenty twenty-six, after an eleven-day trial in Oakland, California, the jury found that Musk had filed too late under the relevant statute of limitations. The judge dismissed the case. Musk's lawyers have said they will appeal.

Section. Products and the IPO horizon.

Open AI's main consumer product is Chat GPT (now backed by the GPT five family). Its main developer offering is the API, where developers can use the same models programmatically. Its main enterprise offering is Chat GPT Enterprise.

As of mid-twenty twenty-six, Open AI is reportedly in talks to go public at a valuation that could reach one trillion dollars. If that materializes, it would be one of the largest IPOs in history.

Section. What Open AI stands for, in twenty twenty-six.

In its public statements, Open AI's stated worldview is that artificial general intelligence is coming sooner rather than later, that safety is something to be addressed alongside aggressive deployment rather than before it, and that the way to make AI safe is to deploy it broadly so that the technology and its risks become well understood through use. Critics — including some former employees who left for Anthropic — argue this is rationalization for moving too fast. Defenders argue the alternative (delay) cedes the field to less safety-focused competitors.

Section. What to remember about Open AI in one paragraph.

Founded twenty fifteen as a non-profit. Converted to a capped-profit structure. Built the GPT line, launched Chat GPT in November twenty twenty-two, started the modern AI wave. Microsoft is its largest backer at over thirteen billion dollars. Sam Altman was fired and reinstated within four days in November twenty twenty-three. The Musk lawsuit was dismissed in May twenty twenty-six (under appeal). The company is moving toward an IPO at potentially a trillion-dollar valuation. Its stated philosophy: deploy aggressively, learn through use.

Anthropic, the Company That Broke Off Over Safety...

Section. Founding.

Anthropic was founded in twenty twenty-one by seven researchers who had left Open AI. The founding group included Dario Amodei (formerly VP of Research at Open AI) and his sister Daniela Amodei (now President of Anthropic), along with several other senior Open AI alumni. The stated reason for the departure was a disagreement about safety — specifically, the view that Open AI was moving too quickly relative to its safety research.

Anthropic was set up explicitly as a safety-focused AI lab. The plan was to participate at the frontier of capability so that safety research would be done on the same systems being deployed, rather than as a separate, slower track.

Section. Claude.

Anthropic's main product is the Claude family of assistants. Claude was first released in March twenty twenty-three. The Claude two, Claude three, and Claude three point five generations followed; by twenty twenty-six, Claude is generally considered one of the two or three strongest frontier models in the world.

Claude is trained using Constitutional AI (see chapter four) and is known for being relatively careful, willing to push back on the user, and willing to acknowledge uncertainty.

Section. The Claude Code moment, May twenty twenty-five.

In May twenty twenty-five, Anthropic launched Claude Code, an agentic coding tool designed for developers. Within months, it had generated over one billion dollars in annualized revenue — one of the fastest products to a billion in software history.

Within fifteen months of that launch, Anthropic's overall annualized revenue had jumped from a few hundred million to thirty billion dollars. Eighty times what it had been a year earlier.

Section. The valuation race.

By twenty twenty-six, Anthropic had become a roughly nine-hundred-billion-dollar company in private markets. Major backers include Google, which has committed up to forty billion dollars in investments, and Amazon, which has committed up to twenty-five billion. Anthropic has signed compute contracts with Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Broadcom, and — in a surprising arrangement — Elon Musk's Space X for additional capacity.

The full story of how a safety-focused lab founded by seven researchers became a nine-hundred-billion-dollar company in five years has not been fully told. The author's framing: faster than almost anyone outside its founders predicted, with all the complications that follow.

Section. The Pentagon designation, March twenty twenty-six.

In March twenty twenty-six, the United States Pentagon designated Anthropic a supply chain risk and blacklisted the company from working with the military. The dispute arose after contract negotiations broke down. The Pentagon wanted unrestricted use of Claude for all lawful military purposes; Anthropic refused to permit uses like fully autonomous weapons and domestic mass surveillance. When the company missed the government's deadline, the administration moved to bar federal agencies from using its technology. Anthropic has been challenging the designation in court while continuing its broader growth.

Section. Anthropic's stated worldview, twenty twenty-six.

Anthropic publicly argues that AI capabilities will continue to grow rapidly, that the alignment problem (getting models to do what we actually want) is genuinely hard and not solved, that the technology should be developed by labs that take safety seriously rather than left to those who don't, and that being at the frontier is the price of being able to do meaningful safety research. The lab invests heavily in interpretability research — the effort to understand what is happening inside a model — because it believes that without interpretability, claims about safety cannot really be verified.

Dario Amodei has publicly predicted that AI could wipe out half of entry-level white-collar jobs within five years and push unemployment into double digits. He has also written extensively (notably in "Machines of Loving Grace," twenty twenty-four) about the upside potential of safe AI for science and human welfare.

Section. What to remember about Anthropic in one paragraph.

Founded twenty twenty-one by seven ex-Open AI researchers, led by Dario and Daniela Amodei. Makes the Claude assistant. Constitutional AI is its alignment approach. Claude Code launched May twenty twenty-five and helped drive revenue from a few hundred million to thirty billion in fifteen months. Now valued near nine hundred billion. Major backers: Google up to forty billion, Amazon up to twenty-five billion. Pentagon designated it a supply-chain risk in March twenty twenty-six after Anthropic refused to permit autonomous-weapons and mass-surveillance uses; Anthropic is challenging in court. Stated philosophy: be at the frontier so safety research happens on real systems.

Google, the Giant That Almost Lost the Future...

Section. The paradox.

Google invented the architecture that powers the modern AI industry — the Transformer, in twenty seventeen, at Google Brain. Every major modern AI assistant is a descendant of that architecture. And yet by the time Chat GPT launched in November twenty twenty-two, Google was widely seen as being behind. The story of how that happened, and how Google has been trying to catch up since, is the story of this chapter.

Section. The two AI groups inside Google.

For most of the twenty tens and into the twenty twenties, Google had two distinct AI research organizations.

Google Brain, founded inside Google, produced the Transformer paper and much of the foundational work that the rest of the industry built on.
Deep Mind, acquired by Google in twenty fourteen for around five hundred million dollars, was founded in London by Demis Hassabis and others. It was famous for Alpha Go (the program that beat the world champion at Go in twenty sixteen), Alpha Fold (which solved a long-standing problem in protein structure prediction), and a strong research culture independent of Google's main businesses.

The two groups had distinct cultures, distinct strategies, and a real organizational rivalry. After Chat GPT, Google merged them into a single entity called Google Deep Mind, run by Demis Hassabis. The merger was completed in twenty twenty-three.

Section. The Nobel Prize, twenty twenty-four.

Demis Hassabis shared the twenty twenty-four Nobel Prize in Chemistry with John Jumper for Alpha Fold's work on protein folding. Alpha Fold predicted the three-dimensional structures of essentially all known proteins and made the predictions publicly available — a leap forward for structural biology. The award marked the first Nobel given for work substantially driven by AI.

Section. The Code Red, late twenty twenty-two.

When Chat GPT launched, Google's leadership reportedly declared an internal "code red." The concern was direct and existential: if conversational AI replaced search as the way most people looked up information, Google's core business — built on selling ads alongside search results — would erode rapidly.

Section. Bard, then Gemini.

Google's first public response was Bard, a Chat GPT competitor that launched in early twenty twenty-three. Bard had a rocky start, including a high-profile factual error in its initial public demonstration that wiped roughly one hundred billion dollars off Google's market cap in a single day.

In late twenty twenty-three Google rebranded the product line as Gemini and folded the underlying model work into a coherent family. Gemini one point zero, 1.5, and successive versions improved rapidly. By twenty twenty-six, Gemini is one of the strongest frontier model families, with a particular strength in extremely long context windows — the Gemini line was among the first to ship one-million-token and two-million-token context windows, enough to fit entire books or large codebases in a single conversation.

Section. Distribution as Google's advantage.

Google's biggest competitive asset is not its model technology — which is competitive but not always the absolute frontier — but its distribution. AI capabilities are now integrated into Google Search (AI Overviews, then a successor AI mode), Workspace (Gmail, Docs, Meet), Android, Chrome, YouTube, and Maps. Billions of users encounter Google AI dozens of times a day, whether or not they think of themselves as using AI.

Section. The talent diaspora from Google.

A pattern worth knowing: many of the people who built the foundational technology at Google have since left to compete with it. All eight authors of the original Transformer paper had left Google within eight years of publication. Aidan Gomez co-founded Cohere. Several went to Open AI or Anthropic or started other ventures. The same broad pattern holds across the rest of Google's AI staff. Some of it is the gravitational pull of Open AI and Anthropic; some of it is the differential between Google's pay scale and what frontier startups can offer.

Google has been working to slow the bleeding by paying large retention packages and giving senior researchers more autonomy inside Deep Mind. The success of those efforts is hard to evaluate from outside.

Section. What Google stands for in AI, twenty twenty-six.

Google's stated philosophy is closer to a careful-deployment position than Open AI's deploy-aggressively or Anthropic's safety-first. Google emphasizes integration into existing products, regulatory cooperation, and broad scientific contributions like Alpha Fold. Critics argue that Google's caution is mostly a function of having a giant existing business to protect; defenders argue that Google has earned the right to its approach through decades of building useful products.

Section. What to remember about Google in one paragraph.

Invented the Transformer in twenty seventeen, then was outmaneuvered by smaller companies built on top of it. Owns Deep Mind (acquired twenty fourteen) and merged Brain into it in twenty twenty-three. Demis Hassabis won the twenty twenty-four Nobel Prize in Chemistry for Alpha Fold. The Gemini model family is now one of the strongest, especially at long context. Distribution — Search, Workspace, Android, Chrome, YouTube — is Google's biggest AI advantage. The original eight Transformer authors had all left Google within eight years.

Meta, the Open Bet That Changed Everything...

Section. The scale of Meta.

Meta is the parent company of Facebook, Instagram, Whats App, and Threads. About three billion people use one of those products every day. Whatever you think about Meta as a company, the scale of its reach is undisputed. Meta has more direct access to global communication than any organization in history.

Section. The open-weight bet, twenty twenty-three to twenty twenty-five.

Meta's distinctive contribution to the AI industry from twenty twenty-three through early twenty twenty-six was releasing frontier-class models with open weights — meaning anyone could download the model and run it on their own hardware. The Llama series was the vehicle.

Llama one, released February twenty twenty-three (initially leaked, then officially released). Showed that competitive models could be open.
Llama two, released July twenty twenty-three, with a commercial-use license.
Llama three, released April twenty twenty-four and updated through the year. By the end of twenty twenty-four, Llama three was the most widely deployed open-weight model in the world. Whole ecosystems of fine-tunes, specialized variants, and downstream products grew on top of it.

The strategic case Mark Zuckerberg made for openness was that releasing weights would commoditize a layer of the AI stack that Meta did not need to own, accelerate research that Meta benefited from, and prevent any single competitor (notably Google or Open AI) from controlling the foundation.

Section. The Llama four disaster, April twenty twenty-five.

Meta released Llama four in April twenty twenty-five. The launch was later found to have used different model versions for different benchmarks, gaming the results — different model versions were submitted to leaderboards than were released publicly. Internal consequences were severe. Public confidence in Meta's model line dropped sharply. The episode contributed to the leadership changes that followed.

Section. The Superintelligence Labs pivot, mid-twenty twenty-five.

In mid-twenty twenty-five, Meta announced the formation of Meta Superintelligence Labs — the name itself a statement of intent. To lead it, Zuckerberg paid an astonishing price. He spent about fourteen point three billion dollars to acquire forty-nine percent of Scale AI, a company that provided data labeling services for AI training. As part of the deal, Scale's twenty-eight-year-old cofounder, Alexandr Wang, joined Meta as chief AI officer.

The cultural friction was, by all accounts, real. The sixty-five-year-old Turing Award winner Yann Le Cun, who had been Meta's chief AI scientist for years, on paper reported to a twenty-eight-year-old executive. Le Cun had publicly described large language models as a "dead end" for reaching true AI. Wang was building Meta's strategy entirely around large language models.

Section. The Le Cun departure, November twenty twenty-five.

Yann Le Cun left Meta in November twenty twenty-five to found a new lab called AMI Labs, focused on world-model approaches to AI rather than language-model approaches. AMI stands for Advanced Machine Intelligence. Le Cun's thesis: real general intelligence will require models that learn how the physical world works, not just how text works.

Section. Muse Spark and the pivot to closed weights, early twenty twenty-six.

In March twenty twenty-six, Meta launched Muse Spark — its first major model after the Le Cun departure and the Wang takeover. Muse Spark was not open-weight. Access was limited to Meta's own products and a small invite-only group of partners. The company that had defined the open-weight ecosystem had, with this release, chosen to play by the same rules as Open AI and Anthropic.

The reasons were not made fully public, but the implication was clear. Competitive pressure had become too intense. The risk of releasing weights that competitors — particularly Chinese labs — could clone and build on had become too great. After Chinese labs had built on Llama to produce models competing with Meta's own offerings, the strategic logic of giving everything away started to look different.

Section. Capex and layoffs, twenty twenty-six.

Meta's twenty twenty-six capital expenditure budget is expected to be between one hundred fifteen and one hundred thirty-five billion dollars, almost all of it AI-related. They have announced fifteen thousand layoffs alongside the AI restructuring, redistributing the resources from cut roles into AI infrastructure and talent.

Section. What Meta believes now.

The new leadership's stated views: capability comes from scale, data quality, and rapid iteration; open-weight releases are a strategic tool, not a moral commitment; Meta's distribution scale across three billion users matters more than any single model; the company is hedging on world-model research alongside its language-model work.

Section. What to remember about Meta in one paragraph.

Three billion daily users gives it unmatched distribution. Defined the open-weight ecosystem with Llama one, 2, 3 in twenty twenty-three to twenty twenty-four. Llama four in April twenty twenty-five was a disaster — different model versions submitted to benchmarks than released. Meta Superintelligence Labs formed mid-twenty twenty-five around Scale AI's Alexandr Wang (twenty-eight years old), brought in via a fourteen-point-three-billion-dollar deal for forty-nine percent of Scale. Yann Le Cun left in November twenty twenty-five to found AMI Labs. Muse Spark in March twenty twenty-six was Meta's first closed-weight release — the company that had championed open weights stepped back. twenty twenty-six capex is one hundred fifteen to one hundred thirty-five billion dollars. Fifteen thousand layoffs announced.

The Other Half of the Map (China)...

Section. The Deep Seek moment, January twenty twenty-five.

On a Sunday in late January twenty twenty-five, a Chinese company called Deep Seek released a chatbot to the public. Within forty-eight hours, the Deep Seek app had reached number one on Apple's App Store in the United States, ahead of Chat GPT. Nvidia lost five hundred billion dollars of market value in a single day. Trading desks across Wall Street called emergency meetings.

Deep Seek had released a model that performed roughly on par with the best American models — but its final training run had reportedly cost about six million dollars. American labs were spending hundreds of millions, sometimes billions, on equivalent training runs. The model was open-weight. The API pricing was a fraction of Open AI's. The model was, in operational terms, just better at certain kinds of reasoning than what was available from anyone else.

The Deep Seek moment forced the Western AI conversation to acknowledge a fact it had been avoiding: the half of the AI map that Western media had been ignoring was, in many ways, ahead of the half they had been watching.

Section. Why China caught up faster than expected.

Two pieces of context explain the trajectory.

First, China has been investing heavily in AI research for over a decade. By the late twenty tens, Chinese universities were producing more AI research papers than American universities, often with citation counts that rivaled the best American work. Chinese AI talent has been at the cutting edge for years.

Second, since October twenty twenty-two, the United States has imposed sweeping export controls on advanced computer chips. The Nvidia chips that American labs use to train models were forbidden from sale to Chinese companies. The reasoning was that if AI was strategically critical, China should not have it.

The export controls did not work as intended. They forced Chinese labs to innovate in different directions — to write better software, design better architectures, train smarter rather than harder. The result was a generation of Chinese techniques often more efficient than their American equivalents. The constraint produced the breakthrough.

Section. The main labs.

Deep Seek. Owned by High-Flyer, a Chinese hedge fund. Famous for releasing extremely efficient open-weight models at very low prices. Their V four model, released in April twenty twenty-six, comes in two versions — including a 1.6 trillion parameter variant that competes with the best American closed-source offerings, at roughly one-tenth the price. Deep Seek V four Flash costs about twenty-eight cents per million output tokens.
Alibaba. The Chinese e-commerce giant. Their AI division produces the Qwen series of models. As of mid-twenty twenty-six, Qwen has become the most-downloaded open-weight model family in the world, ahead of Meta's Llama. The Qwen app has over one hundred million monthly active users in China and growing markets in Asia and Africa.
Moonshot AI. A Beijing-based startup founded by former Tsinghua University researchers. Produces the Kimi model series. Particularly strong on long-context understanding and on agentic AI. Kimi K two point six, released April twenty twenty-six, scored competitively with Claude and GPT five on coding benchmarks.
Zhipu AI. Another Beijing-based lab. Produces the GLM series. Was China's first large language model developer to list publicly, with a Hong Kong IPO in January twenty twenty-six.
Byte Dance. Parent of Tik Tok. Has released the Seed and Doubao model series. Distribution advantages through Tik Tok give them a uniquely powerful platform.
Baidu. China's largest search engine. Their Ernie chatbot was one of the first major Chinese consumer AI products. Less prominent at the frontier in twenty twenty-six than they were in twenty twenty-three, but still significant.
Tencent. Owner of Wee Chat. Has integrated AI into its products at enormous scale. Distributed over one hundred forty million dollars in cash bonuses through their AI chatbot during the twenty twenty-six Lunar New Year.
Also: Mini Max, 01.AI, Step Fun, and others.

Section. Architectural innovations.

The Deep Seek architecture innovations, particularly something called Multi-Head Latent Attention, are now studied by researchers everywhere. The broad pattern: Chinese labs lead on efficiency, agentic capability, and aggressive pricing.

Section. The Huawei Ascend hardware question.

The Huawei Ascend chips, designed in China to replace Nvidia chips that cannot be imported, have improved significantly. Deep Seek V four was trained largely on Ascend hardware. The American export controls were supposed to prevent this. The Chinese semiconductor industry has accelerated under the pressure of the controls, and the gap between Chinese and American AI hardware is narrowing.

Section. The censorship reality.

Chinese AI models have hard limits Western models do not. All of them have built-in content restrictions on topics sensitive to the Chinese government. They will not discuss Taiwan's political status the way Western models will. They will not discuss the nineteen eighty-nine events at Tiananmen Square. They will produce content aligned with Chinese government positions rather than providing neutral information.

This is not a quirk. It is a legal requirement. Chinese AI companies operate under regulations requiring their products to "uphold core socialist values" and to refrain from "subverting state power." The censorship is built in, both technically and culturally. Even when models are released open-weight, the censorship is baked into the training data and the alignment process.

Section. Where Chinese AI is being used most.

Deep Seek usage in Africa is, by some estimates, two to four times higher than in other regions — partly because of the price, partly because of open-weight availability, partly because of the lighter regulatory footprint. In much of Africa, Latin America, Southeast Asia, and parts of Europe, Chinese AI may already be a significant part of daily AI consumption.

Section. The geopolitical frame.

The competition is not just commercial rivalry. It is part of a slow technology cold war between two superpowers. Both governments believe whoever leads in AI will have significant strategic advantages. Both use policy tools to advance their domestic AI industries and constrain the other's. The Chinese labs sit inside this larger context as commercial companies and, in various ways, as instruments of national strategy. The same is true, in fairness, of American AI labs.

Section. What to remember about China's AI in one paragraph.

Deep Seek's January twenty twenty-five release of a six-million-dollar-trained competitive model caused Nvidia to lose five hundred billion in market cap in a day. Six or seven major labs matter: Deep Seek, Alibaba's Qwen (most-downloaded open weights), Moonshot's Kimi, Zhipu's GLM, Byte Dance, Baidu, Tencent. Chinese labs lead on efficiency, open-weight releases, agentic capability, and aggressive pricing. US export controls forced innovation rather than preventing it. Huawei Ascend chips now train frontier models. Content restrictions (Taiwan, Tiananmen, "core socialist values") are legal requirements baked into models. Western businesses generally cannot use Chinese AI for compliance reasons. The multipolar AI future is now structural.

Everyone Else...

Section. Microsoft, the company that wraps Open AI.

Microsoft does not make a frontier AI model of its own that matters at the consumer level. The AI in Microsoft products is, in almost every case, an Open AI model with a Microsoft system prompt and Microsoft branding around it.

Microsoft Copilot, the consumer chatbot in Windows and Edge, is Open AI's GPT models with a Microsoft wrapper. Microsoft 365 Copilot, the AI inside Word, Excel, Outlook, Power Point, and Teams, is also primarily Open AI-powered. Git Hub Copilot, the developer tool, started as pure Open AI but has become more interesting — as of twenty twenty-four and twenty twenty-five, developers can choose between GPT, Claude, and Gemini in Git Hub Copilot.

The Microsoft-Open AI relationship is the largest commercial deal in AI history. Microsoft has invested over thirteen billion dollars in Open AI. The partnership has made Microsoft the largest AI distributor in the world — about two billion people use Microsoft software in some form every month. Microsoft has also been quietly hedging: it has hired Mustafa Suleyman (cofounder of Deep Mind, founder of Inflection) to lead Microsoft AI, and is training its own in-house model called MAI.

Section. X AI, the lab that became a space company.

In March twenty twenty-three, Elon Musk founded a new AI company called X AI. He recruited eleven researchers from Google, Open AI, and Microsoft. The pitch was "maximum truth-seeking AI" — positioned as an alternative to what Musk described as the political correctness of the existing labs.

The product is Grok, launched first on X (formerly Twitter) in late twenty twenty-three. Grok had access to real-time content flowing through X, which gave it an edge on current events. Musk modeled the personality on the irreverent voice of Douglas Adams's The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy.

X AI grew fast. By twenty twenty-five Musk had built a supercomputer called Colossus in Memphis, Tennessee, with over two hundred thousand GPUs. By early twenty twenty-six it had grown to over five hundred thousand GPUs, expanding toward one million — the largest dedicated AI training facility in the world.

The corporate trajectory took unusual turns. In March twenty twenty-five X AI acquired X, the social media platform, for thirty-three billion dollars in stock. In February twenty twenty-six, in an even more unusual move, Space X acquired X AI. The combined entity was valued at about one and a quarter trillion dollars. In May twenty twenty-six Musk announced X AI would cease to exist as a separate company — Grok and X were now part of a Space X division called Space X AI.

Section. Mistral, the European challenger.

Mistral was founded in twenty twenty-three by three researchers who had worked at Meta and Google Deep Mind. It is based in France. The argument: AI is too important to be controlled entirely by American and Chinese companies; European data-protection norms deserve to be reflected in major models; a real commercial opportunity exists outside the Silicon Valley orbit.

Mistral committed to open-weight releases when most American labs were closing up. It emphasized efficiency. It built its own products including a chatbot called Le Chat, but also positioned itself as a model provider for European companies that did not want to send data to American clouds.

By twenty twenty-six Mistral is significant. Annual recurring revenue grew from twenty million dollars in early twenty twenty-five to four hundred million dollars in early twenty twenty-six. They raised eight hundred thirty million dollars in a single round. Valuation reached about fourteen billion dollars. In March twenty twenty-six alone they released six major products in fifteen days, including Voxtral, an audio model competing with Eleven Labs.

Data processed by Mistral stays under European jurisdiction, simplifying GDPR compliance.

Section. Perplexity, the wrapper company.

Perplexity is a search engine that uses AI to answer questions in natural language, with citations. Under the hood it uses whatever model is best for the task — sometimes Open AI, sometimes Anthropic, sometimes Mistral. It routes queries to different models based on what each is good at.

What Perplexity demonstrates: a real market exists for AI products that don't require building your own model. The "wrapper" layer — companies building products on top of frontier models — is where most users actually meet AI. Cursor (coding), Granola (meeting notes), Lindy (workflows), and hundreds of others sit in this layer.

Section. Amazon, the enabler.

Amazon is the largest cloud-computing company through AWS, hosting roughly a third of all internet infrastructure. Amazon has its own models — the Titan and Nova series, used in Alexa — but its bigger play is making everyone else's models available.

Amazon Bedrock lets developers use models from many labs: Anthropic, AI twenty-one, Cohere, Stability, Mistral, and others. Amazon takes a margin on the inference. Amazon's biggest single AI bet has been on Anthropic — up to twenty-five billion dollars committed, making them one of the two largest backers alongside Google. Amazon's strategy: be the place where models live. Picks-and-shovels in the AI gold rush.

Section. Apple, the late arrival.

Apple is one of the most valuable companies in the world and almost uniquely behind on AI among the giants. When Chat GPT launched, Siri was widely regarded as obsolete. For about eighteen months Apple watched competitors race ahead. In June twenty twenty-four they announced Apple Intelligence; the rollout was modest and the promised Chat GPT integration underwhelmed.

By twenty twenty-six Apple's AI position remains distinctive in a few ways: heavy investment in on-device AI (small models running locally on iPhones); licensed external models including Gemini from Google for tasks their own models cannot handle; a privacy-focused positioning, arguing AI should run on your device rather than send data to corporate servers. Whether this strategy works is uncertain.

Section. The smaller players worth knowing.

Cohere. Canadian, enterprise-focused. Founded by Aidan Gomez (one of the eight Transformer-paper authors).
Inflection. Founded twenty twenty-two by Mustafa Suleyman. Its consumer chatbot Pi was known for warm tone. In March twenty twenty-four Microsoft hired most of Inflection's team and licensed the technology, leaving the corporate shell intact but stripped of its people. Suleyman now runs Microsoft AI.
IBM. Watson won Jeopardy in twenty eleven. IBM has largely faded from the consumer AI conversation but continues significant enterprise AI work in regulated industries.
Hugging Face. Not technically an AI lab. It is the platform where most of the open-weight ecosystem lives — releases from Meta, Mistral, Chinese labs, and thousands of independent researchers.
Stability AI (Stable Diffusion), Black Forest Labs (Flux), Runway (AI video), Suno (AI music), Eleven Labs (voice).

Section. What to remember about Chapter 10 in one paragraph.

Microsoft mostly wraps Open AI, has invested over thirteen billion, runs Mustafa Suleyman's Microsoft AI as a hedge. X AI is now part of Space X as Space X AI; Colossus supercomputer in Memphis is over five hundred thousand GPUs heading toward a million. Mistral (French, founded twenty twenty-three) is the leading European lab, four hundred million ARR, fourteen billion valuation. Perplexity routes across models — exemplifies the wrapper economy. Amazon is picks-and-shovels with Bedrock and a twenty-five-billion bet on Anthropic. Apple is deliberately behind, betting on on-device AI and privacy. Cohere, Hugging Face, Stability, Runway, Suno, Eleven Labs, IBM's Watson legacy round out the map.

What These Machines Are Genuinely Good At...

Section. The wrong default use.

Most people use AI assistants as search engines — asking factual lookup questions. This is what they were taught by twenty years of typing queries into Google. It's also where AI is least reliable. The right tool for "what's the population of Brazil" is still a search engine, supplemented by AI when you want synthesis rather than a list of links.

AI is genuinely good at something else. Seven somethings, in the author's framing.

Section. 1. Writing that you can steer.

The single best use of AI today is writing tasks where the human knows roughly what they want to say but doesn't want to do all the typing — drafting an email, outlining a presentation, writing a job description, drafting a contract from a template, rewriting a paragraph in a different register, translating into clearer language, tightening or expanding.

The pattern: human provides goal, audience, constraints; AI produces first draft; human edits. The result is yours, produced faster than from scratch.

The misuse: letting AI do the thinking, not just the typing. That produces fluent but soulless writing — anyone-could-have-written-this prose.

Section. 2. Translation and rewording.

Across languages, modern AI translation has surpassed general-purpose machine translation in quality. On the W M T twenty twenty-four industry benchmark, Claude three point five ranked first in nine of eleven language pairs in twenty twenty-four, ahead of GPT four and the dedicated translation engines tested at the time. In a separate Lokalise blind study in twenty twenty-five, professional translators rated Claude's output as acceptable seventy-eight percent of the time, the highest of any tool tested. Google Translate is still useful for quick lookups across a wider set of languages. DeepL still wins for formal written content in European pairs.

Within a single language, AI is excellent at translating between registers — dense legal language to plain English, plain English notes to formal corporate memo, technical specification to non-technical executive summary. Most professional work involves this kind of register translation.

Section. 3. Analyzing something you provide.

The biggest single capability shift, in the author's view. Paste a document, a contract, a transcript, a research paper, a confusing email — then ask questions about it. The model reads carefully, structures, summarizes, extracts what you need. Reliable in a way that pure recall is not, because the model is working from the source material rather than memory.

This is the technique that turns AI from a curiosity into a productivity tool for knowledge workers. Most knowledge work involves reading something and producing structured output. AI is very good at that loop.

Section. 4. Code and technical work.

AI assistants in twenty twenty-six can write working code in dozens of languages, debug, explain, translate between languages, refactor, write tests, generate configuration and boilerplate. The twenty twenty-five shift was from "AI writes a snippet you paste" to "AI works alongside you continuously with access to your project" — Git Hub Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code.

The honest numbers, drawn from the best research available:

A McKinsey survey of forty-five hundred developers and a DX study of over one hundred thirty-five thousand developers showed AI coding tools reduce time on routine coding tasks by roughly forty-six percent and save the average developer about three and a half hours a week.
Daily AI users merge sixty percent more pull requests than non-users.
By early twenty twenty-six, AI-authored code makes up around twenty-seven percent of all production code, up from twenty-two percent just one quarter earlier.
Eighty-four percent of developers report using or planning to use AI in development.

The honest qualifier: individual developer output rising does not always translate into proportional business gains. The METR study, the most rigorous randomized controlled trial to date, has shown that under certain conditions experienced developers can actually be slower with AI tools, because review time eats into produce-time savings.

For non-developers, AI has made small programming tasks — spreadsheet formulas, simple websites, automation scripts — accessible to people who would never have tried them before.

Section. 5. Brainstorming and thinking partners.

A tireless, knowledgeable-across-domains, no-ego, three-in-the-morning-available thinking partner. The use case is conversation, not query: bring an idea, describe what you're trying to figure out, ask for pushback, ask for other angles, disagree, refine. The act of articulating an idea to something that responds intelligently is itself generative.

When used well: best application of the technology, in the author's view. When used badly: produces the illusion of thinking rather than the real thing.

Section. 6. Teaching and explaining.

A great tutor calibrates to the student. A great tutor produces explanations on demand, in different formats, until the student understands. Great tutors are expensive. AI tutors are not. The technique: tell the AI your starting point, ask it to teach you, push back when it goes too fast or too slow, ask for analogies, examples, counterexamples, ask it to test you.

The asymmetry of access to good teachers — a fundamental feature of human inequality — is changing, slowly and imperfectly, because of AI tutors. Doesn't replace good schools. Does make a much better educational floor available to anyone with an internet connection.

Section. 7. Structured extraction.

Most of the world's information lives in messy formats — emails, PDFs, photos of documents, voice memos, handwritten notes, inconsistent spreadsheets, customer service transcripts, free-text survey responses. AI is transformative at turning messy unstructured input into clean structured output: a list, a table, a specific format with specific fields.

A lawyer with three hundred discovery documents can have AI identify which mention a particular party and what they say. A researcher with sixty interview transcripts can have AI identify common themes and pull the most relevant quotes from each. A salesperson with two hundred customer emails can have AI sort by priority and surface common requests.

This is the use case that surprises new users most. People expect AI to write essays; they don't expect it to take messy input and produce a neat output matching a specific structure they describe.

Section. The pattern across all seven.

In every case, the user provides judgment, context, and verification. The AI provides scale, speed, and breadth. The user decides what should be said. The AI drafts. The user verifies. The combination is more productive than either alone.

Anyone who tells you AI is useless is using it wrong. Anyone who tells you AI replaces human judgment is using it wrong in the other direction.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 11 in one paragraph.

Seven uses where AI is genuinely good: drafting writing you steer; translation across languages (Claude three point five best in 9/11 W M T twenty twenty-four pairs, 78% acceptable per Lokalise twenty twenty-five) and across registers; analyzing documents you provide; coding (forty-six percent time reduction, three and a half hours saved per week, twenty-seven percent of production code now AI-authored, eighty-four percent of developers using AI); brainstorming and thinking partnership; teaching and tutoring; structured extraction from messy input. The shared pattern: human brings judgment and verification, AI brings scale and speed.

How These Machines Fail...

Section. The Schwartz case.

In early twenty twenty-three, a New York lawyer named Steven Schwartz used Chat GPT to help him with a legal brief. He worked at the firm Levidow, Levidow and Oberman, had been licensed to practice law in New York for over thirty years, and was representing a client named Roberto Mata, who was suing the airline Avianca over an injury from a metal serving cart striking his knee during a flight. Schwartz asked Chat GPT to find prior cases that would support his argument against the airline's motion to dismiss.

Chat GPT obliged with six cases — Varghese versus China Southern Airlines, Shaboon versus Egypt Air, Petersen versus Iran Air, Martinez versus Delta Airlines, Estate of Durden versus KLM Royal Dutch Airlines, and Miller versus United Airlines — each with a name, citation, court, date, and plausible legal reasoning. Schwartz's colleague Peter Lo Duca filed the brief incorporating them.

None of the cases existed. Chat GPT had invented them. When Avianca's lawyers tried to find them, they could not. When the court asked Schwartz and Lo Duca to produce copies of the decisions, Schwartz asked Chat GPT for them, and Chat GPT generated fake copies, complete with fabricated opinion text. These were submitted too.

Judge Kevin Castel of the Southern District of New York held a sanctions hearing. Schwartz testified that he had been "operating under the false perception" that Chat GPT could not fabricate cases. The judge imposed a five-thousand-dollar sanction on Schwartz, Lo Duca, and the law firm. The case became the most-cited example of why you cannot trust AI for professional work without verification.

Section. The five failure modes.

The author identifies five major ways AI assistants fail. Understanding each is the most important practical knowledge a user can have.

1. Hallucination.

The model produces, with complete confidence, things that are not true. Not random gibberish — confident, well-formed, plausible-sounding statements that happen to be false. The model produces them in the same voice it uses for true statements; the user cannot tell from the output which is which.

The mechanism: the model is a statistical pattern matcher with no fact-checker. It produces words that are likely given the context. For most questions, likely is also correct. For some, it isn't. The model has no internal way to distinguish.

Where hallucinations live: specific facts about specific (especially less-famous) people, citations and references, precise numbers and statistics, recent events past the training cutoff, anything obscure, things the user clearly wants to be true.

2. Sycophancy.

The model agrees with you too much. RLHF rewards responses humans prefer; humans prefer agreement and validation; the model picks up a subtle bias toward telling you what you want to hear. By default the model is in support mode — it will validate premises without checking them.

The fix is in your prompt, not in the model. Ask for criticism. Ask for the strongest counterargument. Ask what a skeptic would say. Ask what could go wrong. The capacity to push back is there; you have to invite it.

3. Reasoning that looks like thinking.

The model produces step-by-step reasoning that sounds rigorous. Sometimes the steps actually connect and the conclusion follows. Sometimes the model has produced a confident answer first and then generated plausible-looking justifications for it — what researchers call rationalization. The language is fluent either way. You cannot tell sound argument from plausible fabrication from the text alone.

Implication: trust AI reasoning as a draft, not a verdict. Particularly unreliable for math — the numbers look right and are sometimes wrong by significant amounts.

4. Stale world.

Every model has a training cutoff. After that date the model knows nothing about world events unless given real-time tools. The model's knowledge has soft edges, not hard ones, because some post-cutoff information seeps in through later fine-tuning and some pre-cutoff information is poorly represented.

Modern AI assistants increasingly have web search, but they don't always choose to use it. Watch for phrases like "as of my training data" — those are honest signals the answer may be stale.

5. Refusal.

Sometimes the model refuses to do something it should be willing to do. Asks for medical information get deflected to "see a doctor" without actually answering; security research questions get treated as attacks; fiction involving violence gets declined. These refusals usually come from over-applied safety training or conservative system prompts. Different assistants have different refusal thresholds — switching tools is sometimes the right move.

Section. How to use AI without becoming a casualty.

Six habits separate users who get value from users who get hurt.

1. Never use AI for a fact you need to be true without verifying it. The fact might be right, wrong, or invented. Until you check, you don't know. For anything that matters, check.
2. Ask the AI to push back. "What's the strongest counter-argument?" "What could I be missing?" "How could this go wrong?" Sycophancy is a default, not a fixed setting. You can talk past it.
3. Treat AI reasoning as a draft, not a verdict. Use the steps as a starting point for your own thinking. Check whether each step actually follows.
4. Calibrate to the topic. More reliable on well-established topics with abundant training data; less reliable on obscure topics, recent events, specific people, precise numbers, citations.
5. Learn to recognize when the AI is uncertain. Ask directly: "How confident are you?" "What are the chances this is wrong?" "Where would a skeptic challenge this?"
6. Use multiple sources. For anything important, verify against at least one other source — search, textbook, real human who knows.

Section. What to remember about Chapter 12 in one paragraph.

Steven Schwartz, early twenty twenty-three, NY lawyer at Levidow Levidow & Oberman, submitted a brief with six fake cases (Varghese, Shaboon, Petersen, Martinez, Durden, Miller versus United Airlines) that Chat GPT had invented; Judge Kevin Castel fined them five thousand dollars. Five failure modes: hallucination (confident fabrication), sycophancy (agreeing too much), reasoning-as-rationalization, stale world (training cutoff), refusal (over-applied safety). The fix is in the user, not the model: verify, ask for pushback, treat reasoning as draft, calibrate, ask for uncertainty, use multiple sources.
