  Chapter Seven

  Google, the Giant That Almost Lost the Future

  They built the architecture that powers every AI assistant in the world. Then they sat on it.

Let me tell you a story that's almost too on the nose to be true.

In 2017, eight researchers at Google published the paper we keep coming back to in this book. The one titled "Attention Is All You Need." The one that introduced the Transformer. The one that became the foundation for every AI assistant you've ever used.

Those eight researchers worked at Google. They published the paper through Google. Google paid for the research. Google owned the patents. The breakthrough was, by any reasonable measure, Google's breakthrough.

They went on to found or join companies that, collectively, are now worth hundreds of billions of dollars. They built products that compete with Google. They reshaped the industry that Google had quietly dominated for two decades. And they did it all using the architecture they had invented while working at Google, but which Google itself, for several crucial years, did not turn into a product.

This is one of the great unforced errors in the history of business.

It is also, I will argue in this chapter, more complicated than it sounds. Because while Google was slow to ship a chatbot, it was not slow on AI overall. The company was, and is, doing some of the most ambitious AI research in the world. Demis Hassabis, who runs Google's AI efforts, shared a Nobel Prize in 2024 with his colleague John Jumper for their work on AlphaFold, an AI system that predicts protein structures. That work, if it pays off the way it might, could transform medicine in ways that make ChatGPT look small.

But Google did stumble badly on consumer AI assistants, in a way that left billions of dollars on the table and gave their competitors a head start they may never fully overcome. This chapter is about how a giant nearly lost the future, and what they have done to claw it back.

The company before the stumble.

To understand why Google's failure matters, you have to understand what Google was, and is.

Google has been the most important AI company in the world for much longer than most people realize. They started in 1998 with an algorithm that ranked web pages. That algorithm was, in its way, an early piece of AI. By the 2010s, Google was using neural networks to translate languages, recognize speech, identify images, recommend videos, and target ads. The company's products quietly ran on machine learning for years before "AI" became a buzzword.

In 2014, Google made one of the most consequential acquisitions in technology history. They bought a small London-based AI research lab called DeepMind for about six hundred and fifty million dollars. DeepMind was the brainchild of Demis Hassabis, a former chess prodigy turned neuroscientist turned AI researcher. The lab had a singular focus: solving general intelligence. Not chatbots. Not search engines. General intelligence.

Mark Zuckerberg, at Facebook, had also tried to acquire DeepMind. He offered more money. Hassabis chose Google anyway, partly because Google had the compute resources DeepMind needed, and partly because Larry Page agreed to keep DeepMind operationally independent in London.

For most of the next decade, DeepMind worked on hard problems. AlphaGo, which beat the world champion at Go in 2016. AlphaZero, which taught itself chess and Go and shogi from scratch. AlphaFold, which solved a fifty-year-old grand challenge in biology by predicting how proteins fold. This last achievement is what won Hassabis and his colleague John Jumper a share of the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2024.

Meanwhile, inside Google, a separate AI research group called Google Brain was doing the work that produced the Transformer. The two groups, DeepMind in London and Google Brain in Mountain View, did world-class research in parallel.

And that, in retrospect, was part of the problem.

THE STUMBLE

When ChatGPT launched in November 2022, Google's response was slow.

The company had the technology to build a competing product. It had, internally, models that were arguably better than what OpenAI had released. But Google had not deployed those models as a consumer chatbot. There were several reasons. Concerns about brand safety. Concerns about the impact on Google's core search business. Concerns about the cost of running a chatbot at Google scale. And honest concerns about whether the technology was ready for the public.

These concerns were not crazy. Some of them, in retrospect, were genuinely well-founded. But the result was that for several critical months, Google watched OpenAI define the consumer category. Watched ChatGPT become a household name. Watched Microsoft, which had invested in OpenAI, start integrating GPT into Bing and into Office.

In December 2022, Google declared a "code red." In early 2023, they hurriedly released their own chatbot, called Bard. Bard's launch demo, at a press event, included a factual error that wiped one hundred billion dollars off Alphabet's stock value in a single day. The Bard product itself was widely regarded as inferior to ChatGPT. Google rebranded it to "Gemini" a year later, hoping to escape the bad associations of the Bard name.

For about eighteen months, the narrative around Google was brutal. Here was the company that had invented the underlying architecture, that owned the entire AI research stack, that had more data than anyone, more compute than anyone, more engineering talent than anyone. And they were being humiliated, in public, by a former non-profit a fraction of their size.

I want to flag that this narrative was, in important ways, incomplete. Google had not actually stopped doing serious AI work. Their products, like Google Translate and Google Photos and YouTube recommendations, continued to improve. AlphaFold was making real scientific contributions. DeepMind was doing research that nobody else was doing. The narrative of "Google fell behind" was true if you measured by "consumer chatbots" and false if you measured by "research depth."

But the public was measuring by chatbots. And by that measure, Google was losing. Badly.

THE COMEBACK

In April 2023, Google did something that, in hindsight, was probably necessary. They merged the two AI research groups. Google Brain and DeepMind became "Google DeepMind." Demis Hassabis took charge of the combined operation. The era of parallel research with internal coordination problems was supposed to be over.

The product wins started coming.

Gemini 1.0 launched in December 2023. It was a real model, multimodal from the start, designed to compete head-on with GPT-4. The first version was uneven. The second was better. By the time Gemini 2.5 shipped in March 2025, Google was, by Hassabis's own description, "in our groove" again. In November 2025, they launched Gemini 3, which was the first Google AI product to be widely regarded as on par with or better than OpenAI's flagship offerings.

By early 2026, Google's position looked entirely different than it had in 2023.

The Gemini app had over seven hundred and fifty million monthly active users, putting it in the same scale conversation as ChatGPT. Google's first-party AI models, embedded across Search, YouTube, Workspace, and dozens of other products, were processing more than ten billion tokens per minute. Alphabet's revenue in the fourth quarter of 2025 was nearly one hundred and fourteen billion dollars. Their stock had its best year since 2009.

The company was, in many ways, doing what it had always done well. Quiet, methodical, infrastructure-heavy execution. Once the strategy was clear, once the organizational confusion was resolved, Google had advantages no startup could match. Their custom chips, called TPUs, are designed specifically for the kind of computation AI models do. Their global infrastructure can deliver AI products to billions of users without breaking. Their existing products, used by huge fractions of humanity, give them distribution channels nobody else has.

For 2026, Alphabet is planning to spend somewhere between one hundred and seventy-five and one hundred and eighty-five billion dollars on capital expenditures, almost entirely on AI infrastructure. That number is, by itself, larger than the total valuation of most companies in the AI race. Google may have stumbled. But once it found its footing, the scale of its commitment was unlike anything any competitor could match.

The two rooms.

There's something specific about Google's position that distinguishes it from OpenAI and Anthropic, and it's worth naming. Google has two very different rooms in which it is doing AI.

The first room is the consumer chatbot room. Gemini, ChatGPT, Claude, all sit in this room. They compete on benchmarks. They compete on consumer features. They argue about who has the best coding model this week. This room is loud, fast, and the lead changes every few months. Google is doing fine in this room now, though not yet dominant.

The second room is the scientific AI room. AlphaFold and its successors. Isomorphic Labs, which is Hassabis's drug discovery spinoff, now working on cancer treatments with major pharmaceutical partners. The work using AI to understand protein folding, RNA structure, gene therapy, and materials science. This room is quieter. The wins compound over years, not weeks. And in this room, Google is genuinely ahead of everyone else.

The dual nature of Google's AI work means the company is, in a real sense, betting on two different futures. The chatbot future, in which AI assistants become the way people interact with computers. The scientific future, in which AI accelerates the rate of discovery in medicine, materials, and climate research. Both futures could happen. Both could be enormous.

I find this combination genuinely interesting. OpenAI and Anthropic, for all their excellence, are focused mostly on the first room. Google is the only company at frontier scale that is also pursuing the second room as a serious priority. If the scientific AI thesis pays off the way Hassabis seems to believe it will, Google's bet may turn out to be the most consequential in the industry, even if their chatbot remains in second or third place commercially.

What GOOGLE believes.

Like the previous two company chapters, let me try to lay out what Google's AI leadership actually thinks. Hassabis is more public than most AI executives and has been remarkably consistent in his views.

The first belief is that AGI, however you define it, is genuinely coming. Hassabis estimates we are five to ten years away. He is less certain than some of his peers and more careful about the prediction, but he believes the trajectory is real.

The second belief is that scaling, while important, is not sufficient. Hassabis has said publicly that he sees diminishing returns from pure scaling and that "one or two more breakthroughs" in areas like reasoning, memory, and reliability are needed for true AGI. This puts him in mild but real disagreement with some at OpenAI who have been more aggressive about pure-scaling claims.

The third belief is that world models, AIs that learn to predict and simulate physical reality, will be the next big advance. Google has invested heavily in this area through systems like Veo and Genie. The argument is that an AI that genuinely understands how the physical world works will eventually be necessary for robotics, for science, and for general intelligence.

The fourth belief is that the AI industry is, in significant part, in a bubble. Hassabis has said this publicly. He has also said that Google, because of its mature business and existing distribution, is well-positioned regardless of how the bubble resolves. This is corporate confidence, but it is also a reasonable read of the situation.

The fifth belief, and this one is interesting because it puts Google in opposition to OpenAI in a specific way, is that AI assistants should not include advertising. Hassabis has been explicit that Google has "no plans" to put ads into the Gemini app, on the grounds that an assistant the user trusts cannot also be commercially incentivized to recommend particular products. Given that Google is a company whose core business is advertising, this is a genuinely interesting position to take.

These beliefs shape what Google ships. They explain why Gemini behaves the way it does. They explain why Google is investing in scientific AI as well as consumer products. And they explain why, despite the early stumble, Google is now one of the three or four companies most likely to define what AI looks like in the second half of this decade.

What this means for you.

If you use Google products, and you probably do, you are increasingly using AI made by Google DeepMind. When you search, AI summarizes results. When you draft an email in Gmail, AI suggests phrases. When you ask your Android phone something, AI mediates the answer. The Gemini integration is now deep enough that for many users, Google AI is the dominant AI in their lives, even if they have never opened a chatbot.

This means a few things.

It means that Google's choices about safety, accuracy, refusals, and tone affect a larger number of people than perhaps any other AI company's choices. Two billion people use Search every month. Hundreds of millions use the Gemini app. The personality and behavior of those products, shaped by Google's design decisions, is one of the most consequential things being decided right now.

It means that Google has more reason than any other AI company to be cautious. They have a brand to protect. They have regulatory exposure. They have a search business to defend. The Gemini product reflects all of this caution, sometimes in ways users find frustrating, sometimes in ways users appreciate.

It also means that Google's scientific AI work, which most people will never use directly, may be the most important thing the company is doing. The drug discovery breakthroughs. The protein folding work. The materials science applications. These may, in the long run, matter more than which company has the best chatbot in 2026.

If you want to understand AI in our era, you have to understand Google. They invented the architecture. They built the infrastructure. They almost lost the future. And they are now back in the race, with advantages nobody else can match, and a vision that extends beyond the chatbot war that has dominated the headlines.
